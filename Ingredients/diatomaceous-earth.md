# Diatomaceous Earth (Food-Grade)
> Fossilized diatom shells composed of amorphous silica that mechanically damage insect exoskeletons, providing chemical-free pest control with no possibility of resistance development.

## Overview

Diatomaceous earth (DE) is a naturally occurring sedimentary deposit composed of the fossilized skeletal remains of diatoms, single-celled aquatic algae belonging to the class Bacillariophyceae. These microscopic organisms construct intricate siliceous frustules (cell walls) from dissolved silica in water, and upon death, their remains accumulate on lake and ocean floors over millions of years. The resulting deposits, when mined and processed, yield a fine powder with remarkable physical properties stemming from the complex three-dimensional microstructure of individual diatom frustules.

The distinction between food-grade and pool-grade (also called filter-grade) diatomaceous earth is critical and cannot be overstated. Food-grade DE contains less than 1% crystalline silica (typically 0.1-0.5%) and consists overwhelmingly of amorphous silica, which the body can process and eliminate. Pool-grade DE has been calcined (heat-treated at 800-1200 degrees C), converting the amorphous silica to crystalline silica (cristobalite and tridymite), which is a known human carcinogen classified as Group 1 by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC). Pool-grade DE must never be used for any application involving human or animal contact.

Diatomaceous earth has been used commercially since the mid-19th century, when Alfred Nobel discovered that nitroglycerin could be stabilized by absorbing it into kieselguhr, creating dynamite in 1867. Its use as an insecticide dates to at least the 1950s, when researchers first documented its efficacy against stored-grain pests. Today, food-grade DE is registered with the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA Registration No. 73729-1, among others) as a mechanical insecticide and is approved by the FDA as a food-grade substance under 21 CFR 573.340 for use as an anti-caking agent and filter aid.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Elemental Composition

Food-grade diatomaceous earth is predominantly silicon dioxide (SiO2), typically comprising 80-94% of the total mass. The remaining fraction consists of minor mineral components that vary by deposit source:

- **Silicon dioxide (SiO2):** 80-94%
- **Alumina (Al2O3):** 1-5%
- **Iron oxide (Fe2O3):** 0.5-2%
- **Calcium oxide (CaO):** 0.5-2%
- **Magnesium oxide (MgO):** 0.3-1.5%
- **Sodium oxide (Na2O):** 0.5-3%
- **Trace minerals:** titanium, manganese, boron, zinc, copper, selenium

### Amorphous vs. Crystalline Silica

The silica in food-grade DE exists in the amorphous (non-crystalline) form, meaning the SiO4 tetrahedra are arranged in a disordered, random network rather than the highly ordered lattice found in crystalline forms like quartz, cristobalite, or tridymite. This distinction is biologically crucial:

- **Amorphous silica** has a disordered surface with abundant silanol groups (Si-OH), making it relatively biocompatible. It is cleared from lung tissue and does not cause progressive fibrosis.
- **Crystalline silica** has a regular, repeating surface that generates persistent free radicals upon fracture, causes sustained inflammation, and leads to silicosis and lung cancer with chronic exposure.

### Microstructure

Individual diatom frustules range from 10 to 200 micrometers in diameter, though the powder is typically milled to a particle size distribution of 10-50 micrometers. Under scanning electron microscopy (SEM), each frustule reveals an intricate architecture of pores, channels, and spines arranged in species-specific geometric patterns. The porosity of individual frustules ranges from 40-60%, and the pore diameters typically range from 0.1 to 10 micrometers.

This microstructure gives DE its remarkable properties:
- **High surface area:** 10-30 m2/g (some sources report up to 50 m2/g for certain deposits)
- **High absorptive capacity:** Can absorb 1.0-1.5 times its own weight in liquid
- **Low bulk density:** 0.12-0.25 g/cm3
- **High porosity:** Up to 90% void space in bulk powder

### Surface Chemistry

The surface of amorphous silica in DE is characterized by silanol groups (Si-OH) at a density of approximately 4-5 groups per nm2. These groups are weakly acidic (pKa approximately 6.8) and can form hydrogen bonds with water and organic molecules. This hydrophilic surface is responsible for DE's ability to absorb lipids from insect cuticles and to adsorb moisture from the surrounding environment.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Geological Formation

Diatomaceous earth deposits form over millions of years through the following natural process:

1. **Diatom growth:** Living diatoms extract dissolved silica (orthosilicic acid, Si(OH)4) from water to construct their frustules through biological silicification.
2. **Death and sedimentation:** Upon death, diatom cells sink and accumulate on the bottom of lakes, seas, or oceans.
3. **Diagenesis:** Over geological time, the accumulated siliceous ooze undergoes compaction and mild chemical alteration, with organic material decomposing and the silica becoming more consolidated.
4. **Uplift:** Tectonic activity raises former seabeds and lakebeds above water level, making deposits accessible for mining.

Major deposits are found in the western United States (particularly Nevada, Oregon, California, and Washington), China, Denmark (Fur Island), Kenya, and Turkey.

### Industrial Production

1. **Mining:** DE is extracted from open-pit mines using conventional earthmoving equipment. Deposits can be several meters thick.
2. **Crushing:** Raw DE is crushed to break up large aggregates.
3. **Drying:** The material is dried in rotary kilns at 400-500 degrees C (well below the 800-1200 degrees C temperatures that would convert amorphous silica to crystalline forms) to reduce moisture content from 30-60% to less than 10%.
4. **Milling and classification:** Dried material is milled in hammer mills or roller mills and classified by air separation or screening to achieve desired particle size distribution.
5. **Quality control:** Each batch is tested for crystalline silica content (must be below 1% for food-grade), heavy metals, and particle size distribution. The product is certified to meet the specifications of the Food Chemicals Codex or equivalent standards.

### Food-Grade vs. Pool-Grade Processing

The critical difference occurs at the drying/calcination stage:
- **Food-grade:** Dried at low temperature (under 600 degrees C). Retains amorphous structure. May be uncalcined or flash-calcined.
- **Pool-grade:** Calcined at 800-1200 degrees C, often with flux agents (soda ash). Converts to crystalline silica. Fuses particles into larger, more uniform aggregates optimized for filtration.

### Home Processing

Diatomaceous earth cannot be practically produced at home. The geological timescale required for deposit formation (millions of years) and the industrial equipment needed for mining, drying, and classification make home production impossible. Always purchase from reputable suppliers who provide certificates of analysis confirming food-grade status and crystalline silica content below 1%.

## Mechanism of Action

### Mechanical Insecticidal Action

Diatomaceous earth kills insects through purely physical (mechanical) mechanisms, not chemical ones. This is significant because it means insects cannot develop biochemical resistance to DE, unlike chemical pesticides where resistance mutations are common.

The mechanism involves three synergistic physical processes:

1. **Cuticular abrasion:** The sharp, angular edges of fractured diatom frustules scratch and abrade the waxy epicuticle (the outermost lipid layer of the insect exoskeleton). This layer is typically only 0.1-1.0 micrometers thick and consists primarily of long-chain hydrocarbons, wax esters, and fatty acids. The microscopic projections and edges of DE particles, which can be as sharp as 1-2 micrometers at their tips, create micro-lacerations in this protective layer.

2. **Lipid adsorption:** The high surface area and hydrophilic silanol groups on DE particles actively adsorb (bind to their surface) the cuticular lipids and waxes. Research by Ebeling (1971) demonstrated that DE can absorb lipids equivalent to several times the weight of the wax layer present on insect cuticles. This stripping of the lipid layer is arguably more important than the physical scratching.

3. **Desiccation:** With the waxy epicuticle compromised by both abrasion and lipid removal, the insect loses its ability to regulate water loss. Insects are already under significant desiccation stress due to their high surface-area-to-volume ratio, and the intact epicuticle is their primary defense against water loss. Once compromised, the insect loses body water through the cuticle at a dramatically accelerated rate and dies of dehydration, typically within 24-72 hours depending on species, ambient humidity, and temperature.

### Factors Affecting Efficacy

- **Humidity:** DE is significantly less effective at high humidity (above 70% RH) because the adsorbed moisture on DE particles reduces their ability to absorb cuticular lipids, and insects lose water more slowly in humid environments.
- **Temperature:** Higher temperatures increase insect metabolic rate and water loss, enhancing DE efficacy.
- **Particle size:** Finer particles (under 10 micrometers) adhere better to insect cuticles but pose greater inhalation risk. Optimal particle size for efficacy is 10-50 micrometers.
- **Insect species:** Effectiveness varies significantly by species. Insects with thicker cuticles (e.g., some beetles) require longer exposure times than those with thin cuticles (e.g., bed bugs, fleas).

### Timeline of Action

Unlike chemical insecticides that may kill within minutes to hours, DE typically requires:
- **Ants:** 12-24 hours
- **Fleas:** 24-48 hours
- **Bed bugs:** 48-72 hours or longer
- **Cockroaches:** 24-72 hours
- **Stored grain pests:** 7-21 days at optimal conditions

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Indoor Pest Control (Crawling Insects)**
Apply a thin, barely visible layer of food-grade DE along baseboards, behind appliances, in cracks and crevices, around door and window frames, and in other areas where crawling insects travel. Key principles:
- Less is more. Insects will avoid thick piles. The powder should be barely visible.
- Use a bulb duster, squeeze bottle, or purpose-made DE applicator for controlled application.
- Reapply after vacuuming or if the powder becomes wet and cakes.

**Garden Pest Control**
- Apply around the base of plants to deter slugs, snails, and crawling insects.
- Dust directly on plants for aphids and other soft-bodied pests (reapply after rain).
- Note: DE also kills beneficial insects (ladybugs, bees) on contact, so avoid application to flowers or during active pollinator hours.

**Flea Control in Pet Areas**
- Sprinkle lightly on pet bedding, carpets, and upholstered furniture.
- Leave for 48-72 hours, then vacuum thoroughly.
- Repeat every 7-10 days for 3-4 weeks to address flea life cycle stages.
- Do not apply directly to pets without veterinary guidance.

**Stored Grain Protection**
- Mix at a rate of approximately 1 cup (approximately 130 g) per 25 kg of grain.
- Creates a protective barrier that kills grain weevils, flour beetles, and other stored-product pests.

**General Household Deodorizer/Absorber**
- Place open containers of DE in refrigerators, closets, or shoes to absorb odors and moisture.
- Can be mixed with essential oils for scented sachets.

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Filtration:** Used extensively in brewing, wine-making, swimming pool filtration, and water treatment as a filter medium (typically using calcined grades for pools).
- **Absorbent:** Used to absorb chemical spills in industrial settings, and as a carrier for liquid pesticides and fertilizers.
- **Filler:** Used as a filler in paints, plastics, rubber, and paper.
- **Abrasive:** Mild abrasive in toothpaste, metal polishes, and facial scrubs.
- **Insulation:** Historical use as thermal insulation (kieselguhr bricks).
- **Stabilizer:** Nobel's use as a stabilizer for nitroglycerin (dynamite).
- **Animal feed additive:** Approved anti-caking agent in animal feeds (FDA 21 CFR 573.340).

### Historical Uses

- **Dynamite production:** Alfred Nobel's 1867 patent for dynamite used kieselguhr as the absorbent matrix for nitroglycerin.
- **Ancient Greek uses:** Theophrastus (circa 300 BCE) described "easily powdered earth" used as a lightweight building material and polishing agent, which some scholars identify as diatomaceous earth.
- **Traditional grain storage:** Various cultures used mineral dusts including diatomaceous earth deposits for grain preservation, a practice documented in parts of Africa and Asia.
- **Thermal insulation:** Kieselguhr bricks were widely used for high-temperature insulation in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

## Efficacy & Research

**Against Stored-Product Insects:**
Korunic, Z. (1998) reviewed the insecticidal properties of diatomaceous earth in a comprehensive paper published in the *Journal of Stored Products Research* (34: 87-97). The review confirmed DE's efficacy against major stored-product pests and identified particle size, oil absorption capacity, and pH as key determinants of effectiveness.

**Against Bed Bugs:**
Akhtar, Y. and Isman, M.B. (2016) published in the *Journal of Economic Entomology* (109: 2565-2571) demonstrating that DE caused significant mortality in bed bug populations, though complete elimination required extended exposure periods of 7-14 days and was significantly reduced at high humidity.

**Against Cockroaches:**
Faulde, M.K. et al. (2006) published in the *Journal of Medical Entomology* (43: 352-358) showing that DE-based formulations achieved 80-100% mortality of German cockroaches within 10 days of continuous exposure, with enhanced activity when combined with low concentrations of pyrethroid insecticides.

**Silica Safety (Amorphous vs. Crystalline):**
The IARC Monograph Volume 68 (1997) classified crystalline silica (quartz, cristobalite) as a Group 1 carcinogen but noted that amorphous silica did not warrant classification as a carcinogen. The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) has established a recommended exposure limit (REL) of 6 mg/m3 for amorphous silica (total dust) vs. 0.05 mg/m3 for crystalline silica.

**Humidity Effects:**
Fields, P. and Korunic, Z. (2000) published in the *Journal of Stored Products Research* (36: 1-13) demonstrating that DE efficacy against stored-grain insects was reduced by 50-90% when relative humidity exceeded 70%, confirming that desiccation is the primary lethal mechanism.

**Long-Term Residual Activity:**
Arthur, F.H. (2000) published in the *Journal of Stored Products Research* (36: 313-320) demonstrating that DE maintained its insecticidal activity for months to years in dry, undisturbed conditions, as the mechanical mode of action does not degrade over time.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Critical Safety Distinction
**USE ONLY FOOD-GRADE DIATOMACEOUS EARTH.** Pool-grade (calcined, filter-grade) DE contains 60-70% crystalline silica and is a serious respiratory hazard. Always verify food-grade certification before purchase.

### Respiratory Protection
Even food-grade DE is a nuisance dust that can irritate the respiratory tract:
- **Always wear a NIOSH-approved N95 or P100 dust mask** when applying DE.
- Avoid creating dust clouds. Apply gently and in thin layers.
- Keep children and pets out of the area during application until dust settles.
- The OSHA Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL) for amorphous silica (nuisance dust) is 15 mg/m3 (total dust) or 5 mg/m3 (respirable fraction).

### Eye and Skin Protection
- DE can cause eye irritation due to its fine, abrasive particles. Wear safety glasses during application.
- Prolonged skin contact can cause dryness and irritation, as DE absorbs skin oils. Wear gloves for extended handling.

### Ingestion
- Food-grade DE is classified as Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) by the FDA when used as a food additive.
- Some people consume food-grade DE as a dietary supplement, though evidence for health benefits from ingestion is limited and largely anecdotal.
- The EPA has established no dietary risk from food-grade DE residues on grains.

### Environmental Considerations
- DE is non-selective and will kill beneficial insects (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps, ground beetles) as readily as pests.
- Avoid applying to flowers where pollinators (bees, butterflies) are active.
- DE is non-toxic to earthworms in soil at typical application rates.
- DE poses no risk to aquatic environments at normal use levels but should not be deliberately introduced into waterways.

### Contraindications
- Do not use in areas with sustained high humidity (above 70% RH) as efficacy will be greatly reduced.
- Do not apply to wet surfaces; DE must remain dry to function.
- Do not use pool-grade DE for any pest control, food contact, or human/animal exposure purpose.

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages
- Purely mechanical mode of action: no chemical toxicity to humans or mammals at normal exposure levels
- No resistance development possible in target insects
- Long residual activity when kept dry (months to years)
- Food-grade product approved for use around food, in grain storage, and as a food additive
- Effective against a broad spectrum of crawling insects
- Environmentally benign: no chemical residues, no water contamination
- Inexpensive and widely available
- Can be used in organic agriculture (OMRI-listed)
- No known target pest resistance after decades of use

### Disadvantages / Limitations
- Slow-acting compared to chemical insecticides (hours to days vs. minutes)
- Ineffective when wet; must be reapplied after rain or cleaning
- Significantly reduced efficacy at high humidity (above 70% RH)
- Non-selective: kills beneficial insects as well as pests
- Respiratory irritant requiring dust mask during application
- Messy: leaves visible white residue on surfaces
- Ineffective against flying insects (requires physical contact)
- Can dry out skin with prolonged contact
- Not effective against insect eggs (only affects active life stages that move through treated areas)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life:** Indefinite when stored dry. The mechanical mode of action does not degrade over time.
- **Storage conditions:** Keep in a sealed, moisture-proof container in a cool, dry location. Moisture absorption will cause caking and reduce pourability (though dried and re-crushed DE regains its properties).
- **Container:** Original bag or transfer to a sealed plastic bucket or container with a tight-fitting lid.
- **Stability:** No chemical degradation occurs. There is no expiration date for dry, uncontaminated food-grade DE.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Typical price:** $10-25 USD for a 10 lb (4.5 kg) bag of food-grade DE; $25-50 for 50 lb bags.
- **Sources:** Garden centers, hardware stores, farm supply stores, online retailers (Amazon, specialty pest control suppliers).
- **Key brands:** Harris, DiatomaceousEarth.com, Safer Brand, Perma-Guard, Lumino.
- **What to verify when purchasing:**
  - "Food grade" or "food chemical codex grade" labeling
  - Crystalline silica content listed as less than 1% (ideally less than 0.5%)
  - OMRI-listed if intended for organic gardening
  - EPA registration number if sold as a pesticide

## Regulatory Status

- **EPA:** Registered as a biopesticide (mechanical) under FIFRA. Exempt from tolerance requirements when used on raw agricultural commodities (40 CFR 180.1001(c)).
- **FDA:** Approved as a food additive (direct and indirect) and as a component of animal feed (21 CFR 573.340). Listed as GRAS.
- **USDA/NOP:** Allowed for use in organic crop production (listed in the National List of Allowed and Prohibited Substances, 7 CFR 205.601).
- **OMRI:** Listed for organic use when product meets food-grade specifications.
- **OSHA:** Regulated as a nuisance dust. PEL for amorphous silica: 15 mg/m3 total dust, 5 mg/m3 respirable. Crystalline silica PEL: 0.05 mg/m3 (much more stringent).
- **EU:** Approved as a biocide under the Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR, EU 528/2012). Also approved as a food additive (E551, silicon dioxide).

## Related Ingredients

- **[Neem Oil](neem-oil.md):** Complementary organic pest control approach using biochemical rather than mechanical action. Can be combined with DE for integrated pest management.
- **Borax / Boric Acid:** Another mineral-based insecticide, though it works through ingestion toxicity rather than mechanical desiccation.
- **Kaolin Clay (Surround WP):** Another mineral-based pest deterrent used in organic agriculture; works by creating a particle film barrier on plants.
- **Silicon dioxide (fumed/precipitated):** Synthetic amorphous silica products with higher surface area, used in some enhanced DE formulations.
