# Magnesium Chloride Flakes
> A highly soluble magnesium salt used to create "magnesium oil" for topical magnesium delivery, foot soaks, and bath applications, though clinical evidence for significant transdermal absorption remains limited.

## Overview

Magnesium chloride (MgCl2) is an inorganic salt that occurs naturally in seawater, salt lakes, and underground brine deposits. In the health and wellness community, it is best known as the source material for "magnesium oil" -- a concentrated aqueous solution of magnesium chloride that, despite its name, is not an oil at all but rather a supersaturated salt solution with an oily or slippery feel on the skin. Magnesium chloride is also sold as bath flakes for magnesium-enriched soaks and foot baths.

Magnesium is an essential mineral that serves as a cofactor in more than 300 enzymatic reactions in the human body, including energy production (ATP synthesis), protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, blood glucose control, blood pressure regulation, and bone development. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) estimates that 50-60% of American adults do not meet the Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for magnesium (400-420 mg/day for adult men, 310-320 mg/day for adult women). This widespread dietary insufficiency has driven intense consumer interest in alternative delivery methods, particularly transdermal (through-the-skin) magnesium supplementation.

The concept of transdermal magnesium delivery via magnesium chloride solutions has been popularized by Mark Sircus (2007) in "Transdermal Magnesium Therapy" and by various wellness practitioners. However, a 2017 systematic review commissioned by the NIH (Grier et al., published in *Nutrients*) concluded that evidence supporting clinically significant transdermal magnesium absorption through intact human skin is limited and methodologically weak. While many users report subjective benefits from topical magnesium chloride use (reduced muscle tension, improved sleep, reduced cramping), the extent to which these effects are attributable to actual transdermal magnesium absorption versus placebo, thermal effects of warm soaks, or other mechanisms remains an open scientific question.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Crystal Structure

Magnesium chloride hexahydrate (MgCl2 . 6H2O) crystallizes in the monoclinic system. The magnesium ion is octahedrally coordinated by six water molecules, forming the [Mg(H2O)6]2+ complex cation. The chloride ions occupy positions in the crystal lattice between these hydrated magnesium complexes, held by electrostatic attraction and hydrogen bonding with the coordinated water molecules.

### Hydration States

| Form | Formula | MW (g/mol) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anhydrous | MgCl2 | 95.21 | White powder, extremely hygroscopic, reacts exothermically with water |
| Monohydrate | MgCl2 . H2O | 113.24 | Intermediate hydrate |
| Dihydrate | MgCl2 . 2H2O | 131.26 | Mineral: bischofite precursor |
| Tetrahydrate | MgCl2 . 4H2O | 167.29 | Intermediate |
| Hexahydrate | MgCl2 . 6H2O | 203.30 | Most common commercial form; mineral: bischofite |
| Dodecahydrate | MgCl2 . 12H2O | 311.38 | Forms below -16.4 degrees C |

The hexahydrate is the form sold as "magnesium flakes." It is stable at room temperature and moderate humidity.

### Dissociation in Water

MgCl2 -> Mg2+ + 2Cl-

The dissociation is complete in dilute solution. The high solubility of MgCl2 allows the preparation of very concentrated solutions (up to approximately 35% w/v at room temperature), which is the basis for "magnesium oil" preparations.

| Hygroscopicity | Very high (deliquescent) | Moderate |

### "Magnesium Oil" Composition

Standard magnesium oil is simply a concentrated aqueous solution of MgCl2:
- Typical concentration: 25-35% MgCl2 by weight
- At 31% concentration: approximately 3.1 g MgCl2 per 10 mL = approximately 370 mg elemental Mg per 10 mL
- The "oily" feel results from the high ionic strength of the solution altering the friction properties of skin -- there is no actual oil present
- pH of typical magnesium oil: 5.5-7.0

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Natural Sources

**Seawater:** The primary source. Seawater contains approximately 1.3 g/L of magnesium, primarily as MgCl2 and MgSO4. Magnesium chloride is recovered from seawater or concentrated brines after sodium chloride (table salt) has crystallized out.

**The Zechstein Sea deposit:** A major commercial source is the ancient Zechstein Sea deposit in the Netherlands and northern Germany, a 250-million-year-old evaporite formation buried approximately 1,600-2,000 meters underground. Magnesium chloride is extracted by solution mining (pumping water into the deposit and recovering the brine). "Zechstein" magnesium chloride is marketed as particularly pure due to the geological isolation of the deposit from modern pollution.

**Dead Sea:** The Dead Sea contains approximately 50 g/L of magnesium chloride (approximately 10 times the concentration of normal seawater). Dead Sea magnesium chloride is a commercial product, though it contains significant quantities of other minerals (potassium, sodium, calcium, bromide).

**Great Salt Lake, Utah:** Another commercial source of magnesium chloride, recovered from solar evaporation ponds.

### Industrial Production

**From seawater/brine:**
1. Seawater or natural brine is concentrated by solar evaporation in shallow ponds
2. As concentration increases, sodium chloride crystallizes first (at approximately 26% total salt)
3. The remaining mother liquor (bittern) is enriched in MgCl2, MgSO4, KCl, and other salts
4. Further concentration and selective crystallization separates MgCl2
5. The product may be further purified by recrystallization

**From magnesite or dolomite:**
MgCO3 + 2HCl -> MgCl2 + H2O + CO2
CaMg(CO3)2 + 4HCl -> MgCl2 + CaCl2 + 2H2O + 2CO2 (followed by separation)

**From magnesium hydroxide:**
Mg(OH)2 + 2HCl -> MgCl2 + 2H2O

### Making Magnesium Oil at Home

Magnesium oil is easily prepared at home:

**Materials:**
- Magnesium chloride flakes (food-grade or pharmaceutical-grade)
- Distilled water
- Clean spray bottle or glass jar

**Procedure:**
1. Measure 1/2 cup (approximately 65 g) magnesium chloride flakes
2. Heat 1/2 cup (120 mL) distilled water until hot (not boiling)
3. Add flakes to hot water and stir until completely dissolved
4. Allow to cool to room temperature
5. Transfer to a spray bottle
6. Label with date and contents

This produces an approximately 35% MgCl2 solution -- standard magnesium oil concentration. Shelf life is approximately 6-12 months (the high salt concentration inhibits microbial growth).

**Usage:** Spray onto skin (arms, legs, abdomen, or feet), allow to absorb for 20-30 minutes, then rinse or leave on. Initial tingling or stinging is normal, especially on freshly shaved skin, and typically diminishes with regular use.

## Mechanism of Action

### Transdermal Absorption (Proposed Mechanism)

The hypothesized mechanism for topical magnesium chloride involves:

1. **Passive diffusion:** Mg2+ ions diffuse across the stratum corneum (the outermost layer of dead skin cells) driven by the concentration gradient between the concentrated MgCl2 solution on the skin surface and the lower Mg2+ concentration in the epidermis and dermis.

2. **Transport through appendages:** Some researchers hypothesize that hair follicles and sweat glands provide shunt pathways that bypass the stratum corneum barrier, potentially allowing ion transport.

3. **Warm water enhancement:** When used in baths or soaks, the warm water hydrates and softens the stratum corneum, theoretically increasing its permeability to ions.

**Critical Assessment:**

The stratum corneum is an exceptionally effective barrier against the passage of charged species (ions). Key barriers to transdermal Mg2+ absorption include:

- The lipophilic nature of the stratum corneum's intercellular lipid matrix (ceramides, cholesterol, free fatty acids), which strongly opposes the passage of hydrophilic, divalent cations
- The "brick and mortar" model of the stratum corneum, where corneocytes (protein-rich "bricks") are embedded in a lipid matrix ("mortar"), creating a tortuous pathway
- The high charge density of Mg2+ (small ionic radius, +2 charge) makes it a poorly permeable species compared to uncharged or lipophilic molecules

**Current Evidence Status:**
- Waring, R.H. (2004): Unpublished pilot study from the University of Birmingham reported increased serum and urinary magnesium after Epsom salt baths. Never peer-reviewed, small sample, no control group.
- Chandrasekaran, N.C. et al. (2016): Published in *PLOS ONE* (11(6): e0157485). Applied MgCl2 to excised human skin in vitro (Franz diffusion cell) and reported measurable permeation. However, in vitro skin permeation may not reflect in vivo conditions accurately.
- Grier, T. et al. (2017): Systematic review in *Nutrients* (9(8): 813) concluded that "the evidence supporting transdermal magnesium absorption from topical applications and bathing is limited by small study sizes, methodological limitations, and lack of placebo controls."
- Kass, L. et al. (2017): Published in *PLOS ONE* (12(4): e0174817) a pilot RCT of transdermal MgCl2 cream showing increased serum magnesium. However, the study was small (n=25), short-duration (2 weeks), and the increase, while statistically significant, was modest.

### Magnesium's Biochemical Role (If Absorbed)

Magnesium, if successfully delivered to tissues, serves as a cofactor in critical biochemical processes:

1. **ATP function:** All ATP (adenosine triphosphate) in cells is actually Mg-ATP complex. Magnesium bridges the phosphate groups of ATP, enabling its function as an energy carrier.

2. **Enzyme activation:** Cofactor for 300+ enzymes, including:
   - All kinases (phosphotransferases)
   - DNA and RNA polymerases
   - ATPases (Na+/K+-ATPase, Ca2+-ATPase)
   - Glutathione synthetase (critical antioxidant)

3. **Muscle function:** Magnesium acts as a natural calcium channel blocker, competing with Ca2+ at voltage-gated calcium channels and the NMDA receptor. This relaxes smooth and skeletal muscle by reducing calcium-dependent contraction.

4. **Nerve function:** Blocks NMDA receptors (excitatory), reducing neuronal excitability. This may explain reported improvements in sleep and anxiety with magnesium supplementation.

5. **Bone metabolism:** 50-60% of total body magnesium is stored in bone. Magnesium is required for vitamin D activation and parathyroid hormone secretion.

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Magnesium Oil Spray**
- Prepare as described above (equal parts MgCl2 flakes and distilled water)
- Spray 10-20 sprays onto skin (legs, arms, abdomen)
- Allow to absorb for 20-30 minutes
- Rinse if desired (some users find the salt residue uncomfortable)
- Use daily, ideally before bed

**Magnesium Foot Soak**
- Dissolve 1/2 cup MgCl2 flakes in a basin of warm water (40-42 degrees C)
- Soak feet for 20-30 minutes
- The sole of the foot has a thinner stratum corneum than most body areas, potentially facilitating absorption
- Can add 5-10 drops essential oil (lavender for relaxation, peppermint for invigoration)

**Magnesium Bath**
- Add 2-3 cups MgCl2 flakes to a warm bath (37-40 degrees C)
- Soak for 20-30 minutes
- More expensive per bath than Epsom salt but preferred by some users for the skin feel (less drying)

**Magnesium Body Butter**
- 1/4 cup coconut oil (solid)
- 2 tablespoons shea butter
- 2 tablespoons magnesium oil (prepared as above)
- 10 drops essential oil (optional)
- Melt coconut oil and shea butter together, cool slightly, whisk in magnesium oil and essential oil
- Transfer to jar; allow to solidify
- The emulsified format reduces the tingling sensation compared to straight magnesium oil

**Magnesium Lotion**
- Add magnesium oil to an unscented, natural lotion at a ratio of 1:1 to 1:3 (magnesium oil : lotion)
- Mix thoroughly
- The lotion base reduces direct skin contact with the concentrated salt solution, minimizing tingling

**Restless Legs / Muscle Cramp Roll-On**
- Fill a roll-on applicator bottle with magnesium oil
- Optional: add 5 drops peppermint essential oil and 5 drops lavender essential oil per 30 mL
- Apply directly to cramping or restless muscles

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **De-icing:** Magnesium chloride is widely used as a road de-icer, effective to -15 degrees C (5 degrees F). Less corrosive to concrete and metals than sodium chloride.
- **Dust control:** Applied to unpaved roads to suppress dust (hygroscopic nature binds surface moisture).
- **Sorel cement:** MgCl2 combined with magnesium oxide (MgO) forms magnesium oxychloride cement (Sorel cement), used for flooring, fire protection, and specialty applications.
- **Food industry:** Nigari (food-grade MgCl2) is the traditional coagulant for making tofu from soy milk. Also used as a mineral supplement in some sports drinks and mineral waters.
- **Wastewater treatment:** Magnesium chloride is used for phosphorus removal and pH adjustment.
- **Textile and paper:** Fire retardant treatment for fabrics and paper products.

### Historical Uses

- **Tofu production:** MgCl2 (nigari) has been used for centuries in Japanese and Chinese cuisine as the coagulant for tofu making. The bitter compounds remaining after salt crystallization from seawater (bittern/nigari) are rich in MgCl2.
- **Traditional medicine:** Seawater and mineral spring bathing (thalassotherapy) has been practiced for millennia, with magnesium chloride being one of the active mineral components.
- **Dead Sea therapeutics:** Bathing in the Dead Sea (which is approximately 5% MgCl2) for skin conditions has been practiced since at least the time of Herod the Great and is still promoted today for psoriasis and other inflammatory skin conditions.

## Efficacy & Research

**Transdermal Absorption (Systematic Review):**
Grier, T. et al. (2017) published in *Nutrients* (9(8): 813) a systematic review of the evidence for transdermal magnesium absorption. The review identified only a handful of studies, most with significant methodological limitations. The authors concluded: "Currently there is insufficient evidence to recommend transdermal magnesium as a method for increasing magnesium status."

**In Vitro Skin Permeation:**
Chandrasekaran, N.C. et al. (2016) published in *PLOS ONE* (11(6): e0157485) demonstrating that magnesium ions permeated excised human skin in vitro when applied as a 20% MgCl2 solution. Permeation was measurable through the stratum corneum. However, in vitro permeation studies may overestimate in vivo absorption due to the absence of dermal blood flow, intact barrier function, and other in vivo factors.

**Pilot RCT (Transdermal MgCl2 Cream):**
Kass, L. et al. (2017) published in *PLOS ONE* (12(4): e0174817) a small randomized controlled trial in which participants applied a MgCl2-containing cream for 14 days. Serum magnesium levels increased significantly in the treatment group compared to controls. The study was limited by small sample size (n=25), short duration, and lack of long-term follow-up.

**Magnesium Deficiency and Health:**
de Baaij, J.H.F. et al. (2015) published a comprehensive review in *Physiological Reviews* (95(1): 1-46) documenting the role of magnesium in human health. The review confirms that magnesium deficiency is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, osteoporosis, migraine, depression, and muscle cramps. However, the review focuses on oral and IV magnesium supplementation, not transdermal.

**Dead Sea Bathing (Psoriasis):**
Harari, M. et al. (2000) published in the *International Journal of Dermatology* (39(11): 828-832) demonstrating that Dead Sea bathing (rich in MgCl2) improved psoriasis symptoms. However, the therapeutic effect cannot be attributed solely to MgCl2, as Dead Sea water contains multiple minerals and UV exposure at the Dead Sea (low altitude, high atmospheric filtering) is a known psoriasis treatment.

**Muscle Cramps:**
Garrison, S.R. et al. (2012) published in the *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews* (9: CD009402) a systematic review of magnesium for muscle cramps. The review found limited evidence that oral magnesium supplementation reduced leg cramps in non-pregnant adults. No studies of topical magnesium for muscle cramps met inclusion criteria.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Skin Application
- **Tingling/stinging:** Normal upon initial application, especially on freshly shaved, damaged, or sensitive skin. Typically diminishes with regular use over 1-2 weeks. If persistent or severe, dilute the magnesium oil with more water.
- **Skin irritation:** Some individuals may experience redness, itching, or rash. Discontinue use and consult a dermatologist if this occurs.
- **Patch test:** Always perform a patch test on a small area of intact skin before widespread use.
- **Avoid broken skin:** Do not apply to cuts, abrasions, burns, or inflamed skin (will sting intensely).
- **Sensitive areas:** Avoid contact with eyes, mucous membranes, and genital areas.

### Oral Ingestion
- Oral magnesium chloride is available as a supplement but should be taken under medical guidance
- Excessive oral magnesium can cause diarrhea (osmotic laxative effect), nausea, and abdominal cramping
- Severe hypermagnesemia (from massive oral intake or in renal failure) can cause cardiac arrhythmia, respiratory depression, and cardiac arrest
- **Contraindicated** in severe renal impairment (kidneys cannot excrete excess magnesium)

### Drug Interactions
- Magnesium can reduce absorption of certain antibiotics (tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones) and bisphosphonates if taken orally simultaneously
- May potentiate neuromuscular blocking agents
- Theoretical interactions with calcium channel blockers (additive hypotensive effect)
- For topical use, drug interactions are less concerning due to uncertain absorption, but inform healthcare providers of regular magnesium supplementation

### Pregnancy
- Topical use is generally considered safe during pregnancy
- Intravenous magnesium sulfate is an established treatment for pre-eclampsia (well-studied, different route and salt form)
- Consult healthcare provider before starting any new supplement during pregnancy

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages
- Provides higher elemental magnesium per gram than Epsom salt (MgSO4)
- Easy to prepare magnesium oil at home (just dissolve in water)
- Non-invasive alternative to oral supplements (avoids GI side effects like diarrhea)
- Widely reported subjective benefits for muscle relaxation, sleep, and cramping
- Extremely soluble in water, allowing highly concentrated preparations
- Long shelf life (concentrated salt solution resists microbial growth)
- Multiple application formats (spray, bath, lotion, foot soak)
- Pleasant skin feel after rinse (less drying than Epsom salt due to absence of sulfate)
- Available from ancient, naturally pure deposits (Zechstein)

### Disadvantages / Limitations
- Transdermal absorption is not well-established in peer-reviewed literature
- Initial tingling/stinging may deter some users
- More expensive than Epsom salt for bath use
- Leaves a salt residue on skin if not rinsed
- Highly hygroscopic: absorbs moisture from air, making storage challenging in humid climates
- "Magnesium oil" is a misleading name (not an oil)
- Many health claims are based on anecdotal evidence rather than clinical trials
- The degree of absorption, if any, may be too low to meaningfully impact total body magnesium status
- Cannot replace medical treatment for diagnosed magnesium deficiency

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life (flakes):** 2-5 years when stored in sealed, moisture-proof containers. The hexahydrate is stable at room temperature.
- **Shelf life (magnesium oil):** 6-12 months. The high salt concentration provides natural preservation against microbial growth. Use distilled water to extend shelf life.
- **Storage conditions:** Store flakes in a tightly sealed container in a cool, dry place. MgCl2 is deliquescent (absorbs moisture from the air until it dissolves itself), so exposure to humid air will cause flakes to liquefy.
- **Container:** Airtight plastic or glass containers with secure lids. Original resealable bags are usually acceptable. Do not use metal containers (corrosion potential).
- **Signs of degradation:** Liquefaction of flakes indicates moisture absorption but does not affect potency -- the dissolved magnesium chloride is still usable. Re-drying is impractical; simply use the liquid.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Typical price:** $15-25 USD for 2 lb (907 g) of flakes; $25-45 for 5 lb (2.27 kg).
- **Price per bath:** Approximately $3-5 per bath (using 2-3 cups), more expensive than Epsom salt ($0.50-1.50 per bath).
- **Sources:** Health food stores, online retailers (Amazon, iHerb, specialty magnesium retailers), pharmacies.
- **Quality considerations:**
  - **Zechstein source:** Marketed as the purest form; extracted from ancient deposits protected from modern pollution. Commands a price premium.
  - **Dead Sea source:** Contains other minerals (potassium, sodium, calcium, bromide). Less pure MgCl2 but the additional minerals may be desired.
  - **Food-grade/USP-grade:** Tested for heavy metals and purity. Recommended for topical use.
  - **Industrial/de-icing grade:** NOT suitable for topical use (may contain impurities).
- **Key brands:** Ancient Minerals (Zechstein), Life-flo, Seven Minerals, EASE Magnesium.

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA:** Magnesium chloride is Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) as a food additive (firming agent, flavor enhancer, nutrient supplement) under 21 CFR 184.1426. As a dietary supplement ingredient, it is regulated under DSHEA. Topical products containing MgCl2 are regulated as cosmetics.
- **EPA:** Not regulated as a pesticide. Used as a de-icing agent (subject to state/local environmental regulations regarding chloride discharge).
- **EU:** Approved as a food additive (E511). Permitted in cosmetic products without concentration limits.
- **USP:** Monograph exists for Magnesium Chloride (pharmaceutical grade).
- **General:** No purchase restrictions. Available without prescription.

## Related Ingredients

- **[Epsom Salt (Magnesium Sulfate)](epsom-salt.md):** Alternative magnesium salt for bath use. Less expensive, widely available. Sulfate anion vs. chloride anion. Similar proposed benefits, similar limitations in evidence for transdermal absorption.
- **[Magnesium Hydroxide](magnesium-hydroxide.md):** Used in natural deodorants and as an antacid. Different applications but related as a magnesium compound.
- **Magnesium Glycinate / Bisglycinate:** Chelated oral magnesium supplement with high bioavailability and minimal GI side effects. Preferred oral form for addressing magnesium deficiency.
- **Magnesium L-Threonate:** Oral supplement specifically studied for brain magnesium levels and cognitive function (Slutsky, I. et al., 2010, *Neuron*).
- **Dead Sea Salt:** Complex mineral mixture containing MgCl2, KCl, NaCl, CaCl2, and bromides. Used for bath soaks and skin conditions (psoriasis).
