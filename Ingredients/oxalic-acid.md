# Oxalic Acid

> A strong dicarboxylic organic acid that forms highly stable complexes with iron and calcium, making it exceptionally effective at removing rust stains, tannin stains, and discoloration from surfaces — the active ingredient in Bar Keeper's Friend.

## Overview

Oxalic acid is the simplest dicarboxylic acid, consisting of just two carboxyl groups bonded together. Despite this molecular simplicity, it has remarkably specific and powerful chemistry — particularly its ability to form extremely stable complexes with iron (Fe³⁺) and calcium (Ca²⁺) ions. This chelation chemistry makes oxalic acid uniquely effective at dissolving rust (iron oxide), removing tannin stains (which are tannin-iron complexes), and brightening discolored wood and surfaces.

Oxalic acid occurs naturally in many plants, including rhubarb leaves (which are toxic partly due to high oxalate content), spinach, sorrel, and star fruit. It was first synthesized in 1776 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele through the oxidation of sugar with nitric acid. Commercially, it is produced from the oxidation of carbohydrates or as a byproduct of other industrial processes.

In the DIY household context, oxalic acid is a **specialty acid** — more powerful and more toxic than vinegar or citric acid, reserved for jobs those milder acids cannot handle: rust stains on sinks and tubs, tannin stains on wood and fabric, discoloration on concrete, and wood brightening/restoration. It is the active ingredient in the well-known cleaning product Bar Keeper's Friend.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Molecular Structure

Oxalic acid is the simplest dicarboxylic acid:
- Two carboxyl groups (-COOH) directly bonded: HOOC-COOH
- As a diprotic acid, it can donate two protons
- pKa₁ = 1.25 (strong first dissociation — stronger than acetic or citric acid)
- pKa₂ = 4.27

### Chelation Chemistry

The key to oxalic acid's cleaning power:

1. **Iron chelation**: Oxalate ions form extremely stable complexes with Fe³⁺:
   - Fe₂O₃ (rust) + 6H₂C₂O₄ → 2[Fe(C₂O₄)₃]³⁻ + 3H₂O + 6H⁺
   - The iron(III) oxalate complex is water-soluble, removing rust stains completely
   - Stability constant (log K) for Fe(C₂O₄)₃³⁻ ≈ 20 — extremely stable

2. **Calcium chelation**: Oxalate binds Ca²⁺, dissolving calcium-containing deposits:
   - Forms calcium oxalate (CaC₂O₄), which is actually insoluble — but in excess oxalic acid, soluble calcium bis-oxalate complexes form

3. **Tannin-iron complex dissolution**: Many dark stains on wood, fabric, and surfaces are tannin-iron complexes (Fe³⁺ crosslinked with polyphenols). Oxalic acid strips the iron out of these complexes, decolorizing the stain.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

1. **Oxidation of carbohydrates**: Sugar, starch, or cellulose oxidized with nitric acid or air in the presence of catalysts
2. **Sodium formate fusion**: Heating sodium formate produces sodium oxalate: 2HCOONa → Na₂C₂O₄ + H₂↑. Acidification yields oxalic acid.
3. **Ethylene glycol oxidation**: Modern process using nitric acid oxidation
4. **Bio-based production**: *Aspergillus niger* produces oxalic acid as a metabolic byproduct (same organism used for citric acid, different conditions)

### Natural Occurrence

- Rhubarb leaves: 0.5–1% oxalic acid (toxic levels)
- Spinach: 0.3–1.2% oxalic acid
- Sorrel: High oxalate content
- Star fruit: Contains significant oxalic acid
- Cocoa: Contains oxalates

### Home Production

- Not practical or safe. Oxalic acid is inexpensive and readily available as a powder.

## Mechanism of Action

### Rust Removal

1. Oxalic acid dissociates, providing H⁺ (acid attack) and C₂O₄²⁻ (chelation)
2. H⁺ attacks iron oxide surface, loosening Fe³⁺ ions
3. Oxalate ions immediately chelate freed Fe³⁺, forming soluble iron(III) oxalate
4. The rust dissolves and is carried away in solution
5. More effective than other acids because the chelation prevents re-deposition of iron

### Tannin Stain Removal

1. Dark stains on wood, concrete, and fabric are often tannin-iron complexes
2. Tannins (polyphenols from wood, tea, wine) crosslink with iron from water or hardware
3. Oxalic acid strips Fe³⁺ from the tannin-iron complex via stronger chelation
4. Without the iron, the tannin becomes colorless or light-colored
5. The stain visibly lightens or disappears

### Wood Brightening

1. Weathered gray wood contains oxidized lignin and tannin-iron stains
2. Oxalic acid dissolves the iron complexes causing discoloration
3. Reveals the lighter, natural wood color beneath
4. Standard practice in deck restoration and wood refinishing

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Rust Stain Removal (Primary Use)**:
- Dissolve 1 tbsp oxalic acid in 1 cup warm water
- Apply to rust-stained surface (sink, tub, concrete, tile)
- Let sit 10–20 minutes
- Scrub with brush, rinse thoroughly
- Repeat for stubborn stains

**Wood Brightening / Deck Restoration**:
- Dissolve 4 oz (½ cup) oxalic acid in 1 gallon warm water
- Apply liberally to gray or stained wood with brush or sprayer
- Let sit 15–20 minutes (do not let dry on surface)
- Scrub with stiff brush
- Rinse thoroughly with water
- Neutralize with baking soda solution (1 cup per gallon) — optional but recommended before staining/sealing

**Tannin Stain Removal (Fabric)**:
- Dissolve 1 tbsp in 1 cup warm water
- Apply to stain (wine, tea, coffee on white fabric)
- Let sit 10–15 minutes
- Rinse thoroughly, then wash as normal
- Test on inconspicuous area first — may bleach colored fabrics

**Bar Keeper's Friend Alternative**:
- Oxalic acid is the active ingredient in Bar Keeper's Friend
- Make a paste with oxalic acid powder and water
- Apply to stainless steel, ceramic, porcelain, copper
- Scrub gently, rinse
- Removes rust rings, tannin stains, hard water deposits, discoloration

**Concrete Stain Removal**:
- Iron stain on concrete (from furniture legs, tools, rebar bleed):
- Apply strong oxalic acid solution (2–3 tbsp per cup)
- Scrub, let sit 20–30 minutes, rinse

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Toxicity — More Dangerous Than Other Household Acids

- **Oxalic acid is significantly more toxic than vinegar or citric acid**
- Oral LD₅₀ (rat): 375 mg/kg (moderately toxic)
- Lethal dose for adult human: estimated 15–30 grams (though severe harm at lower doses)
- Mechanism of toxicity: oxalate chelates calcium in blood and tissues → hypocalcemia, kidney damage (calcium oxalate crystal deposition)

### Required Precautions

- **Wear gloves** — causes skin irritation and potential burns with prolonged contact
- **Wear eye protection** — can cause serious eye damage
- **Ventilate well** — avoid inhaling dust when mixing powder
- **Rinse thoroughly** — do not leave residue on surfaces, especially food contact surfaces
- **Keep away from children and pets**
- **Do not ingest** — seek immediate medical attention if swallowed

### Surface Compatibility

- **Safe on**: Stainless steel, ceramic, porcelain, concrete, wood, most stone, glass
- **Caution on**: Marble and limestone (acid can etch — but often used for iron stain removal on these surfaces at low concentration with careful timing)
- **Test first**: Always test on an inconspicuous area

### First Aid

- **Skin contact**: Wash immediately with soap and water
- **Eye contact**: Flush with water for 15+ minutes; seek medical attention
- **Ingestion**: Do NOT induce vomiting; call poison control immediately; administer calcium (milk) if conscious
- **Inhalation**: Move to fresh air; seek medical attention if symptoms persist

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Uniquely effective at rust removal (no household substitute)
- Excellent for tannin stain removal
- Active ingredient in Bar Keeper's Friend (proven efficacy)
- Brightens weathered wood
- Works on stains that vinegar and citric acid cannot touch
- Relatively inexpensive
- Available as pure powder

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- More toxic than other household acids — requires gloves, ventilation, eye protection
- Can bleach colored fabrics and surfaces
- Not for everyday cleaning (specialty use only)
- Must rinse thoroughly
- Can etch sensitive stone surfaces
- Harmful if ingested, inhaled, or contacted with eyes
- Not suitable for food contact surface routine cleaning without thorough rinsing

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: Indefinite when stored dry
- **Storage**: Cool, dry place in sealed container. Keep away from children and pets.
- **Container**: Original packaging or sealed glass/plastic jar. Label clearly.
- **Hygroscopy**: Slightly hygroscopic — keep sealed
- **Safety**: Store separately from strong bases and oxidizers

## Related Ingredients

- **Citric Acid** — Milder chelating acid for everyday descaling. Use citric acid when possible; reserve oxalic acid for iron/tannin stains that citric acid can't handle.
- **White Vinegar** — Weakest household acid. Cannot remove rust stains. Good for light mineral deposits only.
- **Hydrogen Peroxide** — Oxidizing agent for organic stains (blood, wine). Different mechanism from oxalic acid's chelation.
- **Washing Soda** — Alkaline counterpart for grease. Use after oxalic acid treatment on wood (as neutralizer before staining).
- **Bar Keeper's Friend** — Commercial product containing oxalic acid as the active ingredient.
