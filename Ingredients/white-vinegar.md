# White Vinegar (5% Acetic Acid)

> A clear, colorless solution of approximately 5% acetic acid in water, produced by bacterial fermentation of ethanol, serving as a versatile household acid for cleaning, descaling, laundry, and hair care applications.

## Quick Reference

| Chemical Name / IUPAC | Acetic acid (ethanoic acid) aqueous solution |

| CAS Number | 64-19-7 (acetic acid) |

## Overview

White vinegar is one of the oldest and most ubiquitous household chemicals, with a history of use spanning at least 10,000 years. The word "vinegar" derives from the French *vin aigre* ("sour wine"), reflecting its original production method — the natural acetification of wine or other alcoholic beverages by acetic acid bacteria (*Acetobacter* species).

Modern white distilled vinegar is produced by the fermentation of distilled grain alcohol (ethanol) by *Acetobacter* bacteria, producing a clean, clear solution of approximately 5% acetic acid (50 grain) in water. Unlike wine vinegar, apple cider vinegar, or other specialty vinegars, white distilled vinegar contains no residual sugars, colors, or flavors — making it the ideal choice for cleaning, laundry, and other non-culinary household applications where purity is preferred.

Acetic acid is a weak organic acid, but at 5% concentration it is acidic enough (pH ~2.4) to dissolve mineral deposits (limescale, hard water stains), cut through grease when combined with surfactants, kill many common bacteria, and neutralize alkaline residues. In the DIY household context, white vinegar is one of the essential "big five" bulk ingredients (alongside baking soda, washing soda, hydrogen peroxide, and castile soap) that can replace most commercial cleaning and laundry products.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Acetic Acid

Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) is the simplest carboxylic acid after formic acid. Its molecular structure consists of:
- A methyl group (CH₃)
- A carboxyl group (COOH)
- The carboxyl group donates a proton (H⁺) in aqueous solution, making it an acid

As a **weak acid**, acetic acid does not fully dissociate in water:
- pKa = 4.76
- At 5% concentration (~0.83M), only about 0.4% of the molecules are dissociated
- This partial dissociation makes it much safer than strong acids (HCl, H₂SO₄) while still effective for household cleaning

### Physical Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Boiling point | 118°C (pure acetic acid); ~100°C for 5% solution |
| Freezing point | 16.6°C (pure glacial acetic acid); below 0°C for 5% solution |
| Density | 1.005 g/mL (5% solution) |
| Flash point | 39°C (pure glacial acetic acid); not flammable at 5% |
| Vapor pressure | Higher than water — produces noticeable fumes |

### Cleaning Chemistry

Acetic acid works through several chemical mechanisms:
1. **Acid-base dissolution**: Dissolves alkaline minerals (CaCO₃ limescale, Mg(OH)₂ hard water deposits) through neutralization: CaCO₃ + 2CH₃COOH → Ca(CH₃COO)₂ + H₂O + CO₂
2. **Chelation**: Acetate ions complex with metal ions, keeping dissolved minerals in solution
3. **Grease cutting**: When paired with a surfactant (dish soap), the acidic environment aids emulsification of fatty deposits
4. **Antimicrobial**: Low pH environment inhibits bacterial growth; some direct membrane disruption

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production (Modern)

1. **Ethanol source**: Grain alcohol (corn, wheat) is distilled to produce pure ethanol
2. **Acetification**: Ethanol is converted to acetic acid by *Acetobacter aceti* bacteria in large fermentation tanks:
   - C₂H₅OH + O₂ → CH₃COOH + H₂O
3. **Submerged fermentation**: Modern process uses aerated tanks where bacteria are suspended in the liquid (faster than traditional surface methods)
4. **Distillation**: The resulting vinegar is distilled to produce a pure, clear solution
5. **Standardization**: Diluted to exactly 5% acetic acid (50-grain strength)
6. **Bottling**: Packaged in food-grade containers

### Traditional Methods

- **Orleans process**: Wine is exposed to air in partially-filled barrels; a film of *Acetobacter* ("mother of vinegar") forms on the surface and slowly converts alcohol to acetic acid over weeks to months
- **Trickling generators**: Alcohol is trickled over wood shavings colonized by *Acetobacter*; faster than Orleans method

### Home Production

It is possible to make vinegar at home:
1. Start with an alcoholic liquid (wine, hard cider, beer)
2. Introduce a vinegar "mother" (available from brewing suppliers)
3. Keep in a warm (75–85°F), dark place with cheesecloth cover (bacteria need oxygen)
4. Wait 2–4 weeks
5. Resulting vinegar will vary in acidity — unreliable for cleaning where consistent strength matters

## Mechanism of Action

### Descaling / Mineral Dissolution

The primary household application:
- Limescale (calcium carbonate) and hard water deposits are alkaline minerals
- Acetic acid reacts with and dissolves them: CaCO₃ + 2CH₃COOH → Ca(CH₃COO)₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
- The fizzing is CO₂ gas release
- Effective on faucets, showerheads, kettles, coffee makers, glass, tile

### Fabric Softening

In laundry:
- Neutralizes alkaline residue from detergent/soap (which makes fabric stiff)
- Breaks down mineral deposits in fabric fibers (hard water buildup)
- Restores fabric flexibility without coating fibers (unlike commercial softeners which deposit wax/silicone)
- Eliminates odors by neutralizing alkaline odor compounds
- Evaporates completely — no vinegar smell remains

### Hair Rinse

When used as a hair rinse:
- Restores scalp pH to ~4.5–5.5 (acidic) after alkaline castile soap wash
- Closes hair cuticle scales (lie flat in acidic conditions), increasing shine and reducing tangling
- Dissolves product buildup and hard water mineral deposits

### Antimicrobial

- Effective against *E. coli*, *Salmonella*, and some other common bacteria
- Works primarily through pH reduction — most bacteria cannot survive below pH 4
- Not a registered disinfectant (H₂O₂ is stronger for disinfection)
- Adequate for everyday surface sanitizing, not for medical-grade sterilization

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**All-Purpose Spray**:
- 1:1 white vinegar and water in spray bottle
- Optional: squeeze of dish soap for grease cutting
- Handles countertops, glass, mirrors, appliances, bathroom fixtures
- Do NOT use on marble, granite, or natural stone (acid etches them)

**Fabric Softener**:
- ½ cup in the fabric softener compartment
- No vinegar smell remains after drying
- Replaces commercial softener and dryer sheets
- Also removes musty/mildew odors from towels

**Hair Rinse (Conditioner)**:
- 1–2 tbsp vinegar in 1 cup water
- Pour through hair after washing, leave 1–2 minutes, rinse with cool water
- Closes cuticle, adds shine, removes buildup
- Do NOT mix with castile soap simultaneously (forms sticky precipitate)

**Citrus-Infused Cleaner**:
- Fill jar with citrus peels (orange, lemon, grapefruit)
- Cover with white vinegar, seal, wait 2 weeks
- Strain and dilute 1:1 with water
- Pleasant scent, enhanced degreasing from citrus oils (d-limonene)

**Dishwasher Rinse Aid**:
- Fill rinse aid compartment with white vinegar
- Prevents water spots and mineral buildup

**Descaling**:
- Kettles: Fill with equal parts vinegar and water, boil, let sit 1 hour, rinse
- Showerheads: Submerge in bag of vinegar overnight
- Coffee makers: Run full vinegar cycle, then 2–3 water cycles

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Critical Incompatibilities

- **NEVER mix with bleach**: Produces toxic chlorine gas (Cl₂): NaOCl + 2CH₃COOH → Cl₂ + 2CH₃COONa + H₂O
- **Do NOT mix with hydrogen peroxide**: Creates peracetic acid — corrosive and dangerous in enclosed spaces
- **Do NOT mix directly with castile soap**: Acid + alkaline soap = insoluble fatty acid precipitate (curdled soap scum). Use sequentially with rinse between.
- **Do NOT mix with baking soda/washing soda for cleaning**: They neutralize each other (acid + base = salt + water). The fizzing looks impressive but produces a weaker cleaning solution than either alone. Use sequentially only.

### Surface Damage

- **Natural stone** (marble, granite, travertine, limestone): Acid etches and dulls polished surfaces permanently
- **Hardwood floors**: Can damage finishes over time
- **Cast iron**: Strips seasoning
- **Aluminum**: Prolonged contact causes corrosion
- **Certain grout**: Can erode unsealed or epoxy-based grout

### General Safety

- 5% acetic acid is not dangerous but can irritate eyes and skin with prolonged contact
- Fumes can irritate respiratory passages in enclosed spaces — ventilate
- Keep away from eyes; rinse with water if contact occurs
- Not toxic if ingested at 5% concentration (it's a food product), but can cause GI irritation

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Extremely cheap ($3–4/gallon)
- Dissolves mineral deposits effectively
- Excellent fabric softener with no chemical residue
- Effective hair rinse
- Biodegradable, non-toxic at standard concentration
- Evaporates cleanly — no residue
- Replaces multiple commercial cleaning products
- Food-grade safety

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Strong odor (dissipates after drying)
- Cannot use on stone, marble, granite
- Not a registered disinfectant (use H₂O₂ for that)
- Must not be mixed with bleach, peroxide, or soap
- Less effective than citric acid for heavy descaling
- Corrosive to some metals with prolonged contact
- Not effective against all bacteria/viruses

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: Essentially indefinite. Vinegar is self-preserving due to acidity.
- **Storage**: Room temperature, away from direct sunlight
- **Container**: Original plastic jug is fine. Glass also works.
- **Notes**: May develop harmless "mother" (cellulose biofilm) over very long storage — strain out if desired; does not indicate spoilage

### Cleaning Vinegar vs. Standard

- **Standard (5%)**: Food-grade; fine for all household uses
- **Cleaning vinegar (6%)**: 20% stronger; not food-safe; slightly more effective for heavy cleaning
- **Industrial (30%)**: Dangerous concentration — causes chemical burns. Not for household use without extreme dilution.

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: Regulated as a food product. Must be at least 4% acetic acid by FDA standard.
- **EU**: Regulated as a food product. Standard table vinegar is 5–8% acetic acid.
- **EPA**: Not registered as a disinfectant at household concentrations.

## Related Ingredients

- **Apple Cider Vinegar** — Contains "mother" (beneficial bacteria), malic acid. Preferred for hair/skin rinses. More expensive.
- **Citric Acid** — Stronger chelating acid for descaling; odorless. Better for heavy limescale.
- **Washing Soda** — Alkaline counterpart; do not mix directly. Use for grease (base), use vinegar for minerals (acid).
- **Hydrogen Peroxide** — The disinfectant counterpart to vinegar's descaling. Don't mix; use sequentially.
- **Lemon Juice** — Natural acid (~5–6% citric acid); more expensive, weaker, but pleasant smell.
