# Jojoba Oil
> A liquid wax ester (not a true oil) extracted from the seeds of Simmondsia chinensis that closely mimics human sebum, offering exceptional oxidative stability, non-comedogenic skin compatibility, and versatile applications in skin care, hair care, and cosmetic formulations.

## Quick Reference
| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Common Names | Jojoba oil, jojoba wax, jojoba seed oil, ho-ho-ba oil |
| Chemical Name / IUPAC | Mixture of long-chain wax esters (primarily eicosenoyl eicosenoate and related C20-C22 monounsaturated wax esters) |
| Chemical Formula | Primarily C40-C44 wax esters (e.g., C42H82O2 for the predominant eicosenoyl eicosenoate) |
| CAS Number | 61789-91-1 |
| Molecular Weight | ~606-662 g/mol (predominant wax ester components) |
| Appearance | Clear to golden yellow liquid at room temperature; odorless to very faint nutty odor |
| pH (in solution) | Not applicable (non-aqueous; neutral when tested with pH-reactive indicators) |
| Solubility | Insoluble in water; miscible with most non-polar solvents, other oils, and waxes; partially miscible with ethanol |

## Overview

Jojoba oil is the liquid wax extracted from the seeds of the jojoba plant (Simmondsia chinensis), an evergreen shrub native to the Sonoran Desert region spanning southern Arizona, southern California, and northwestern Mexico. Despite its common name, jojoba "oil" is not a triglyceride oil at all but rather a complex mixture of long-chain monounsaturated wax esters -- making it structurally unique among all commercially available plant-derived lipids. This distinction is not merely academic: it fundamentally explains jojoba's exceptional oxidative stability, its remarkable similarity to human sebum, and its superior performance characteristics in cosmetic formulations.

The jojoba plant has been used by indigenous peoples of the Sonoran Desert for centuries. The Tohono O'odham and other Native American groups used jojoba seed oil to treat sores and wounds, condition hair, and soften animal hides. The name "jojoba" derives from the O'odham word "hohowi." Modern commercial interest in jojoba surged dramatically in the 1970s following the international ban on sperm whale oil (spermaceti), which had been the primary source of liquid wax esters for the cosmetic and industrial lubrication industries. Jojoba wax esters proved to be an excellent renewable substitute, and the jojoba industry rapidly developed in the southwestern United States, Argentina, Israel, Peru, Australia, and other arid-region nations.

Today, jojoba oil is one of the most widely used natural ingredients in the cosmetic and personal care industry, appearing in moisturizers, serums, cleansers, lip balms, sunscreens, hair conditioners, and makeup. Its popularity stems from its non-comedogenic nature, excellent skin compatibility, long shelf life, and the growing consumer demand for naturally derived cosmetic ingredients. Annual global production is estimated at 3,000-5,000 tonnes, with Israel, Argentina, and the United States being the primary producers.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Wax Ester Composition

Jojoba "oil" is composed of approximately 97% wax esters, with the remaining 3% consisting of free fatty acids, free fatty alcohols, tocopherols, sterols, and trace hydrocarbons. The wax esters are formed by esterification of long-chain monounsaturated fatty acids with long-chain monounsaturated fatty alcohols:

Fatty acid + Fatty alcohol -> Wax ester + H2O

The predominant components are:

| Wax Ester | Acid Moiety | Alcohol Moiety | Approximate % |
|---|---|---|---|
| C40:2 | C20:1 (gondoic acid) | C20:1 (gadoleyl alcohol) | 45-55% |
| C42:2 | C22:1 (erucic acid) | C20:1 (gadoleyl alcohol) | 15-25% |
| C42:2 | C20:1 (gondoic acid) | C22:1 (erucyl alcohol) | 10-15% |
| C44:2 | C22:1 (erucic acid) | C22:1 (erucyl alcohol) | 5-10% |
| C38:2 | C18:1 (oleic acid) | C20:1 (gadoleyl alcohol) | 1-3% |

The double bonds in both the acid and alcohol chains are in the cis (Z) configuration at the delta-9 position (for C20:1 chains) or delta-13 position (for C22:1 chains).

### Why Wax Esters Matter (Comparison with Triglyceride Oils)

The distinction between wax esters and triglycerides is central to understanding jojoba's unique properties:

| Property | Wax Esters (Jojoba) | Triglycerides (Most Plant Oils) |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Fatty acid + Fatty alcohol (1:1 ester) | Glycerol + 3 Fatty acids (triester) |
| Oxidative stability | Very high (no glycerol backbone to oxidize) | Lower (glycerol backbone susceptible) |
| Rancidity | Extremely resistant | Susceptible (especially polyunsaturated oils) |
| Comedogenicity | Very low (0-2 on scale of 0-5) | Variable (0-5 depending on fatty acid profile) |
| Similarity to sebum | Very high | Low |
| Feel on skin | Light, non-greasy, absorbs well | Variable, often heavier |
| Shelf life | 5+ years (some sources cite indefinite) | 6 months to 2 years typically |

### Similarity to Human Sebum

Human sebum is composed of approximately:
- 25% wax esters
- 41% triglycerides and fatty acids
- 12% squalene
- 16% wax diesters
- 6% cholesterol and cholesterol esters

Jojoba's wax esters closely resemble the wax ester fraction of human sebum in chain length, degree of unsaturation, and molecular geometry. No other commercially available plant product matches human sebum wax esters as closely. This structural similarity is the basis for jojoba's excellent skin compatibility and its reputed ability to "balance" sebum production (discussed under Mechanism of Action).

### Minor Components

- **Tocopherols (Vitamin E):** 30-70 mg/100g (primarily alpha-tocopherol). Natural antioxidants that contribute to jojoba's stability.
- **Phytosterols:** Campesterol, stigmasterol, beta-sitosterol. Anti-inflammatory properties.
- **Simmondsin:** A cyanoglucoside found in jojoba meal (seed cake after pressing) but present only in trace amounts in the expressed oil. Simmondsin is an appetite suppressant in animals and is the primary reason jojoba meal cannot be used as animal feed without detoxification.
- **Free fatty acids:** Less than 2% in high-quality cold-pressed oil
- **Wax ester hydrocarbons:** Trace amounts

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Cultivation

Jojoba is a hardy desert shrub that thrives in arid and semi-arid conditions:
- **Climate:** Requires hot, dry summers and mild winters. Tolerates temperatures from -5 to 50 degrees C. Requires less than 500 mm annual rainfall.
- **Soil:** Grows in well-drained sandy or gravelly soils; tolerates poor, alkaline soils. Cannot tolerate waterlogging.
- **Growth:** Slow-growing perennial; begins bearing seeds at 3-5 years, reaches full production at 8-10 years. Plants can live 100-200 years.
- **Yield:** 1-3 kg seeds per plant per year at maturity; seeds contain 45-60% wax by weight
- **Harvesting:** Seeds (technically capsules containing 1-3 seeds) are harvested when mature, typically by collecting fallen seeds from the ground or by mechanical shaking/sweeping.

### Cold-Press Extraction

The preferred method for cosmetic-grade jojoba oil:

1. **Seed cleaning:** Seeds are cleaned to remove debris, leaves, and immature seeds.
2. **Mechanical pressing:** Seeds are fed into an expeller press (screw press) at ambient or slightly elevated temperature (below 50 degrees C to qualify as "cold-pressed"). The press applies mechanical pressure to crush the seeds and squeeze out the liquid wax.
3. **Filtration:** The crude pressed oil is filtered to remove seed meal particles. Multiple filtration stages may be used (coarse filtration, fine filtration, and sometimes microfiltration).
4. **Settling/Degumming:** The oil is allowed to settle, and any phospholipids or mucilaginous material is removed.
5. **Quality testing:** The finished oil is tested for acid value (free fatty acid content), peroxide value, color, odor, and pesticide residues.

Cold-pressed jojoba oil retains its natural golden color and full complement of tocopherols and other minor components.

### Solvent Extraction

For maximum yield, solvent extraction using hexane can recover additional wax from the seed meal remaining after pressing:

1. The pressed seed cake is extracted with food-grade hexane
2. The hexane-oil miscella is separated from the extracted meal
3. Hexane is removed by distillation (recovered and recycled)
4. The resulting oil may have different characteristics from cold-pressed oil

Solvent-extracted jojoba oil may be refined (bleached and deodorized) for use in formulations where a colorless, odorless product is desired.

### Refining (Optional)

Crude jojoba oil may be refined through:
- **Acid degumming:** Phosphoric or citric acid to remove phospholipids
- **Bleaching:** Activated clay (bentonite or montmorillonite) to remove pigments
- **Deodorization:** Steam distillation under vacuum to remove volatile odor compounds
- **Winterization:** Cooling to precipitate and remove higher-melting-point wax esters (produces a more uniformly liquid product)

Refined jojoba oil is clear, nearly colorless, and odorless. While preferred for some cosmetic applications, it has lost some of its natural antioxidants and bioactive minor components.

### Home Extraction

Home extraction of jojoba oil is theoretically possible but impractical:
- Jojoba seeds can be pressed using a manual or small electric oil press
- Yield is low (home presses are less efficient than industrial expellers)
- Seeds are expensive and difficult to obtain raw in most regions
- The resulting crude oil requires filtration
- Commercial cold-pressed jojoba oil is of higher quality and more cost-effective than home-pressed

## Mechanism of Action

### Sebum Regulation Hypothesis

The most widely cited mechanism for jojoba's skin benefits is the "sebum balancing" effect:

1. **Structural mimicry:** Because jojoba wax esters closely resemble the wax ester component of human sebum, they are hypothesized to "trick" the skin's sebum-regulating mechanisms into reducing sebum production. The theory proposes that sebocytes (sebum-producing cells) receive feedback signals from the presence of wax esters on the skin surface, and jojoba's wax esters provide a signal indicating adequate sebum coverage.

2. **Evidence assessment:** This mechanism is widely repeated in cosmetic marketing and dermatological literature but has limited direct experimental evidence. No controlled clinical trial has conclusively demonstrated that topical jojoba application reduces sebum production rates through receptor-mediated feedback. The concept is biologically plausible (sebaceous gland activity is regulated by multiple feedback mechanisms) but remains a hypothesis rather than an established mechanism.

3. **Practical observation:** Many users with oily skin report that regular jojoba oil use does not exacerbate oiliness and may subjectively reduce it. This may be because jojoba dissolves and helps remove sebum plugs (comedones) and provides non-comedogenic moisture, reducing the compensatory overproduction of sebum that can occur with drying cleansers.

### Moisturization / Barrier Function

Jojoba oil improves skin hydration through several established mechanisms:

1. **Occlusive barrier:** Jojoba wax esters form a semi-occlusive film on the skin surface that reduces transepidermal water loss (TEWL) without completely blocking the skin's ability to breathe. This is a gentler occlusion than petroleum-based products.

2. **Integration with skin lipids:** The structural similarity of jojoba wax esters to skin lipids allows them to integrate into the intercellular lipid matrix of the stratum corneum, reinforcing the skin barrier.

3. **Emollient effect:** Jojoba fills the gaps between desquamating corneocytes, creating a smooth skin surface that reflects light more evenly (reducing the appearance of dryness and fine lines).

### Anti-Inflammatory Properties

Jojoba oil has demonstrated anti-inflammatory activity:
- The wax esters themselves have mild anti-inflammatory properties
- Gadoleic acid (C20:1, the predominant fatty acid) has been shown to have anti-inflammatory activity in some in vitro studies
- Phytosterols (campesterol, stigmasterol, beta-sitosterol) are known anti-inflammatory agents that inhibit the complement system and reduce prostaglandin synthesis

### Antimicrobial Properties

Limited antimicrobial activity has been documented:
- Jojoba oil has shown inhibitory effects against some bacteria (Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa) and fungi (Candida albicans) in in vitro studies
- The antimicrobial activity is modest compared to essential oils or dedicated antimicrobial agents
- May contribute to anti-acne effects by suppressing Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes)

### Hair Care Mechanisms

1. **Cuticle smoothing:** Jojoba wax esters coat and smooth hair cuticle scales, reducing friction, tangling, and breakage
2. **Moisture retention:** The semi-occlusive film reduces moisture loss from the hair cortex
3. **Shine enhancement:** The smooth wax ester film reflects light, increasing apparent shine
4. **Sebum dissolution:** Jojoba can dissolve and help remove excess sebum and product buildup from the scalp

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Facial Moisturizer / Serum Base**
- Use 3-5 drops of jojoba oil alone as a facial moisturizer
- Or blend with other carrier oils: 50% jojoba, 30% rosehip seed oil, 20% argan oil
- Apply to slightly damp skin for best absorption
- Suitable for all skin types, including oily and acne-prone

**Oil Cleansing Method (OCM)**
- Apply 1-2 teaspoons jojoba oil to dry face
- Massage gently for 1-2 minutes, focusing on areas with blackheads or makeup
- Place a warm, damp washcloth over face for 30 seconds (steam softens)
- Gently wipe away oil and impurities
- The principle: "like dissolves like" -- jojoba's similarity to sebum helps dissolve sebum plugs

**Lip Balm**
- 2 tablespoons jojoba oil
- 2 tablespoons beeswax (grated or pellets)
- 1 tablespoon coconut oil (for hardness and flavor)
- Optional: 5-10 drops essential oil (peppermint, vanilla)
- Melt beeswax and coconut oil together, stir in jojoba oil, add essential oil, pour into tubes or tins

**Beard Oil**
- 1 oz (30 mL) jojoba oil (base)
- 0.5 oz (15 mL) argan oil
- 5-8 drops essential oil blend (cedarwood, sandalwood, or tea tree)
- Combine in a dropper bottle; apply 3-5 drops to beard and skin beneath

**Hair Treatment / Hot Oil Treatment**
- Warm 2-3 tablespoons jojoba oil to a comfortable temperature (not hot)
- Apply to scalp and hair, concentrating on dry ends
- Wrap hair in a warm towel or shower cap for 20-30 minutes
- Shampoo out (may require two washes)

**Cuticle Oil**
- Use pure jojoba oil directly on cuticles
- Apply a drop to each nail bed and massage in
- Use daily for soft, healthy cuticles

**DIY Makeup Remover**
- Jojoba oil alone is an effective makeup remover, including waterproof mascara
- Apply to a cotton pad or directly to skin
- Gently wipe or massage to dissolve makeup
- Follow with a gentle cleanser if desired

**Carrier Oil for Essential Oils**
- Jojoba is one of the best carrier oils for diluting essential oils for topical application
- Standard dilution: 2-3% essential oil (12-18 drops per ounce of jojoba)
- Its long shelf life means essential oil blends made with jojoba remain stable

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Cosmetics and personal care:** The largest commercial use. Found in moisturizers, sunscreens, lip products, foundations, hair conditioners, shampoos, and anti-aging products.
- **Pharmaceutical:** Carrier/vehicle in topical drug formulations; soft gelatin capsule lubricant.
- **Lubrication:** High-performance lubricant for precision instruments, especially where petroleum-based lubricants are undesirable. Used in some automotive and industrial applications.
- **Leather treatment:** Conditions and preserves leather without darkening.
- **Candles:** Can be blended with other waxes for candle making (remains liquid, so typically blended).

### Historical Uses

- **Indigenous medicine:** Tohono O'odham, Seri, and other Sonoran Desert peoples used jojoba seed paste to treat sores, burns, and skin conditions. Seeds were roasted and eaten in small quantities. The wax was used as hair conditioner and leather softener.
- **Sperm whale oil replacement:** Following the 1971 Endangered Species Conservation Act and the 1972 Marine Mammal Protection Act, which effectively banned sperm whale oil imports into the United States, jojoba became the primary renewable replacement for spermaceti wax in cosmetics and industrial applications.
- **World War II:** The U.S. government investigated jojoba oil as a domestic alternative to imported sperm whale oil for precision lubrication in military equipment.

## Efficacy & Research

**Skin Barrier / Moisturization:**
Habashy, R.R. et al. (2005) published in *Pharmacological Research* (51(2): 95-105) demonstrating that jojoba liquid wax accelerated wound closure in an in vivo model and was well-tolerated by skin tissue, supporting its use as a skin-compatible emollient.

**Anti-Inflammatory:**
Ranzato, E. et al. (2011) published in *Phytomedicine* (18(12): 1101-1105) showing that jojoba oil promoted wound healing and demonstrated anti-inflammatory activity in a scratch wound assay model, reducing inflammatory cytokine production.

**Anti-Acne:**
Meier, L. et al. (2012) published in the *Forschende Komplementarmedizin* (19(2): 75-80) a small clinical trial showing that jojoba oil-based facial masks applied 2-3 times weekly for 6 weeks produced a statistically significant reduction in acne lesion count (mean reduction of 54%) in subjects with mild acne. The study was small (n=194 completed) and did not have a placebo control group, limiting the strength of the evidence.

**Oxidative Stability:**
Wisniak, J. (1994) published in *Industrial Crops and Products* (3(1-2): 43-68) a comprehensive review of jojoba oil chemistry confirming its exceptional oxidative stability, with peroxide values remaining low for years under proper storage conditions. The wax ester structure, combined with natural tocopherols, confers an estimated shelf life of 5+ years.

**Hair Effects:**
Keis, K. et al. (2005) published in the *Journal of Cosmetic Science* (56(5): 327-339) demonstrating that wax ester-based oils (including jojoba) were effective at reducing combing force (detangling) and improving hair manageability, with effects proportional to the degree of hair damage.

**Non-Comedogenicity:**
Jojoba oil is rated 2 on the comedogenicity scale of 0-5 (where 0 is non-comedogenic and 5 is highly comedogenic), based on the rabbit ear assay methodology established by Fulton, J.E. et al. (1976) in the *Journal of Investigative Dermatology*. However, comedogenicity testing has significant limitations, and individual responses vary.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### General Safety
- Jojoba oil has an excellent safety profile for topical use
- Non-toxic, non-irritating, and non-sensitizing in the vast majority of users
- The Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) Expert Panel has assessed jojoba oil as safe for use in cosmetic formulations at current concentrations of use

### Allergic Reactions
- True allergy to jojoba oil is extremely rare but has been documented in case reports
- Always perform a patch test before first use: apply a small amount to the inner forearm, cover with a bandage, and observe for 24-48 hours
- Individuals with known sensitivity to plants in the family Simmondsiaceae should avoid use (though the family contains only this one species)

### Ingestion
- Jojoba oil is NOT edible and should not be consumed
- Wax esters are not digestible by human lipases (which are specific to triglyceride ester bonds)
- Ingestion may cause gastrointestinal distress, diarrhea, and steatorrhea (fatty stools)
- Simmondsin contamination in crude/unrefined products could pose additional concerns if ingested

### Pregnancy and Nursing
- No known contraindications for topical use during pregnancy or nursing
- As with all cosmetic products, consult healthcare provider if concerns arise

### Pet Safety
- Jojoba oil is generally considered safe for use on dogs and horses (skin and coat conditioning)
- Avoid use on cats (cats groom themselves and may ingest the oil, and their livers have limited capacity for metabolizing unusual lipids)

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages
- Closest plant match to human sebum (wax ester composition)
- Exceptional oxidative stability and shelf life (5+ years)
- Non-comedogenic (does not clog pores for most users)
- Suitable for all skin types, including oily and acne-prone
- Light, non-greasy feel with excellent skin absorption
- No trans fats, no allergens (extremely rare sensitization)
- Sustainable desert crop requiring minimal water and no pesticides
- Versatile: skin, hair, nails, lip care, carrier oil
- Does not go rancid under normal storage conditions
- Excellent carrier oil for essential oils (stability extends essential oil blend shelf life)

### Disadvantages / Limitations
- Relatively expensive compared to other carrier oils ($10-25 per 4 oz vs. $3-8 for sweet almond or grapeseed)
- Not edible (unlike olive oil, coconut oil)
- Limited global supply (niche desert crop)
- Pure jojoba provides minimal sun protection (SPF approximately 4) and should not be relied upon as sunscreen
- "Sebum-balancing" claims lack strong clinical evidence
- Liquid at room temperature; not suitable as sole ingredient where a solid product is needed
- May feel insufficiently moisturizing for very dry skin when used alone (lacks the heavy occlusion of petroleum jelly or shea butter)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life:** 5+ years (some sources report essentially indefinite shelf life for pure, properly stored jojoba oil)
- **Storage conditions:** Store in a cool, dark place. While jojoba is extremely stable, heat and light exposure should be minimized to preserve the tocopherol content.
- **Container:** Dark glass bottles (amber or cobalt blue) are ideal. Aluminum bottles are also acceptable. Avoid clear glass or plastic for long-term storage.
- **Temperature:** Jojoba oil may partially solidify or become cloudy below approximately 10 degrees C (50 degrees F). This is normal behavior for wax esters and does not indicate spoilage. Warming to room temperature restores clarity and fluidity with no loss of quality.
- **Signs of degradation:** Off odor (rancid, painty) or significant color change (darkening) may indicate degradation, though these are extremely rare with jojoba due to its inherent stability.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Typical price:** $8-15 USD per 4 oz (120 mL) for cold-pressed, unrefined; $15-30 per 8 oz. Organic and golden (unrefined) varieties command a premium.
- **Price factors:** Jojoba is more expensive than most carrier oils because:
  - Plants take 5-10 years to reach full production
  - Yields are relatively low (desert crop)
  - Growing regions are limited (arid/semi-arid climates)
  - Demand from the cosmetics industry is strong
- **Sources:** Health food stores, online specialty retailers (Mountain Rose Herbs, Plant Therapy, NOW Foods), beauty supply stores, Amazon.
- **Quality indicators:**
  - **Golden/unrefined:** Rich golden color, faint nutty smell. Retains tocopherols and phytosterols. Preferred for DIY skin care.
  - **Clear/refined:** Colorless, odorless. Processing removes some beneficial minor components but may be preferred for formulations where color or scent interference is undesirable.
  - **Organic:** USDA-certified organic is available and preferred by some users.
  - **Cold-pressed:** Extraction without excessive heat; preserves bioactive compounds.

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA:** Jojoba oil is not regulated as a drug. As a cosmetic ingredient, it is subject to FDA cosmetic regulations (Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act). Not approved as a food ingredient in the United States.
- **EU:** Listed in the EU Cosmetic Ingredient database (INCI name: Simmondsia Chinensis Seed Oil). No restrictions on concentration in cosmetic products.
- **CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review):** Assessed as safe for use in cosmetic formulations.
- **USDA Organic:** Organic jojoba oil is available from certified organic producers.
- **CITES:** Not a CITES-listed species. No trade restrictions on jojoba products.

## Related Ingredients

- **Squalane:** Another naturally derived emollient (from olive oil or sugarcane) that mimics a component of human sebum (squalene). Complementary to jojoba in formulations.
- **Argan Oil:** Triglyceride oil from Argania spinosa. Rich in oleic and linoleic acids. Often paired with jojoba in facial oils. Less stable than jojoba due to triglyceride structure.
- **Rosehip Seed Oil:** Rich in linoleic acid and tretinoin (all-trans retinoic acid). Commonly blended with jojoba for anti-aging formulations. Shorter shelf life.
- **Meadowfoam Seed Oil:** Another liquid wax-like oil with good oxidative stability, similar in some respects to jojoba but with a different wax ester profile (C20-C22 fatty acids esterified to C20-C22 fatty alcohols).
- **[Magnesium Hydroxide](magnesium-hydroxide.md):** Sometimes used alongside jojoba oil in natural deodorant formulations (jojoba as the carrier/moisturizer, magnesium hydroxide as the active deodorant).
