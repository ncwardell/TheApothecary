# Apple Cider Vinegar

> A fermented liquid produced from crushed apples through a two-stage microbial process, yielding a complex solution of acetic acid, malic acid, and beneficial bacterial cultures ("the mother") widely used as a hair rinse, skin toner, and household cleaner.

## Overview

Apple cider vinegar (ACV) is produced by the fermentation of apple juice or cider through two sequential microbial processes: first, yeasts (primarily *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) convert sugars to ethanol (alcoholic fermentation), and then acetic acid bacteria (primarily *Acetobacter* and *Gluconacetobacter* species) oxidize the ethanol to acetic acid (acetic fermentation). The resulting product typically contains 4-8% acetic acid by weight, along with residual malic acid from the apples, trace amounts of citric acid, various polyphenols, amino acids, and minerals.

Raw, unfiltered ACV contains "the mother" -- a visible mat of cellulose and acetic acid bacteria that forms during fermentation. This biofilm is composed primarily of bacterial cellulose produced by *Acetobacter* species and contains living bacterial cultures. While often marketed as a probiotic, the bacteria in the mother are acetic acid bacteria, not the lactic acid bacteria (such as *Lactobacillus*) typically associated with gut health benefits. Nevertheless, the mother does contain bioactive compounds including enzymes and proteins that are removed by filtration and pasteurization in commercial clear vinegars.

The use of vinegar dates back at least 7,000 years, with evidence of its production in ancient Babylon. Hippocrates reportedly used vinegar mixed with honey (oxymel) for treating coughs and wounds around 400 BCE. In the context of natural personal care, ACV has experienced a dramatic resurgence in popularity, particularly as a hair rinse for restoring the scalp's natural acid mantle, closing the hair cuticle after washing with alkaline soaps, and as a gentle skin toner. Its effectiveness in these roles is grounded in straightforward acid-base chemistry and the well-established relationship between pH and the structural integrity of hair and skin.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Primary Acid Components

**Acetic acid (CH₃COOH)** constitutes 4-8% of ACV by weight (typically 5% in commercial products). It is a weak acid with a pKa of 4.76, meaning that at the typical pH of diluted ACV (~4.5-5.5), a significant fraction exists in the protonated (undissociated) form. This is relevant because the undissociated form is more effective at penetrating microbial cell membranes and has different skin-interaction properties than the dissociated acetate ion.

**Malic acid (C₄H₆O₅)** is the dominant organic acid in apples and persists through fermentation. It is a dicarboxylic alpha-hydroxy acid (AHA) with pKa values of 3.40 and 5.11. As an AHA, malic acid has mild keratolytic (exfoliating) properties, helping to loosen the bonds between dead skin cells. The concentration in ACV is typically 0.1-0.5%.

### The Mother

The "mother of vinegar" is a biofilm (pellicle) composed primarily of:

- **Bacterial cellulose**: An extracellular polysaccharide produced by *Acetobacter xylinum* and related species. This cellulose is chemically identical to plant cellulose (beta-1,4-linked glucose polymer) but has a unique nanofibrillar structure with fibers 20-100 nm in diameter, forming a dense, hydrated mesh.
- **Acetic acid bacteria**: Living cells of *Acetobacter*, *Gluconacetobacter*, and *Komagataeibacter* species embedded in the cellulose matrix
- **Enzymes**: Including alcohol dehydrogenase and aldehyde dehydrogenase (the enzymes that convert ethanol to acetic acid)
- **Proteins and polypeptides**: From bacterial metabolism and yeast autolysis
- **Yeast cells**: Residual *Saccharomyces* and wild yeasts

### pH Chemistry

The pH of ACV is governed primarily by the acetic acid equilibrium:

CH₃COOH ⇌ CH₃COO⁻ + H⁺ (Ka = 1.8 x 10⁻⁵)

At 5% concentration (~0.83 M), the pH of undiluted ACV is approximately 2.4-2.5. When diluted for hair rinse use (typically 1-2 tablespoons per cup of water, or roughly 1:8 to 1:16 dilution), the pH rises to approximately 3.5-4.5, which falls within the optimal range for hair cuticle closure (pH 3.5-5.5) and is close to the natural pH of the skin's acid mantle (pH 4.5-5.5).

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

Commercial ACV is produced through controlled two-stage fermentation:

**Stage 1 -- Alcoholic Fermentation:**
1. Apples are washed, crushed, and pressed to extract juice
2. The juice (specific gravity ~1.045-1.065, corresponding to ~11-16% sugar) is transferred to fermentation tanks
3. *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* yeast is inoculated (or wild fermentation is allowed in traditional methods)
4. Fermentation proceeds at 15-25°C for 1-4 weeks until most sugar is converted to ethanol (target: 5-8% ABV)

**Stage 2 -- Acetic Fermentation:**

*Submerged fermentation (industrial)*: The cider is inoculated with *Acetobacter* cultures in a large aerated tank (acetator). Air is bubbled continuously through the liquid at 25-30°C. Ethanol is oxidized to acetic acid over 24-72 hours in this highly efficient process.

*Surface culture method (traditional/artisanal)*: The cider is placed in shallow, open containers or barrels. A mother culture is added or allowed to form naturally. The bacteria grow at the air-liquid interface, slowly converting ethanol to acetic acid over 3-6 months. This method produces the characteristic mother pellicle.

*Trickling/generator method*: Cider is trickled over a column packed with wood shavings or other media colonized by acetic acid bacteria. Air flows upward through the column. Conversion takes 1-3 weeks.

**Finishing:**
- Commercial filtered ACV: Filtered to remove the mother, then pasteurized (heated to 60-70°C for 30 minutes)
- Raw unfiltered ACV: Bottled with the mother intact, unpasteurized

### Home Production

Making ACV at home is straightforward and requires minimal equipment:

**Equipment:**
- Large glass jar (1-2 quart / 1-2 liter)
- Cheesecloth or breathable cloth cover
- Rubber band
- pH strips or test kit (optional but recommended)

**Ingredients:**
- Organic apple scraps (cores, peels) or whole apples, chopped -- enough to fill jar 3/4 full
- Filtered water (chlorine-free; chlorine inhibits fermentation)
- 1 tablespoon raw sugar or honey per cup of water (to kickstart fermentation)

**Method:**
1. Fill jar 3/4 with apple scraps. Add sugar-water solution to cover completely
2. Cover with cheesecloth secured by rubber band (bacteria need oxygen)
3. Store in a warm, dark place (65-85°F / 18-30°C)
4. Stir daily for the first 1-2 weeks to prevent mold and promote aeration
5. After 2-3 weeks, strain out apple solids through cheesecloth
6. Return liquid to clean jar, cover again with cheesecloth
7. Allow to ferment for an additional 3-6 weeks, tasting periodically
8. A mother will begin to form on the surface -- this is normal and desirable
9. When the vinegar reaches desired acidity (tangy but not overpowering, pH ~3-4), cap tightly and store
10. The mother can be used to start subsequent batches, dramatically accelerating the process

**Troubleshooting:**
- White or tan film on surface = mother forming (good)
- Green, black, or fuzzy growth = mold (discard and start over; usually caused by exposure above the liquid line)
- Alcohol smell = still in stage 1; needs more time and aeration
- No activity after 1 week = water may contain chlorine, or temperature too low

## Mechanism of Action

### Hair Rinse: pH Restoration and Cuticle Closure

The hair shaft is composed of three layers: the medulla (core), cortex (bulk of the fiber), and cuticle (overlapping scale-like cells on the outer surface). The cuticle scales lie flat and smooth at the hair's natural pH (4.5-5.5) but lift and separate in alkaline conditions (pH >7).

Washing hair with alkaline cleansers (castile soap at pH ~9-10, bar shampoo, baking soda at pH ~8.3) raises the hair's pH, causing cuticle scales to lift. This results in:
- Rough, tangled hair
- Loss of shine (smooth cuticles reflect light; raised cuticles scatter it)
- Increased porosity and moisture loss
- Vulnerability to mechanical damage

An ACV rinse (diluted to pH ~3.5-4.5) rapidly restores the acidic environment around the hair shaft. The hydrogen ions from the acetic and malic acids protonate the negatively charged keratin surface, neutralizing the alkaline residue and causing the cuticle scales to contract and lie flat again. This produces:
- Smooth, detangled hair
- Increased shine and light reflection
- Reduced porosity
- Improved manageability

This is not a cosmetic myth -- the relationship between pH and cuticle structure has been extensively documented in trichology research using scanning electron microscopy.

### Skin Toner: Acid Mantle Restoration

The skin's acid mantle is a thin film of sebum and sweat on the skin surface with a pH of 4.5-5.5. This acidic environment supports the resident microbiome (beneficial bacteria) and inhibits pathogenic organisms. Alkaline cleansers disrupt this acid mantle, temporarily raising skin pH and potentially allowing opportunistic bacteria to proliferate.

An ACV toner (diluted to ~5-10% ACV in water, yielding pH ~4-5) helps restore the acid mantle after cleansing. Additionally, the malic acid in ACV functions as a mild alpha-hydroxy acid, providing gentle chemical exfoliation by weakening the desmosomes (protein bridges) that bind dead corneocytes in the stratum corneum.

### Antimicrobial Activity

Acetic acid has well-documented antimicrobial properties. The mechanism involves:

1. **Membrane disruption**: Undissociated acetic acid (the protonated form) is lipophilic and can pass through bacterial cell membranes. Once inside the more alkaline intracellular environment, it dissociates, releasing protons that acidify the cytoplasm and disrupting cellular processes.

2. **Protein denaturation**: At low pH, bacterial proteins can become denatured, disrupting enzymatic function.

3. **ATP depletion**: Cells expend energy pumping out excess protons, depleting ATP reserves.

ACV at full strength (pH ~2.5) is bactericidal against many common pathogens, including *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Escherichia coli*, and *Candida albicans*. At typical diluted-use concentrations, the effect is bacteriostatic (growth-inhibiting) rather than bactericidal.

### Clarifying / Mineral Deposit Removal

In hard water areas, calcium and magnesium carbonates and sulfates can deposit on hair, leaving it dull, stiff, and difficult to manage. The acetic acid in ACV dissolves these mineral deposits through acid-base reactions:

CaCO₃ + 2CH₃COOH → Ca(CH₃COO)₂ + H₂O + CO₂

This chelation and dissolution of mineral buildup is one reason ACV rinses are particularly beneficial for those washing with hard water.

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Hair Rinse (Most Common Use)**:
- **Standard dilution**: 1-2 tablespoons ACV per 1 cup (240 mL) water
- **Method**: After shampooing (especially after castile soap or bar shampoo), pour the rinse through hair, let sit 1-3 minutes, then rinse out (or leave in for extra conditioning)
- **Frequency**: Every wash or every other wash
- **Enhanced version**: Add 1-2 drops rosemary or lavender essential oil to the rinse water
- **For dry hair**: Use less ACV (1 tablespoon per cup) and consider adding 1 teaspoon honey
- **For oily hair**: Use more ACV (2 tablespoons per cup) and consider adding a squeeze of lemon

**Facial Toner**:
- Dilute ACV 1:3 to 1:5 with water or hydrosol (rose water, witch hazel)
- Apply with cotton pad after cleansing
- Follow with moisturizer
- Start with higher dilution and increase concentration gradually
- Patch test first, as some individuals are sensitive

**Scalp Treatment (for dandruff)**:
- Mix equal parts ACV and water
- Apply directly to scalp with spray bottle or cotton ball
- Massage gently, leave 5-15 minutes
- Shampoo and rinse
- Use 1-2 times per week

**Sunburn Relief Soak**:
- Add 1-2 cups ACV to a lukewarm bath
- Soak for 15-20 minutes
- The mild acidity may help restore skin pH disrupted by UV damage

**All-Purpose Cleaning Spray**:
- Mix equal parts ACV and water in a spray bottle
- Add 10-15 drops of tea tree or lemon essential oil
- Effective on countertops, glass, and general surfaces
- Do NOT use on marble, granite, or other natural stone (acid damage)

**Fruit and Vegetable Wash**:
- 1 tablespoon ACV per 1 cup water
- Soak produce for 5-10 minutes, then rinse with plain water

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Food industry**: Pickling, preserving, salad dressings, condiments
- **Agriculture**: Organic herbicide (high-concentration vinegar), animal feed supplement
- **Commercial personal care**: Ingredient in shampoos, conditioners, toners, and acne treatments
- **Cleaning products**: Component in "green" cleaning product lines

### Historical Uses

- **Ancient Egypt**: Cleopatra reportedly dissolved pearls in vinegar (likely a conflation with strong mineral acid, but vinegar was used as a solvent and preservative)
- **Hippocratic medicine (400 BCE)**: Used as a wound antiseptic and mixed with honey (oxymel) for coughs
- **Roman soldiers**: Drank posca (vinegar diluted with water) as a standard beverage
- **18th-19th century**: "Toilet vinegar" -- scented vinegar used as a skin toner and deodorizer, was a standard part of women's grooming
- **Traditional Chinese medicine**: Used for blood stasis and detoxification (evidence base is weak)
- **American folk medicine**: Widely promoted for a vast range of ailments; popularized in the 20th century by D.C. Jarvis in *Folk Medicine* (1958)

## Efficacy & Research

### Hair and Scalp

- **pH and hair cuticle**: Dias et al. (2014) in the *International Journal of Trichology* demonstrated through SEM imaging that hair treated with alkaline solutions (pH >8) showed significantly raised cuticle scales, while acidic rinses (pH 3.5-5.5) restored cuticle flatness. This directly supports the mechanism of ACV rinses.

- **Shampoo pH survey**: Gavazzoni Dias (2015) published in *International Journal of Trichology* showing that most commercial shampoos have pH values of 5-7 and recommended acidic formulations for hair health, supporting the use of acidic rinses.

- **Dandruff**: The antifungal properties of acetic acid against *Malassezia* species (the yeast implicated in dandruff) have been demonstrated in vitro, though no rigorous clinical trial has compared ACV head-to-head with conventional anti-dandruff treatments.

### Antimicrobial Properties

- **Broad-spectrum activity**: Yagnik et al. (2018) in *Scientific Reports* (Nature) demonstrated that ACV had antimicrobial activity against *E. coli*, *S. aureus*, and *Candida albicans* in vitro, with effectiveness dependent on concentration and contact time.

- **Wound care**: Halstead et al. (2015) in the *International Wound Journal* found that acetic acid (0.5-3%) was effective against biofilms of wound pathogens, supporting traditional use of vinegar in wound management.

### Skin Health

- **Atopic dermatitis**: A small randomized controlled trial by Luu et al. (2019) published in the *Annals of Dermatology* found that ACV soaks did NOT significantly improve skin barrier integrity in atopic dermatitis patients compared to plain water soaks, although the study had methodological limitations and a small sample size.

- **Acid mantle**: Lambers et al. (2006) in the *International Journal of Cosmetic Science* confirmed that the skin's natural pH is approximately 4.7 and that alkaline disruption has measurable effects on barrier function and microbial ecology.

### Blood Sugar / Metabolic Effects (Internal Use)

- **Glycemic response**: Johnston et al. (2004) in *Diabetes Care* showed that vinegar consumption (20 mL in water) before a high-carbohydrate meal improved insulin sensitivity in insulin-resistant subjects by 34%. This is the most robust metabolic finding for ACV, though it applies to oral consumption rather than topical use.

### Strength of Evidence Assessment

The evidence for ACV is strongest for:
- pH-based hair cuticle effects (well-supported by trichology research)
- Antimicrobial activity (well-supported by in vitro studies)
- Mineral deposit removal (straightforward acid-base chemistry)

Evidence is moderate for:
- Skin toning / acid mantle restoration (plausible mechanism, limited clinical data)
- Dandruff treatment (antifungal properties demonstrated but clinical evidence lacking)

Evidence is weak or absent for:
- "Detoxification" claims
- Weight loss
- Cancer prevention
- Most of the folk medicine claims associated with internal consumption

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Dilution Is Critical

**Undiluted ACV (pH ~2.5) can cause chemical burns to skin and mucous membranes.** There are case reports in the dermatology literature of chemical burns from applying undiluted ACV to moles, warts, and skin lesions. Always dilute before topical application.

| Application | Recommended Dilution | Approximate pH |
|---|---|---|
| Hair rinse | 1:8 to 1:16 (ACV:water) | 3.5-4.5 |
| Facial toner | 1:3 to 1:5 | 3.5-4.5 |
| Body soak (bath) | 1-2 cups per full bath | 5.0-6.0 |
| Cleaning spray | 1:1 | 2.8-3.2 |

### Specific Warnings

- **Eyes**: ACV in the eyes causes significant pain and potential corneal damage. Rinse immediately and thoroughly with water for at least 15 minutes. Seek medical attention if pain persists.
- **Dental enamel**: ACV is erosive to tooth enamel. If consuming internally, always dilute heavily, drink through a straw, and rinse mouth with plain water afterward. Do not brush teeth immediately after consumption (enamel is softened by acid).
- **Open wounds**: Do not apply to open wounds, cuts, or broken skin. The pain will be significant and the acid can damage healing tissue.
- **Sensitive skin**: Patch test before first use. Apply diluted ACV to the inner wrist and wait 24 hours. Discontinue if redness, burning, or irritation develops.
- **Drug interactions**: ACV may interact with diuretics, laxatives, and diabetes/insulin medications (if consumed internally). Topical use has no known drug interactions.
- **Children**: Use more dilute solutions on children's skin and hair. Avoid in young children who might get it in their eyes.
- **Natural stone surfaces**: Do not use ACV on marble, granite, travertine, or other calcareous stones. The acid etches and permanently damages these surfaces.

### First Aid

- **Eye contact**: Flush immediately with clean water for 15+ minutes. Seek medical attention.
- **Skin burn** (from undiluted application): Flush with cool water. Apply a bland emollient. Seek medical attention if blistering occurs.
- **Ingestion of large quantity**: Drink water or milk to dilute. Contact poison control if more than a few tablespoons were consumed undiluted.

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Extremely inexpensive and widely available
- Multi-functional (hair care, skin care, cleaning, cooking)
- Effective pH-adjusting rinse for hair after alkaline washing
- Genuine antimicrobial properties at appropriate concentrations
- Biodegradable and environmentally benign
- Raw ACV contains beneficial bacterial cultures and enzymes
- Long shelf life with no special storage requirements
- Can be made at home from apple scraps (zero waste)
- Well-established safety profile when used appropriately

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Strong, persistent odor that some find unpleasant (dissipates as hair/skin dries)
- Can cause chemical burns if used undiluted
- Erosive to tooth enamel
- Damages natural stone surfaces
- Not a standalone hair conditioner (does not replace the need for emollients on dry hair)
- Many health claims are exaggerated or unsupported by evidence
- The "mother" has been heavily mythologized; its benefits are likely modest
- Inconsistent acid concentration between brands and homemade batches
- Cannot be used with castile soap simultaneously (acid + soap = free fatty acid precipitate)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Storage**: Room temperature, in a cool dark place. Keep cap tightly closed when not in use.
- **Container**: Glass preferred (ACV can leach chemicals from some plastics over time). The original bottle is fine for commercial products.
- **Shelf life**: Virtually indefinite. ACV is self-preserving due to its acidity. It does not spoil in the conventional sense. The acidity may increase slightly over years as residual alcohol converts to acetic acid.
- **The mother**: May continue to grow in the bottle, producing new strands and sediment. This is normal and does not indicate spoilage. Shake before use.
- **Temperature**: No refrigeration needed. Avoid freezing (it will survive freezing but may damage the mother culture).
- **Signs of degradation**: True spoilage is essentially impossible in properly made ACV. Off-flavors or loss of acidity would indicate the product was not properly fermented initially.

### Quality Indicators

- **"With the mother"**: For personal care use, choose raw, unfiltered, unpasteurized ACV
- **Organic certification**: Ensures no synthetic pesticide residues from the apples
- **Acidity level**: Should state "5% acidity" on the label (this is standard)
- **Cloudiness**: A hazy appearance with visible strands is desirable in raw ACV
- **Ingredient list**: Should contain only "organic apple cider vinegar" -- no added sugars, colors, or flavors
- **Avoid**: "Apple cider flavored vinegar" (which may be white vinegar with apple flavoring)

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: ACV is a food product regulated under FDA food safety guidelines. When labeled as vinegar, it must contain at least 4% acetic acid. No specific cosmetic regulations govern its use in personal care when sold as an ingredient.
- **EU**: Recognized as a food product. No restrictions on cosmetic use.
- **Organic certification**: Widely available as USDA Organic and EU Organic certified.
- **FTC**: The Federal Trade Commission has taken action against companies making unsubstantiated health claims about ACV, particularly regarding weight loss and disease treatment.

## Related Ingredients

- **White Distilled Vinegar** - Higher acidity (typically 5-7%), no mother, better for cleaning but lacks the polyphenols and malic acid of ACV
- **Citric Acid** - Another food-safe acid used for pH adjustment and cleaning; more consistent concentration
- **Castile Soap** - Alkaline cleanser (pH ~9-10) whose hair effects are commonly counteracted with an ACV rinse. Do NOT mix directly with ACV.
- **Baking Soda** - Alkaline counterpart; sometimes used before an ACV rinse in the "no-poo" hair care method
- **Witch Hazel** - Often used as a base for diluting ACV in toner formulations
- **Lemon Juice** - Alternative acidic rinse; contains citric acid rather than acetic acid
