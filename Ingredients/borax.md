# Borax (Sodium Tetraborate)

> A naturally occurring mineral salt of boron with alkaline pH, used as a water softener, laundry booster, mild antiseptic, and multi-purpose household cleaning agent, particularly effective in hard water conditions.

## Quick Reference

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Common Names | Borax, sodium borate, sodium tetraborate decahydrate, 20 Mule Team Borax |
| Chemical Name / IUPAC | Sodium tetraborate decahydrate |
| Chemical Formula | Na₂B₄O₇·10H₂O (decahydrate); Na₂B₄O₇ (anhydrous) |
| CAS Number | 1303-96-4 (decahydrate); 1330-43-4 (anhydrous) |
| Molecular Weight | 381.37 g/mol (decahydrate); 201.22 g/mol (anhydrous) |
| Appearance | White crystalline powder or granules; odorless |
| pH (in solution) | ~9.2 (1% solution) |
| Solubility | 47.1 g/L at 20°C in water; increases significantly with temperature |

## Overview

Borax is a naturally occurring mineral compound of boron, sodium, oxygen, and water, found in evaporite deposits formed by the repeated evaporation of seasonal lakes. The most famous deposits are in the Mojave Desert of California (Death Valley), Turkey (the world's largest reserves), and the Andean dry lakes of South America. The name "borax" derives from the Arabic *bauraq* and the Persian *burah*, both meaning "white."

The iconic "20 Mule Team Borax" brand takes its name from the mule teams that hauled borax out of Death Valley in the 1880s — one of the most recognizable marketing images in American history. Borax has been used for cleaning, preserving, and soldering for over 4,000 years; it was traded along the Silk Road from Tibetan dry lakes to goldsmiths in Europe who used it as a flux.

In the DIY household, borax serves as a water softener, laundry booster, mild antiseptic, and all-purpose cleaning agent. It is particularly valuable in hard water areas, where it precipitates calcium and magnesium ions that would otherwise form soap scum and reduce cleaning effectiveness. While washing soda is a stronger cleaner, borax provides a more moderate alkalinity with additional boron-specific chemistry.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Boron Chemistry

Borax contains the tetraborate anion [B₄O₅(OH)₄]²⁻, which consists of:
- Two trigonal BO₃ units and two tetrahedral BO₄ units linked by shared oxygen atoms
- This complex anion is responsible for borax's unique properties

In solution, borax dissociates and hydrolyzes:
- Na₂B₄O₇·10H₂O → 2Na⁺ + B₄O₇²⁻ + 10H₂O
- B₄O₇²⁻ + 7H₂O → 4H₃BO₃ + 2OH⁻ (generates boric acid and hydroxide)

### Physical Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Melting point | 743°C (anhydrous) |
| Density | 1.73 g/cm³ (decahydrate) |
| Efflorescence | Slowly loses water in dry air |
| Taste | Slightly alkaline, sweet-saline |

### Relationship to Boric Acid

Borax and boric acid (H₃BO₃) are related but distinct:
- Borax (Na₂B₄O₇·10H₂O) — alkaline salt; used for cleaning, laundry
- Boric acid (H₃BO₃) — weak acid; used as antiseptic, insecticide, flame retardant
- Borax generates boric acid in solution, but at much lower and safer concentrations
- They are NOT interchangeable in formulations

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Mining

1. **Open-pit mining**: Borax ore (tincal, kernite, colemanite) is mined from evaporite deposits
2. **Dissolution**: Ore is dissolved in hot water
3. **Crystallization**: Solution is cooled; borax crystallizes out as the decahydrate
4. **Purification**: Recrystallization removes impurities
5. **Drying/packaging**: Crystals are dried and ground to desired particle size

### Major Sources

- **Turkey**: Eti Maden operates the world's largest boron mines (Bandirma, Emet, Bigadic); Turkey holds ~73% of global boron reserves
- **United States**: Rio Tinto operates the Boron mine in Kern County, California
- **Argentina and Chile**: Smaller deposits in Andean dry lakes

## Mechanism of Action

### Water Softening

1. Borax precipitates hard water minerals:
   - Ca²⁺ + Na₂B₄O₇ → Ca(B₄O₇) + 2Na⁺
2. Removing Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ from wash water prevents soap scum formation
3. Allows soap/detergent to work at full effectiveness

### pH Buffering

- Borax solutions buffer around pH 9.2
- This moderately alkaline pH aids cleaning by keeping grease partially saponified and soil dispersed
- Less aggressive than washing soda (pH 11) — safer for colors and delicates

### Mild Antiseptic

- Boric acid (generated from borax in solution) has weak antiseptic properties
- Inhibits growth of some bacteria and fungi
- Historically used in wound care (now replaced by better antiseptics)
- Contributes to freshness in laundry applications

### Stain Removal

- Alkaline pH helps denature protein stains (blood, sweat, food)
- Boron compounds help bleach some organic stains
- Enhances the cleaning action of soap and detergent

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Laundry Booster**:
- Add ½ cup to wash cycle along with regular detergent
- Softens water, boosts cleaning, freshens clothes
- Particularly effective in hard water areas

**DIY Laundry Detergent**:
- 1 bar grated soap + 1 cup washing soda + 1 cup borax
- 1–2 tbsp per load
- Borax softens water while washing soda handles grease

**All-Purpose Cleaner**:
- 2 tbsp borax dissolved in 2 cups warm water
- Spray on surfaces, wipe clean
- Suitable for countertops, sinks, tubs

**Toilet Bowl Cleaner**:
- Sprinkle ½ cup in toilet bowl before bed
- Let sit overnight
- Scrub in morning — removes stains and mineral deposits

**Ant and Roach Bait**:
- Mix borax with sugar water (1:1) or peanut butter
- Place in shallow dishes near pest trails
- Insects carry bait back to colony; boric acid disrupts their digestive system
- Keep away from children and pets

### Historical / Industrial Uses

- **Metallurgy**: Flux for soldering and welding (dissolves metal oxides)
- **Glass and ceramics**: Component of borosilicate glass (Pyrex), ceramic glazes, enamel
- **Preservation**: Historical food preservative (now banned in most countries for food)
- **Fire retardant**: Boron compounds reduce flammability of wood and textiles
- **Agriculture**: Boron is an essential plant micronutrient; borax used as soil amendment

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Toxicity

- Borax has **moderate toxicity** — it is not harmless
- Oral LD₅₀ (rat): 2.66 g/kg (comparable to table salt at 3.0 g/kg, but this comparison oversimplifies)
- **Keep away from children and pets** — ingestion of significant amounts can cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- Not for internal use

### EU Restrictions

- The EU has classified borax as a **Category 1B reproductive toxicant** (H360FD — may damage fertility and the unborn child)
- This classification is based on high-dose animal studies and is debated; the exposure levels in household cleaning are far below those causing reproductive effects
- Borax is restricted (not banned) in the EU for consumer products; still available for cleaning in the US

### Handling

- Wear gloves for prolonged contact (skin irritant in concentrated solutions)
- Avoid inhalation of dust
- Keep out of reach of children and pets
- Wash hands after handling

### Alternatives

- **Washing soda** is a stronger, equally available alternative for most cleaning applications
- Washing soda does not carry the reproductive toxicity classification
- For laundry, washing soda can replace borax at roughly equivalent amounts

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Excellent water softener
- Effective laundry booster
- Mild antiseptic properties
- Multi-purpose (cleaning, pest control, laundry)
- Widely available and inexpensive
- Buffered pH (gentler than washing soda on fabrics)
- Natural mineral (not synthetically produced)
- Long shelf life

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Moderate toxicity — must keep from children/pets
- EU reproductive toxicity classification (controversial but exists)
- Weaker cleaner than washing soda
- Not effective as a standalone disinfectant
- Dissolves slower in cold water
- Not suitable for personal care/skin applications
- Washing soda is a stronger alternative for most cleaning tasks

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: Indefinite when stored dry
- **Storage**: Cool, dry place in sealed container
- **Efflorescence**: May lose surface water and become powdery in very dry conditions — still effective
- **Container**: Original box or sealed bag/jar

- **FDA (USA)**: Borax is not GRAS for food use (banned as food additive in the US since 1960s).

- **EU**: Classified as reproductive toxicant Category 1B; restricted in consumer products.
- **US consumer**: Freely available for household cleaning and laundry use.

## Related Ingredients

- **Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate)** — Stronger alkaline cleaner (pH 11 vs 9.2). Better degreaser. Does not carry reproductive toxicity classification. Can replace borax in most applications.
- **Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate)** — Milder alkaline (pH 8.3). Not a water softener. Different use case.
- **Boric Acid** — Related compound (weak acid). Used as antiseptic and insecticide. NOT interchangeable with borax.
- **Citric Acid** — Acid counterpart for descaling. Use for mineral removal; use borax for water softening.
