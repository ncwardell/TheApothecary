# Rosemary
> A Mediterranean herb whose essential oil and extracts contain multiple bioactive compounds -- rosmarinic acid, ursolic acid, and carnosic acid -- that together provide anti-inflammatory, vasodilatory, anti-androgenic, and hair-follicle-stimulating effects, making it one of the most evidence-backed botanicals in DIY hair and skin care.

## Overview

Rosemary (*Rosmarinus officinalis*, now *Salvia rosmarinus*) is an aromatic evergreen shrub native to the Mediterranean basin, where it thrives on dry, rocky, calcareous soils in full sun. It has been cultivated for at least two thousand years for culinary, medicinal, and ceremonial purposes. The plant typically reaches 1-2 meters in height, bears needle-like leaves with a dark green upper surface and a woolly white underside, and produces small blue, purple, pink, or white flowers depending on the cultivar.

In the context of DIY natural products, rosemary is valued primarily for three independent pharmacological mechanisms delivered by three distinct chemical families. Rosmarinic acid is a potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant polyphenol. Ursolic acid, a pentacyclic triterpenoid, acts as a vasodilator and inhibitor of 5-alpha-reductase, the enzyme that converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the androgen implicated in androgenetic alopecia. Carnosic acid and its oxidized derivative carnosol stimulate hair follicle growth at the cellular level by activating the Wnt/beta-catenin signaling pathway. This triad of mechanisms was the basis for a landmark 2015 randomized controlled trial that demonstrated rosemary oil's equivalence to 2% minoxidil for hair regrowth.

Rosemary is available to the DIY formulator in several forms: whole dried herb for infusions and decoctions, CO2 or solvent-extracted oleoresin, hydrosol (the aqueous distillate), and steam-distilled essential oil. The essential oil is by far the most concentrated form and must always be diluted before topical application, typically to 1-5% in a carrier oil. Rosemary extract (specifically the carnosic acid fraction) is also widely used as a natural antioxidant to extend the shelf life of oils and fats in formulations.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Essential Oil Composition

Rosemary essential oil is a complex mixture of over 100 identified compounds, dominated by monoterpenes and oxygenated monoterpenes. The composition varies significantly by chemotype:

**1,8-Cineole chemotype (ct. cineole):** Dominant in Morocco and Tunisia. Contains 38-55% 1,8-cineole (eucalyptol), 8-20% camphor, 9-14% alpha-pinene. Best suited for respiratory applications.

**Camphor chemotype (ct. camphor):** Dominant in Spain. Contains 15-25% camphor, 18-26% 1,8-cineole, 5-10% alpha-pinene. Strongly stimulating, used for muscle pain and circulation.

**Verbenone chemotype (ct. verbenone):** Dominant in Corsica and Sardinia. Contains 15-37% verbenone, 10-15% alpha-pinene, lower camphor (<5%). Considered gentlest; preferred for skin and hair care.

### Non-Volatile Bioactive Compounds

**Rosmarinic acid** (C18H16O8, MW 360.31 g/mol): A caffeic acid ester of 3,4-dihydroxyphenyllactic acid. It is a water-soluble polyphenol found in the leaves at concentrations of 1.5-2.5% dry weight. It is a powerful antioxidant (ORAC value roughly 20x that of vitamin E on a molar basis) and inhibits complement activation and prostaglandin synthesis, accounting for its anti-inflammatory activity.

**Carnosic acid** (C20H28O4, MW 332.43 g/mol): A phenolic abietane diterpene, present at 1.5-10% of dried leaf weight. It is the primary contributor to rosemary's antioxidant activity in lipid systems. Carnosic acid is oxidized sequentially to carnosol, rosmanol, epirosmanol, and isorosmanol, each retaining antioxidant activity. In hair biology, carnosic acid activates the Wnt/beta-catenin pathway in dermal papilla cells, promoting the anagen (growth) phase of the hair cycle.

**Ursolic acid** (C30H48O3, MW 456.70 g/mol): A pentacyclic triterpenoid of the ursane type, found at 2-4% of dried leaf weight. It inhibits 5-alpha-reductase type I and type II, reducing local conversion of testosterone to DHT. It also acts as a vasodilator by stimulating endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS), improving blood flow to hair follicles and skin. Additionally, ursolic acid has anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer, and hepatoprotective properties.

**Carnosol** (C20H26O4, MW 330.42 g/mol): The oxidized lactone derivative of carnosic acid. It has independent anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting NF-kB and inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS).

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Growing Rosemary

Rosemary is a hardy perennial in USDA zones 7-10 (some cultivars tolerate zone 6). It requires:

- **Soil:** Well-drained, slightly alkaline (pH 6.0-7.5), sandy or rocky. Wet feet cause root rot.
- **Sun:** Full sun, minimum 6 hours daily.
- **Water:** Drought-tolerant once established; overwatering is the most common cause of death.
- **Propagation:** From semi-hardwood cuttings (most reliable), layering, or seed (slow and inconsistent germination, 15-25%).

**Harvesting:** Cut stems in the morning after dew has dried. For maximum essential oil content, harvest just before or during flowering. Leaves can be dried at low temperature (below 40 C / 104 F) to preserve volatile compounds.

### Making Rosemary Infused Oil (Cold Method)

1. Fill a clean, dry glass jar loosely with dried rosemary leaves (avoid fresh to prevent water contamination and mold).
2. Cover completely with a carrier oil (olive, jojoba, or sweet almond oil).
3. Seal and place in a warm, dark location for 4-6 weeks, shaking daily.
4. Strain through cheesecloth, squeezing out residual oil.
5. Store in amber glass. Shelf life approximately 6-12 months.

### Making Rosemary Infused Oil (Warm Method)

1. Place dried rosemary and carrier oil in a double boiler or slow cooker.
2. Heat at 40-50 C (104-122 F) for 4-8 hours. Do not exceed 60 C to avoid degrading polyphenols.
3. Strain and bottle.

### Making Rosemary Hydrosol (Home Steam Distillation)

1. Place 200-400 g of fresh rosemary in a large pot with a domed lid.
2. Add enough water to cover plant material.
3. Invert the lid and place ice on top.
4. Heat to a gentle simmer (not a rolling boil).
5. Steam rises, condenses on the cold lid, and drips into a collection bowl placed inside the pot.
6. Collect 200-500 mL of hydrosol.
7. Yield of essential oil is approximately 0.5-2% by weight (1-4 mL per 200 g of herb).
8. Refrigerate hydrosol; use within 6-12 months.

### Industrial Essential Oil Production

Commercial rosemary essential oil is produced by steam distillation of the aerial parts (leaves and flowering tops). Typical parameters: steam temperature 100-110 C, distillation time 1.5-3 hours, pressure slightly above atmospheric. Yield is 0.5-2.5% depending on cultivar, chemotype, harvest time, and geography. Supercritical CO2 extraction is used for higher-value rosemary extract (ROE, rosemary oleoresin extract) rich in carnosic acid, avoiding thermal degradation.

## Mechanism of Action

### Hair Growth (Three Independent Mechanisms)

**1. Anti-inflammatory via rosmarinic acid:** Rosmarinic acid inhibits cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2), 5-lipoxygenase (5-LOX), and complement C3-convertase. In the scalp, chronic microinflammation around hair follicles (perifollicular inflammation) contributes to miniaturization in androgenetic alopecia. By suppressing inflammatory mediators (prostaglandin E2, leukotriene B4), rosmarinic acid reduces perifollicular inflammation and creates a more favorable environment for follicle function.

**2. Vasodilation and 5-alpha-reductase inhibition via ursolic acid:** Ursolic acid stimulates eNOS, increasing local nitric oxide production and vasodilation. This improves microcirculation to the dermal papilla, delivering more oxygen and nutrients. Simultaneously, ursolic acid competitively inhibits 5-alpha-reductase types I and II, reducing local DHT concentration. This dual action is mechanistically similar to the combination of minoxidil (vasodilator) and finasteride (5-alpha-reductase inhibitor), but in a single natural compound.

**3. Hair follicle stimulation via carnosic acid:** Carnosic acid activates the Wnt/beta-catenin signaling pathway in dermal papilla cells. Wnt signaling is critical for hair follicle morphogenesis and cycling. Activation promotes the transition from telogen (resting phase) to anagen (growth phase), effectively "waking up" dormant follicles. This mechanism is independent of the hormonal and vascular pathways.

### Antioxidant Activity

Carnosic acid donates hydrogen atoms from its catechol (ortho-dihydroxy) ring to neutralize free radicals, forming carnosol. This cascade continues through rosmanol and other oxidation products, each retaining radical-scavenging capacity. This "cascade" antioxidant mechanism means that rosemary extract remains active even after initial oxidation, unlike most antioxidants that are consumed in a single reaction.

### Antimicrobial Activity

The essential oil's antimicrobial activity is attributed primarily to 1,8-cineole, camphor, and alpha-pinene. These monoterpenes disrupt bacterial cell membranes by intercalating into the lipid bilayer, increasing membrane permeability and causing leakage of intracellular contents. Activity is demonstrated against *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Escherichia coli*, *Bacillus subtilis*, *Candida albicans*, and *Propionibacterium acnes*.

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Scalp treatment oil (for hair growth):**
- 3-5% rosemary essential oil (verbenone chemotype preferred) in jojoba or castor oil
- Apply to scalp, massage for 5 minutes, leave on 30-60 minutes or overnight
- Use 2-3 times per week; expect visible results in 3-6 months

**Rosemary hair rinse:**
- Steep 2 tablespoons dried rosemary in 500 mL boiling water for 30 minutes
- Cool, strain, add 1 tablespoon apple cider vinegar
- Pour through hair after shampooing as a final rinse

**Antioxidant for oil preservation (ROE):**
- Add rosemary oleoresin extract (ROE) at 0.02-0.1% to fixed oils to retard oxidation
- Particularly effective in polyunsaturated oils (hemp seed, rosehip seed)

**Facial toner / hydrosol:**
- Use rosemary hydrosol as a direct facial toner or base for serum formulations
- Suitable for oily and combination skin types

**Muscle rub:**
- 5-10% rosemary essential oil (camphor chemotype) in a carrier oil or balm base
- Apply to sore muscles and joints

**Dandruff treatment:**
- 2-3% rosemary EO + 1% tea tree EO in carrier oil
- Apply to scalp before washing

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Food industry:** Rosemary extract (E392 in the EU) is approved as a natural antioxidant in fats, oils, and lipid-containing foods, replacing synthetic antioxidants BHA and BHT.
- **Cosmetics:** Used as a fragrance component, antioxidant preservative booster, and active ingredient in anti-aging, anti-acne, and hair care products.
- **Aromatherapy:** The 1,8-cineole chemotype is widely used for cognitive enhancement and alertness.
- **Agriculture:** Rosemary essential oil is used as a natural pesticide and insect repellent.

### Historical Uses

Rosemary has been associated with memory and remembrance since ancient Greece, where students wore rosemary garlands during examinations. In medieval Europe, it was burned as incense to ward off plague (its smoke is indeed mildly antimicrobial). It was a key ingredient in "Hungary Water" (14th century), one of the first alcohol-based perfumes, reputedly used by Queen Elizabeth of Hungary to restore her youth. In traditional European herbal medicine, rosemary was prescribed for headaches, poor circulation, digestive complaints, and "weakness of the brain." Sprigs of rosemary were placed under pillows to prevent nightmares and were strewn at funerals as a symbol of remembrance.

## Efficacy & Research

**Hair growth (rosemary oil vs. minoxidil):**
Panahi et al. (2015) - *SKINmed* (13(1):15-21): A randomized controlled trial comparing rosemary essential oil (applied topically) to 2% minoxidil in 100 patients with androgenetic alopecia over 6 months. Both groups showed significant increases in hair count at 6 months, with no statistically significant difference between groups. The rosemary group experienced less scalp itching than the minoxidil group. This is the most widely cited study supporting rosemary for hair growth.

**Carnosic acid and hair follicle stimulation:**
Murata et al. (2013) - *Phytomedicine* (20(12):1095-1100): Demonstrated that carnosic acid promotes hair growth in mice by activating the Wnt/beta-catenin pathway in dermal papilla cells and inducing anagen phase.

**Ursolic acid and 5-alpha-reductase:**
Kondo et al. (2009) - *Biological and Pharmaceutical Bulletin* (32(7):1224-1228): Showed that ursolic acid inhibits 5-alpha-reductase activity in vitro, with IC50 values comparable to some pharmaceutical inhibitors.

**Rosmarinic acid anti-inflammatory activity:**
Petersen & Simmonds (2003) - *Phytochemistry* (62(2):121-125): Comprehensive review of rosmarinic acid's biological activities, including inhibition of complement, COX-2, and 5-LOX.

**Antimicrobial activity:**
Bozin et al. (2007) - *Food Chemistry* (122(3):819-824): Demonstrated antimicrobial activity of rosemary essential oil against a range of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria and Candida species, with MIC values of 0.5-8 mg/mL.

**Cognitive enhancement:**
Moss et al. (2003) - *International Journal of Neuroscience* (113(1):15-38): Inhalation of rosemary essential oil significantly enhanced performance on cognitive tasks related to memory quality and secondary memory factors, with plasma 1,8-cineole levels correlating with cognitive performance.

**Antioxidant mechanism (carnosic acid cascade):**
Masuda et al. (2001) - *Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry* (49(11):5560-5565): Elucidated the sequential oxidation of carnosic acid to carnosol, rosmanol, and epirosmanol, each with retained antioxidant activity.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

- **Always dilute essential oil.** Undiluted rosemary essential oil causes skin irritation, burning, and contact dermatitis. Maximum recommended dermal concentration: 6.5% for the camphor/1,8-cineole chemotype (Tisserand & Young, 2014).
- **Epilepsy:** Camphor and 1,8-cineole are potentially neurotoxic in high doses and can lower the seizure threshold. Avoid rosemary essential oil (especially camphor chemotype) in individuals with epilepsy or seizure disorders.
- **Pregnancy:** Camphor is considered an abortifacient at high doses. Avoid essential oil during pregnancy, especially in the first trimester. Culinary use is considered safe.
- **Hypertension:** Some sources suggest rosemary may raise blood pressure, though evidence is mixed. Exercise caution in those with uncontrolled hypertension.
- **Children under 6:** Avoid camphor-rich chemotypes due to risk of respiratory distress and seizures. If used, select the verbenone chemotype at reduced concentrations (1-2%).
- **Allergic reactions:** Patch test before first use. Cross-reactivity may occur in individuals sensitive to other Lamiaceae family plants (mint, basil, sage, thyme).
- **Oral ingestion of essential oil:** Not recommended for DIY use. Can cause gastric irritation, kidney damage, and in extreme cases, seizures.
- **Pets:** Rosemary essential oil is toxic to cats (which lack glucuronyl transferase needed to metabolize monoterpenes). Use with caution around dogs.

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- One of very few botanicals with RCT evidence supporting efficacy for hair growth
- Three independent mechanisms of action for hair loss (anti-inflammatory, anti-androgenic/vasodilatory, follicle-stimulating)
- Dual-purpose: active ingredient and natural preservative (antioxidant)
- Multiple chemotypes available for different applications
- Widely available and affordable
- Long history of safe use in culinary and medicinal contexts
- Pleasant, familiar fragrance
- Compatible with most DIY formulation ingredients

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Essential oil must always be diluted; cannot be used neat
- Contraindicated in epilepsy, pregnancy, and young children (camphor chemotype)
- Hair growth results require 3-6 months of consistent use
- Chemotype variation means not all rosemary essential oils are equivalent for a given application
- Strong fragrance may be undesirable in some formulations
- ROE (rosemary oleoresin extract) is different from essential oil; they are not interchangeable
- Rosemary-infused oil has lower concentration of active compounds than essential oil or standardized extract

## Storage & Shelf Life

| Form | Storage Conditions | Shelf Life |
|---|---|---|
| Essential oil | Amber glass, cool (15-25 C), dark, tightly sealed | 2-3 years |
| Dried herb | Airtight container, dark, cool, dry | 1-2 years (flavor declines after 6 months) |
| Infused oil | Amber glass, cool, dark | 6-12 months |
| Hydrosol | Refrigerated, dark glass | 6-12 months (no preservative) |
| ROE (oleoresin extract) | Amber glass, cool, dark | 1-2 years |
| Fresh herb | Refrigerated, wrapped in damp paper towel | 1-2 weeks |

Essential oil quality degrades when exposed to heat, light, and oxygen. Oxidized rosemary oil has a harsh, camphorous off-note and increased potential for skin sensitization. Discard if the smell changes significantly.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Essential oil (conventional):** $8-15 per 15 mL (widely available from reputable suppliers such as Plant Therapy, NOW Foods, Aura Cacia)
- **Essential oil (organic, chemotype-specific):** $12-25 per 15 mL (Florame, Pranarom, Florihana)
- **Rosemary oleoresin extract (ROE):** $8-20 per 30-60 mL (from DIY cosmetic suppliers such as Lotioncrafter, Making Cosmetics)
- **Dried herb (organic):** $5-15 per 100 g (Mountain Rose Herbs, Starwest Botanicals)
- **Hydrosol:** $8-15 per 120 mL

When purchasing essential oil, seek suppliers that provide GC-MS (gas chromatography-mass spectrometry) test reports confirming chemotype and purity. Adulteration with synthetic 1,8-cineole, camphor, or alpha-pinene is common in low-cost rosemary oils.

## Regulatory Status

- **United States:** Rosemary essential oil and extracts are Generally Recognized As Safe (GRAS) for use in food (21 CFR 182.10, 182.20). No specific FDA regulation for cosmetic use; governed under general cosmetic safety regulations.
- **European Union:** Rosemary extract is approved as food additive E392. Essential oil is permitted in cosmetics with restrictions on camphor content (specific limits set by IFRA for fragrance applications).
- **IFRA (International Fragrance Association):** Restricts concentration of camphor and 1,8-cineole in leave-on products.
- **Cosmetics Ingredient (INCI) Names:** Rosmarinus Officinalis (Rosemary) Leaf Oil, Rosmarinus Officinalis (Rosemary) Leaf Extract.

- **Thyme essential oil:** Another Lamiaceae oil with hair growth evidence; often included in rosemary-based hair formulations.
