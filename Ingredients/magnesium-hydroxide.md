# Magnesium Hydroxide

> An inorganic compound that creates an alkaline skin surface inhospitable to odor-causing bacteria, offering a gentler alternative to baking soda in natural deodorant formulations, commercially known as Milk of Magnesia.

## Quick Reference

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Common Names | Magnesium hydroxide, Milk of Magnesia (suspension), brucite (mineral form) |
| Chemical Name / IUPAC | Magnesium hydroxide |
| Chemical Formula | Mg(OH)₂ |
| CAS Number | 1309-42-8 |
| Molecular Weight | 58.32 g/mol |
| Appearance | White powder or suspension (milky white liquid when dispersed in water) |
| pH (in solution) | ~10.5 (saturated solution); suspensions are buffered around pH 9–10 |
| Solubility | Poorly soluble in water (0.009 g/L at 25°C); soluble in dilute acids |

## Overview

Magnesium hydroxide is best known commercially as Milk of Magnesia — the iconic milky-white antacid and laxative that has been a medicine cabinet staple since Charles Henry Phillips patented it in 1872. However, in the DIY personal care world, magnesium hydroxide has gained significant attention as a highly effective, gentle deodorant active ingredient that avoids the skin irritation commonly caused by baking soda.

The compound works as a deodorant by creating a mildly alkaline environment on the skin surface. Odor-causing bacteria (primarily *Corynebacterium* and *Staphylococcus* species) thrive in the slightly acidic pH of sweat. By raising the local pH, magnesium hydroxide creates conditions inhospitable to these bacteria without blocking pores, irritating sensitive skin, or being absorbed into the body. It sits on the skin surface as a fine mineral layer.

The key advantage over baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is gentleness. Baking soda causes underarm irritation — ranging from redness to chemical burns — in an estimated 15–20% of users. Magnesium hydroxide provides comparable odor control without this common side effect, making it the preferred baking soda replacement in natural deodorant formulations for sensitive skin.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Crystal Structure

- Magnesium hydroxide crystallizes in a layered hexagonal (brucite) structure
- Each Mg²⁺ ion is surrounded by six OH⁻ ions in an octahedral arrangement
- The layers are held together by hydrogen bonding
- This layered structure gives it a soft, smooth feel on skin

### Chemical Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Melting point | 350°C (decomposes to MgO + H₂O) |
| Density | 2.34 g/cm³ |
| Refractive index | 1.559 |
| Basicity | Weak base (limited solubility limits OH⁻ release) |

### Why It's Gentler Than Baking Soda

| Property | Mg(OH)₂ | NaHCO₃ (Baking Soda) |
|---|---|---|
| pH of saturated solution | ~10.5 | ~8.3 |
| Solubility in water | Very low (0.009 g/L) | High (96 g/L) |
| Effective pH on skin | ~8–9 (buffered) | ~8–9 |
| Skin irritation rate | Very low | 15–20% |
| Mechanism of irritation | Minimal (stays on surface) | Dissolves readily, can abrade and penetrate disrupted skin |

The paradox: Mg(OH)₂ has a higher theoretical pH but causes less irritation because its very low solubility means it releases OH⁻ ions slowly and stays on the skin surface as an insoluble mineral layer rather than dissolving and penetrating.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

1. **Seawater method**: Mg²⁺ ions in seawater are precipitated by adding Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime):
   - MgCl₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → Mg(OH)₂↓ + CaCl₂
2. **Brine method**: Similar precipitation from underground magnesium brine deposits
3. **From magnesite**: Calcining MgCO₃ to MgO, then hydrating: MgO + H₂O → Mg(OH)₂
4. Product is filtered, washed, dried, and ground to desired particle size

### Pharmaceutical Grade (Milk of Magnesia)

- Mg(OH)₂ is suspended in purified water at ~7–8% concentration
- Stabilizers may be added to maintain suspension
- Marketed as Milk of Magnesia (Phillips', store brands)

### For DIY Deodorant Use

Most people purchase either:
- **Milk of Magnesia (unflavored)**: Ready-to-use suspension; can be applied directly or incorporated into formulations
- **Magnesium hydroxide powder**: Available from chemical suppliers; mix into deodorant formulations

## Mechanism of Action

### Deodorant Mechanism

1. **Surface alkalinity**: Mg(OH)₂ deposits as a thin mineral layer on skin, raising local pH to ~8–9
2. **Bacterial inhibition**: Odor-causing bacteria (*Corynebacterium*, *Staphylococcus hominis*) prefer pH 5–7; the alkaline environment suppresses their growth and metabolic activity
3. **Does not block sweat**: Unlike aluminum-based antiperspirants, Mg(OH)₂ does not plug sweat ducts. It allows perspiration while controlling odor.
4. **Stays on surface**: Very low water solubility means it remains as a surface layer rather than being absorbed — reduces systemic exposure concerns
5. **Buffering action**: Slowly neutralizes the acidic components of sweat

### Antacid Mechanism (Internal Use)

- Neutralizes stomach acid: Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O
- Osmotic laxative: Poorly absorbed Mg²⁺ draws water into the intestinal lumen

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Natural Deodorant (Primary Use)**:
- Replaces baking soda 1:1 in any deodorant recipe
- Paste: Mix Mg(OH)₂ powder with coconut oil + arrowroot
- Roll-on: 2 tbsp Mg(OH)₂ + 2 tbsp hot distilled water + 1 tsp witch hazel + 1 tsp arrowroot + essential oils. Whisk. Roller bottle.
- Direct application: Apply unflavored Milk of Magnesia to underarms with fingertips

**Sensitive Skin Deodorant Stick**:
- 1 tbsp Mg(OH)₂ (replacing baking soda)
- 3 tbsp coconut oil
- 2 tbsp arrowroot powder
- 1 tsp beeswax + 1 tbsp shea butter (for stick form)
- 5–10 drops essential oil

### Medical/Pharmaceutical Uses

- **Antacid**: Neutralizes excess stomach acid
- **Laxative**: Osmotic action draws water into bowel
- **Topical**: Canker sore treatment, minor skin irritation

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Topical Safety

- Excellent safety profile for skin application
- Non-toxic, non-irritating for the vast majority of users
- Extremely rare allergic reactions
- Safe for sensitive skin, post-shaving application

### Distinction from Other Magnesium Compounds

- **Magnesium hydroxide Mg(OH)₂**: Insoluble base — deodorant, antacid
- **Magnesium chloride MgCl₂**: Soluble salt — transdermal magnesium spray
- **Magnesium sulfate MgSO₄**: Soluble salt — Epsom salt baths
- These are **not interchangeable** — each has different properties and applications

### Internal Use

- As antacid/laxative: generally safe at recommended doses
- Excessive use can cause hypermagnesemia (elevated blood magnesium), especially in individuals with kidney impairment
- Can interact with certain medications (antibiotics, bisphosphonates)

### First Aid

- Non-hazardous at concentrations used in personal care
- Rinse eyes with water if powder contacts eyes

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Effective odor control comparable to baking soda
- Far gentler on sensitive skin (no rash/irritation)
- Does not block sweat pores
- Stays on skin surface — minimal absorption
- Available as ready-to-use Milk of Magnesia
- Inexpensive
- Non-toxic
- No aluminum compounds
- Easy 1:1 substitution for baking soda in recipes

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Does NOT stop sweating (odor control only)
- May leave white residue on dark clothing
- Powder form harder to source than baking soda
- Milk of Magnesia has limited shelf life once opened
- Less widely known — fewer recipe resources online
- Suspension (MoM) adds water to formulations, which can affect shelf life
- Not as effective for some heavy sweaters (may need reapplication)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Powder**: Indefinite shelf life when stored dry
- **Milk of Magnesia (suspension)**: Follow expiration date on bottle; typically 2–3 years unopened
- **Storage**: Room temperature, sealed container, away from moisture (powder) or heat (liquid)
- **DIY formulations**: Deodorant containing Mg(OH)₂ and water should be used within 1–3 months

- **USP/NF**: Monographed as pharmaceutical ingredient.

## Related Ingredients

- **Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate)** — The ingredient Mg(OH)₂ most often replaces in deodorant. Effective but irritating for ~15–20% of users.
- **Arrowroot Powder** — Moisture absorber commonly paired with Mg(OH)₂ in deodorant formulations.
- **Coconut Oil** — Carrier and antimicrobial commonly paired in deodorant recipes.
- **Magnesium Chloride** — Different compound used for transdermal magnesium supplementation (spray). Not interchangeable.
- **Zinc Oxide** — Another mineral deodorant active; some people respond better to zinc.
