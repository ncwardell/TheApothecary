# Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate)

> A strong alkaline salt (pH ~11) that saponifies grease on contact, softens hard water, and boosts surfactant performance, serving as the primary degreasing and heavy-duty cleaning agent in DIY household formulations.

## Overview

Sodium carbonate, commonly known as washing soda, is one of the oldest industrial chemicals known to humanity. Ancient Egyptians obtained it from natron deposits (natural sodium carbonate/bicarbonate mixtures) in dry lake beds and used it for mummification, glass making, and cleaning. The Leblanc process (1791) and later the Solvay process (1861) enabled large-scale industrial production, making washing soda a cornerstone of the chemical industry.

Washing soda is the alkaline counterpart to vinegar and citric acid in the DIY cleaning toolkit. While acids dissolve mineral deposits, washing soda's strong alkalinity (pH ~11) **saponifies grease** — literally turning fat into soap through base-catalyzed ester hydrolysis. This makes it exceptionally effective for cleaning ovens, range hoods, greasy pans, engine parts, and heavily soiled laundry.

Washing soda is distinct from baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO₃). Washing soda is roughly 10x more alkaline (pH ~11 vs pH ~8.3) and far more powerful as a cleaner. Baking soda can be converted to washing soda by heating in an oven at 400°F for 1 hour (thermal decomposition drives off water and CO₂).

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Chemistry

Sodium carbonate is the disodium salt of carbonic acid. In aqueous solution:

1. Na₂CO₃ → 2Na⁺ + CO₃²⁻
2. CO₃²⁻ + H₂O ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + OH⁻ (hydrolysis — generates hydroxide ions)
3. This hydrolysis produces the high pH (~11) that makes washing soda effective

### Saponification

The primary cleaning mechanism:
- Grease (triglycerides) + NaOH/Na₂CO₃ → Glycerol + Sodium soap (fatty acid salts)
- The strong base catalyzes ester hydrolysis of fats, converting them into water-soluble soap
- This is the same fundamental chemistry used in soap making, occurring spontaneously when washing soda contacts grease

### Relationship to Baking Soda

| Property | Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃) | Baking Soda (NaHCO₃) |
|---|---|---|

| Cleaning strength | Strong | Mild |
| Skin safety | Irritant — wear gloves | Generally safe |
| Water softening | Excellent | Minimal |

| Use | Heavy cleaning, laundry | Gentle cleaning, oral care, deodorizing |

### Conversion

2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ (at ~200°C / 400°F)

Spread baking soda on a baking sheet, heat at 400°F for 1 hour. The resulting powder is washing soda.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Solvay Process (Modern Industrial)

The dominant production method since the 1860s:

1. NaCl + NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NaHCO₃ + NH₄Cl
2. 2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ (heating)
3. Ammonia is recovered and recycled

### Natural Deposits

- Trona (Na₂CO₃·NaHCO₃·2H₂O) deposits in Wyoming contain vast reserves
- Mined, dissolved, and recrystallized to produce pure sodium carbonate
- Green River, Wyoming deposits supply most US washing soda

### Home Production

- Heat baking soda at 400°F (200°C) for 1 hour
- The powder changes texture: baking soda is fine and fluffy; washing soda is grainier and more powdery
- This is a reliable and practical home method

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Oven Cleaner**:
- Make paste with water, apply to oven interior (avoid heating elements)
- Leave overnight (8–12 hours)
- Wipe clean with damp cloth — grease wipes away effortlessly
- The alkali saponifies baked-on grease overnight

**Degreaser Spray**:
- 2 tbsp washing soda dissolved in 1 liter hot water
- Spray on range hoods, stovetops, greasy surfaces
- Let sit 5–15 minutes, wipe
- Add a squeeze of castile soap for extra surfactant action

**Laundry Detergent Base**:
- 1 cup washing soda + 1 cup borax + grated bar soap
- 1–2 tbsp per load
- Washing soda softens water, boosts surfactant action, saponifies grease

**Water Softener**:
- ½ cup in wash cycle softens hard water
- Ca²⁺ + Na₂CO₃ → CaCO₃↓ + 2Na⁺
- Precipitates calcium and magnesium, preventing soap scum formation

**Stain Pre-Treatment (Grease/Oil)**:
- Make paste of washing soda + water
- Apply to grease stains, let sit 30 minutes
- Wash as normal

**Drain Maintenance**:
- ½ cup washing soda down drain, followed by boiling water
- Saponifies grease buildup in pipes
- Monthly maintenance prevents clogs

### Industrial Uses

- **Glass manufacturing**: Primary flux in glass production
- **Paper manufacturing**: Pulping agent
- **Water treatment**: pH adjustment
- **Textile industry**: Desizing, scouring, and dyeing
- **Chemical manufacturing**: Feedstock for numerous sodium compounds

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Skin and Eye Protection

- **Wear gloves**: pH ~11 is alkaline enough to cause skin irritation and drying with prolonged contact
- Not corrosive at typical concentrations but will dry and crack skin with repeated bare-hand use
- Eye contact can cause significant irritation — rinse immediately with water for 15 minutes

### Fabric Safety

- Can fade colored fabrics — test on inconspicuous area first
- Can damage delicate fabrics (silk, wool) — the alkalinity denatures proteins
- Best for cottons, synthetics, and work clothes

### Incompatibilities

- **Do NOT mix with acids** (vinegar, citric acid): Neutralization wastes both ingredients
- Produces CO₂ fizzing when mixed with acids — not dangerous but pointless for cleaning
- Sequential use is fine: degrease with washing soda, rinse, then descale with vinegar

### First Aid

- **Skin contact**: Rinse with water; apply mild vinegar if irritation persists (neutralization)
- **Eye contact**: Flush with water for 15 minutes; seek medical attention if irritation continues
- **Ingestion**: Drink water; may cause GI irritation; seek medical advice for large amounts

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Excellent degreaser — saponifies grease on contact
- Powerful water softener
- Boosts cleaning power of soap/detergent
- Very inexpensive
- Can be made from baking soda at home
- Non-toxic (though irritant)
- Odorless
- Biodegradable
- Indefinite shelf life

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Requires gloves (skin irritant at pH ~11)
- Can fade colors and damage delicates
- Not for stone surfaces (alkaline can etch some finishes)
- Not a disinfectant
- Not suitable for aluminum (causes corrosion)
- Must not be mixed with acids (neutralization)
- Not gentle enough for personal care applications (use baking soda instead)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: Indefinite when stored dry
- **Storage**: Cool, dry place in sealed container
- **Hygroscopy**: The decahydrate (washing soda crystals) loses water in dry conditions, becoming powdery. The anhydrous form absorbs moisture in humid conditions, forming clumps. Neither change affects functionality.
- **Container**: Original box, or sealed plastic/glass container

- **Industrial**: Widely used and regulated in manufacturing.

## Related Ingredients

- **Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate)** — Milder cousin (pH ~8.3). For gentle cleaning, oral care, deodorizing. Converts to washing soda with heat.
- **Borax (Sodium Tetraborate)** — Complementary alkaline cleaner and water softener. Similar pH. Often used together in laundry.
- **White Vinegar** — Acid counterpart. Use for minerals; use washing soda for grease. Never mix directly.
- **Castile Soap** — Surfactant that pairs well with washing soda for enhanced cleaning.
- **Lye (Sodium Hydroxide)** — Much stronger alkali (pH 14). Used in soap making. Washing soda is the safer household alternative.
