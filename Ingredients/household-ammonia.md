# Household Ammonia
> A 5-10% aqueous solution of ammonia (NH3) used as a powerful alkaline cleaner that saponifies grease and denatures proteins, effective on organic stains but requiring careful handling due to toxic fumes and dangerous reactions with bleach.

## Overview

Household ammonia is a dilute aqueous solution of ammonia gas (NH3), typically containing 5-10% ammonia by weight. Ammonia itself is a colorless gas with a distinctively sharp, penetrating odor detectable at concentrations as low as 5 ppm. When dissolved in water, ammonia acts as a weak base, partially reacting with water to form ammonium hydroxide (NH4OH), though in practice the solution is better described as dissolved molecular ammonia (NH3) in equilibrium with ammonium (NH4+) and hydroxide (OH-) ions.

Ammonia has been known since antiquity. The name derives from the Temple of Amun (Ammon) in Libya, near which sal ammoniac (ammonium chloride, NH4Cl) was produced from camel dung. The isolation of pure ammonia gas is credited to Joseph Priestley in 1774, and its composition was determined by Claude Louis Berthollet in 1785. Before the development of synthetic detergents in the mid-20th century, ammonia solution was one of the most important household cleaning agents, valued for its ability to cut through grease, remove protein-based stains, and leave a streak-free finish on glass. It remains widely used today and is one of the most cost-effective cleaning agents available.

Household ammonia must be sharply distinguished from industrial anhydrous ammonia (used in agriculture as a nitrogen fertilizer and in refrigeration) and from concentrated ammonia solutions (25-30%) used in chemical laboratories and industrial processes. The dilute household product (5-10%) is significantly less hazardous than concentrated forms but still requires proper ventilation and should never be mixed with bleach or other oxidizing cleaners due to the production of toxic chloramine gases.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Molecular Structure of Ammonia

Ammonia (NH3) has a trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry with a bond angle of approximately 107.8 degrees (slightly less than the tetrahedral angle of 109.5 degrees due to the lone pair of electrons on nitrogen). Key structural features:

- **Nitrogen atom:** sp3 hybridized with one lone pair of electrons
- **N-H bond length:** 101.7 pm
- **Dipole moment:** 1.47 D (strongly polar)
- **Hydrogen bonding:** Both a donor (via N-H bonds) and acceptor (via the nitrogen lone pair)

### Aqueous Chemistry

In water, ammonia exists in a pH-dependent equilibrium:

NH3 + H2O <-> NH4+ + OH-

The base dissociation constant (Kb) is 1.8 x 10^-5 at 25 degrees C (pKb = 4.74), making ammonia a moderately weak base. The conjugate acid (ammonium ion, NH4+) has a pKa of 9.25.

At the pH range of household ammonia (11-12):
- Approximately 98-99% of the ammonia exists as molecular NH3 (the volatile, odorous form)
- Approximately 1-2% exists as NH4+ ions
- This explains the strong odor even in solution and the tendency for ammonia to escape into the air above the liquid

### Household Ammonia Formulations

Commercial household ammonia products typically contain:
- **Plain (clear) ammonia:** 5-10% NH3 in water, no additives
- **Sudsy ammonia:** 5-10% NH3 with added surfactants (detergents) for enhanced cleaning
- **Lemon ammonia:** 5-10% NH3 with lemon fragrance to partially mask the odor

The exact concentration is usually not specified on the label beyond "contains ammonia" or "ammonium hydroxide," but material safety data sheets (SDS) for specific products provide the concentration range. Most household products fall in the 5-10% range, while "janitorial strength" products may reach 10%.

### Key Chemical Reactions Relevant to Cleaning

**Saponification of fats and oils:**
Ammonia's hydroxide ions react with fats and oils (esters of glycerol and fatty acids) in a base-catalyzed hydrolysis (saponification):

Fat/Oil + OH- -> Glycerol + Fatty acid soap (carboxylate anion)

This converts insoluble greasy deposits into water-soluble soaps, which can then be rinsed away.

**Protein denaturation:**
Ammonia disrupts the hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, and hydrophobic interactions that maintain protein tertiary and quaternary structure. This denatures protein-based stains (blood, egg, sweat, milk), causing them to unfold and become soluble.

**Complexation with metal ions:**
Ammonia forms soluble complex ions (ammines) with certain metal ions, notably copper:

Cu2+ + 4NH3 -> [Cu(NH3)4]2+ (tetraamminecoper(II), deep blue)

This property makes ammonia useful for cleaning tarnished metals, though it can damage some metal finishes.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

Nearly all ammonia produced worldwide is made by the Haber-Bosch process, one of the most important industrial chemical processes ever developed:

N2 + 3H2 <-> 2NH3 (delta H = -92.4 kJ/mol)

**Conditions:**
- Temperature: 400-500 degrees C
- Pressure: 150-300 atm
- Catalyst: Iron with potassium oxide (K2O) and alumina (Al2O3) promoters
- Yield: 10-20% per pass (unreacted gases are recycled)

The hydrogen is typically obtained from natural gas (methane) via steam reforming:

CH4 + H2O -> CO + 3H2 (then CO + H2O -> CO2 + H2)

The nitrogen is obtained from air by fractional distillation or pressure swing adsorption.

Global ammonia production exceeds 175 million tonnes per year, with approximately 80% used for fertilizer production. Only a small fraction is diluted to produce household cleaning ammonia.

### Household Ammonia Production

Household ammonia is produced by dissolving anhydrous ammonia gas in purified water to the desired concentration (5-10% w/w) under controlled conditions. The process is exothermic and requires cooling. The solution is then packaged in HDPE (high-density polyethylene) bottles, which are compatible with ammonia solutions.

### Historical Production

Before the Haber-Bosch process (commercialized 1913), ammonia was obtained from:
- **Destructive distillation of organic matter:** Heating animal horns, bones, and hair produced "spirit of hartshorn" (aqueous ammonia). Heating coal produced coal gas containing ammonia, which was recovered as ammonium sulfate.
- **Fermentation of urine:** Urine, upon standing, undergoes bacterial decomposition of urea to produce ammonia: CO(NH2)2 + H2O -> 2NH3 + CO2. This was historically important in textile processing (fulling of wool) and tanning.
- **Sal ammoniac deposits:** Naturally occurring ammonium chloride near volcanic vents and in areas where animal dung was burned was treated with alkali (lime) to release ammonia.

### Home Concentration Adjustment

If you need to dilute concentrated ammonia or verify concentration:
- Household ammonia (approximately 5-10%) can be diluted with water for specific applications
- A common all-purpose cleaning dilution: 1/4 cup ammonia per gallon of water (approximately 0.5% solution)
- For glass cleaning: 1-2 tablespoons per quart of water
- Never attempt to concentrate household ammonia by heating (this releases ammonia gas)

## Mechanism of Action

### Grease and Oil Removal (Saponification)

Ammonia's cleaning power against grease and oils operates through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Base-catalyzed hydrolysis:** The hydroxide ions (OH-) in ammonia solution catalyze the hydrolysis of ester bonds in fats and oils (triglycerides), breaking them into glycerol and fatty acid anions (soaps). These fatty acid soaps are themselves surfactants, creating a self-reinforcing cleaning process.

2. **Emulsification:** Even before complete saponification occurs, the alkaline pH of ammonia solution helps emulsify oils by ionizing fatty acids at the oil-water interface, creating natural surfactants in situ.

3. **pH-driven solubility:** Many organic soils have increased solubility at high pH. The pH 11-12 environment created by ammonia dissolves and disperses organic deposits that are insoluble at neutral pH.

### Protein Stain Removal

Ammonia is particularly effective against protein-based stains (blood, sweat, egg, milk, grass) because:

1. **Denaturation:** The high pH disrupts the non-covalent bonds (hydrogen bonds, salt bridges, hydrophobic interactions) that maintain protein structure, causing the protein to unfold.

2. **Peptide bond hydrolysis:** While slow at room temperature, the alkaline conditions can partially hydrolyze peptide bonds, breaking large insoluble protein aggregates into smaller, more soluble fragments.

3. **Charge modification:** At pH 11-12, most amino acid side chains are deprotonated, giving the protein a net negative charge. This increases solubility through electrostatic repulsion and hydration.

4. **Blood stain specificity:** Blood stains are particularly responsive to ammonia because the iron in hemoglobin forms soluble complexes with ammonia, and the globin protein component is readily denatured at high pH. Hot water should be avoided for blood stains as it denatures proteins on contact, setting the stain; ammonia works best at cool to room temperature for blood.

### Glass Cleaning (Streak-Free Finish)

Ammonia produces a streak-free finish on glass because:
1. It evaporates completely, leaving no solid residue (unlike soap-based cleaners)
2. It dissolves the oily fingerprint residue (sebum) through saponification
3. The volatile nature of NH3 promotes rapid drying
4. It does not leave surfactant films that scatter light

### Antimicrobial Action

While not primarily used as a disinfectant, ammonia has antimicrobial properties:
- The high pH (11-12) is lethal to most vegetative bacteria
- NH3 penetrates cell membranes and disrupts intracellular pH
- Ammonia disrupts bacterial cell membrane lipids
- Not effective against bacterial spores or mycobacteria
- Not EPA-registered as a disinfectant at household concentrations

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**All-Purpose Cleaner**
- 1/4 cup household ammonia
- 1/2 cup rubbing alcohol (isopropyl alcohol)
- 1 teaspoon dish soap (not bleach-containing)
- Water to fill a quart spray bottle
- Shake gently to combine
- Effective on kitchen counters, appliances, and general surfaces (not marble, granite, or other natural stone)

**Glass and Mirror Cleaner**
- 2 tablespoons household ammonia
- 2 tablespoons rubbing alcohol
- Water to fill a quart spray bottle
- Spray and wipe with newspaper or lint-free cloth for streak-free results

**Heavy-Duty Degreaser (Oven, Range Hood)**
- 1/2 cup ammonia in a shallow dish
- Place inside cold oven overnight (door closed)
- The ammonia fumes soften baked-on grease
- Wipe down with warm soapy water the next day
- Ventilate thoroughly before and after opening

**Protein Stain Pre-Treatment (Blood, Sweat, Grass)**
- Dab (do not rub) the stain with a solution of 1 tablespoon ammonia in 1/2 cup cold water
- Allow to sit for 15-30 minutes
- Blot with clean cloth
- Launder as usual
- Always use cold water for blood stains (hot water sets protein stains)

**Sweat Stain / Yellowing Treatment for White Shirts**
- Mix equal parts ammonia, water, and white vinegar (not simultaneously -- apply ammonia solution first, rinse, then vinegar)
- Or: soak the yellowed area in a solution of 1/4 cup ammonia per quart of warm water for 30 minutes before laundering
- The ammonia breaks down the protein and oil components of sweat stains

**Brass and Silver Polish (Tarnish Removal)**
- Mix 1 tablespoon ammonia in 1 cup warm water
- Soak tarnished items for 10 minutes
- Scrub gently with a soft brush
- Rinse thoroughly and dry
- Note: Do not use on lacquered, plated, or costume jewelry

**Floor Cleaner (Vinyl, Linoleum, Tile)**
- 1/2 cup ammonia per gallon of warm water
- Mop as usual; no rinsing required
- Do not use on hardwood floors (moisture damage) or waxed floors (strips wax)

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Commercial cleaning:** Janitorial-strength ammonia solutions are used in commercial kitchens, hospitals, and industrial facilities for degreasing and general cleaning.
- **Refrigeration:** Anhydrous ammonia is used as a refrigerant (R-717) in large industrial refrigeration systems due to its excellent thermodynamic properties and zero ozone depletion potential.
- **Fertilizer production:** The vast majority of ammonia (approximately 80%) is converted to urea, ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, and other nitrogen fertilizers.
- **Chemical feedstock:** Ammonia is a precursor to nitric acid (Ostwald process), hydrazine, amines, and numerous other chemical products.
- **Metal treatment:** Ammonia is used in nitriding (surface hardening of steel), carbonitriding, and as a reducing agent in metallurgy.
- **Textile industry:** Used in mercerization of cotton and as a pH adjuster in dyeing processes.
- **Water treatment:** Chloramine disinfection (intentional, controlled combination of chlorine and ammonia) is used in municipal water treatment for residual disinfection.

### Historical Uses

- **Fulling of wool:** Ancient Romans collected urine (which decomposes to ammonia) in public vessels (amphorae) and used it to full (clean and thicken) woolen cloth. Emperor Vespasian famously taxed this trade.
- **Smelling salts:** Ammonium carbonate ("sal volatile"), which releases ammonia, was used from at least the 17th century to revive fainting individuals. The sharp odor triggers an inhalation reflex and increases heart rate.
- **Pre-commercial cleaning:** Before the advent of synthetic detergents (1950s), ammonia solution was the primary degreasing agent in American and European households.
- **Photography:** Ammonium thiosulfate is used as a rapid fixer in photographic processing.
- **Leavening:** Ammonium bicarbonate (baker's ammonia, hartshorn) was used as a leavening agent before baking powder, and is still used in some traditional Scandinavian and German cookies.

## Efficacy & Research

**Cleaning Efficacy:**
The alkaline cleaning properties of ammonia are well-established in cleaning science literature. Johansson, I. and Somasundaran, P. (2007) in *Handbook for Cleaning/Decontamination of Surfaces* (Elsevier) document that alkaline cleaners at pH 10-13 effectively remove fats and proteins through saponification and denaturation, with ammonia being a preferred alkaline agent due to its volatility (leaves no residue).

**Protein Stain Removal:**
Olson, L.A. (1970) published in *Textile Research Journal* (40: 975-980) demonstrating that alkaline treatments (including ammonia) were more effective than neutral or acidic treatments for removing blood and protein-based stains from textiles, with the mechanism involving protein denaturation and solubilization.

**Antimicrobial Properties:**
McDonnell, G. and Russell, A.D. (1999) published in *Clinical Microbiology Reviews* (12(1): 147-179) noting that ammonia has moderate antimicrobial activity at household concentrations, effective against vegetative bacteria and some enveloped viruses, but not suitable as a primary disinfectant for healthcare applications due to limited sporicidal and mycobactericidal activity.

**Chloramine Gas Formation:**
The reaction between ammonia and sodium hypochlorite (bleach) to form chloramines is well-characterized in the chemistry literature. Jafvert, C.T. and Valentine, R.L. (1992) published in *Environmental Science & Technology* (26(3): 577-586) detailing the kinetics and products of chloramine formation, which include monochloramine (NH2Cl), dichloramine (NHCl2), and trichloramine (NCl3), all of which are toxic respiratory irritants.

**Indoor Air Quality:**
The U.S. Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) reports that ammonia is detectable by smell at 5-50 ppm and causes eye and respiratory irritation at 50-100 ppm. The OSHA PEL is 50 ppm (8-hour TWA), and the NIOSH IDLH (immediately dangerous to life or health) is 300 ppm. Household use at recommended dilutions in ventilated areas typically produces ambient concentrations well below 50 ppm.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### CRITICAL: Never Mix Ammonia with Bleach

**This is the single most important safety rule for ammonia use.**

Mixing ammonia with sodium hypochlorite (bleach) produces chloramine gases:

NH3 + NaOCl -> NaCl + NH2Cl (monochloramine)
2NH3 + 2NaOCl -> 2NaCl + N2H4 (hydrazine, at certain ratios)
NH3 + 3NaOCl -> 3NaCl + NCl3 (trichloramine/nitrogen trichloride)

These products are acutely toxic:
- **Monochloramine (NH2Cl):** Respiratory irritant, causes coughing, chest pain
- **Dichloramine (NHCl2):** More toxic than monochloramine, causes pulmonary edema
- **Trichloramine (NCl3):** Explosive, severe respiratory toxin
- **Hydrazine (N2H4):** Carcinogen, hepatotoxin

Symptoms of chloramine gas exposure: coughing, shortness of breath, chest pain, wheezing, nausea, watery eyes, throat irritation. Severe exposure can cause pneumonitis, pulmonary edema, and death.

**Also do not mix ammonia with:**
- Products containing bleach (many bathroom and toilet cleaners)
- Hydrogen peroxide (at high concentrations, can form unstable compounds)
- Acids (e.g., vinegar, muriatic acid) -- neutralization produces heat and may splash; also wastes the cleaning power of both

### Ventilation
- Always use ammonia in well-ventilated areas
- Open windows and use fans when cleaning with ammonia
- Never use in enclosed, unventilated spaces (bathrooms with no ventilation, closets)
- If you can smell ammonia strongly, ventilation is inadequate

### Skin and Eye Protection
- Wear rubber or nitrile gloves when using ammonia solutions, especially undiluted
- Ammonia can cause chemical burns to eyes; wear safety glasses for overhead cleaning
- If skin contact occurs, flush with copious water for 15-20 minutes
- If eye contact occurs, flush immediately with water for at least 15-20 minutes and seek medical attention

### Ingestion
- If swallowed, do not induce vomiting (risk of re-exposure to caustic material)
- Drink water or milk to dilute
- Contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222 in the US) immediately

### Inhalation
- If exposed to strong ammonia fumes, move to fresh air immediately
- If breathing is difficult, seek emergency medical attention
- Chronic low-level exposure can cause bronchitis and respiratory sensitization

### Surface Compatibility
Ammonia should NOT be used on:
- **Natural stone** (marble, granite, travertine): dissolves calcium carbonate, etches surface
- **Hardwood floors:** moisture damage
- **Aluminum:** causes pitting and discoloration
- **Copper and brass (prolonged exposure):** excessive corrosion
- **Waxed or oiled surfaces:** strips protective coatings
- **Rubber gaskets and seals:** can degrade over time

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages
- Extremely effective grease cutter through saponification
- Excellent for protein-based stains (blood, sweat, egg, milk)
- Leaves streak-free finish on glass (evaporates completely)
- Very inexpensive ($2-5 per quart)
- No solid residue left after evaporation
- Effective over a wide range of dilutions
- Long track record of safe use when properly handled
- Versatile: floors, windows, laundry, ovens, metal cleaning
- Biodegradable: ammonia is a naturally occurring compound, rapidly metabolized in the environment

### Disadvantages / Limitations
- Strong, unpleasant, potentially harmful fumes
- Requires adequate ventilation for safe use
- Extremely dangerous if mixed with bleach (chloramine gas)
- Can irritate skin, eyes, and respiratory tract
- Damages natural stone (marble, granite), aluminum, and some other surfaces
- Not effective as a disinfectant at household concentrations
- Unpleasant odor persists until fully evaporated
- Not suitable for individuals with asthma or respiratory sensitivity
- Can damage or discolor certain fabrics (silk, wool at high concentrations)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life:** 6-12 months after opening; up to 2 years unopened. Ammonia slowly escapes from solution over time (volatilization), gradually reducing concentration.
- **Storage conditions:** Store in a cool, well-ventilated area away from heat sources and direct sunlight. Keep tightly capped when not in use.
- **Container:** Store only in original HDPE container or compatible plastic. Do not transfer to glass (ammonia solutions can develop pressure from outgassing, and glass may not seal properly). Never use metal containers (corrosion).
- **Temperature:** Keep below 40 degrees C. Higher temperatures increase ammonia vapor pressure and evaporation rate.
- **Separation:** Store away from bleach, acids, and oxidizing agents. Store separately from food and beverages.
- **Signs of degradation:** Reduced or absent ammonia odor when opened indicates significant loss of concentration. The product is still safe but may be less effective.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Typical price:** $2-5 USD per quart (946 mL); $5-10 per gallon (3.78 L). One of the least expensive cleaning agents available.
- **Sources:** Grocery stores, hardware stores, dollar stores, wholesale clubs. Available virtually everywhere.
- **Types available:**
  - **Clear/Plain ammonia:** Pure ammonia solution, no additives. Preferred for DIY formulations and laundry.
  - **Sudsy ammonia:** Contains added surfactants. Convenient for floor cleaning but not ideal for glass.
  - **Lemon ammonia:** Contains lemon fragrance. Masks odor but adds fragrance chemicals.
- **Key brands:** Austin's, James Austin, Parsons, store brands.
- **For DIY formulations:** Always use clear/plain ammonia without additives to avoid unexpected interactions with other ingredients.

## Regulatory Status

- **EPA:** Not regulated as a pesticide. Listed as an inert ingredient in some pesticide formulations. Ammonia releases above 100 pounds (45 kg) must be reported under CERCLA/EPCRA.
- **OSHA:** Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL): 50 ppm (8-hour TWA). Short-Term Exposure Limit (STEL): 35 ppm.
- **FDA:** Ammonium hydroxide is Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) as a food processing aid (pH adjuster, leavening agent, surface-finishing agent) under 21 CFR 184.1139.
- **DOT:** Ammonia solutions greater than 10% but not greater than 35% are classified as Class 8 (Corrosive) for transportation (UN 2672).
- **GHS Classification (household ammonia, 5-10%):** Skin corrosion Category 1B; Serious eye damage Category 1; Specific target organ toxicity (single exposure) Category 3 (respiratory).
- **Consumer Product Safety Commission:** Requires child-resistant packaging and cautionary labeling.

## Related Ingredients

- **[Hydrogen Peroxide](hydrogen-peroxide.md):** Complementary cleaning agent (oxidizer vs. alkali). Can be used sequentially but not mixed directly with ammonia.
- **White Vinegar:** Acidic cleaner that should not be mixed with ammonia (neutralization wastes both). Used for different types of deposits (mineral/lime scale vs. organic/grease).
- **Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate):** Another alkaline cleaner with similar (but milder) grease-cutting ability and no fumes. pH approximately 11.
- **Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate):** Mild alkali (pH approximately 8.3) with gentle abrasive properties. Much milder than ammonia.
- **Castile Soap:** Surfactant-based cleaner. Can be combined with ammonia in dilute solutions for enhanced cleaning.
