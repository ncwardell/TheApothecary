# Castile Soap

> A true soap made from the saponification of plant oils (traditionally olive oil), producing a biodegradable surfactant with both hydrophilic and lipophilic properties, capable of replacing body wash, hand soap, face wash, shaving cream, and household cleaners.

## Quick Reference

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Common Names | Castile soap, vegetable soap, olive oil soap, liquid castile soap |
| Chemical Name / IUPAC | Mixture of sodium or potassium salts of fatty acids (primarily potassium oleate, potassium laurate, potassium palmitate) |
| Chemical Formula | Mixture; e.g., potassium oleate: C₁₇H₃₃COOK |
| CAS Number | Various (mixture of fatty acid salts) |
| Molecular Weight | Variable (~300–320 g/mol for primary components) |
| Appearance | Liquid: amber to brown viscous liquid. Bar: white to cream solid. Mild, characteristic soap scent. |
| pH (in solution) | ~9–10 (alkaline) |
| Solubility | Fully soluble in water; forms micelles above critical micelle concentration |

## Overview

Castile soap takes its name from the Castile region of central Spain, where soap made from local olive oil was produced as early as the 12th century. Unlike most commercial soaps of the era (which used animal tallow), Castile soap was made exclusively from plant oils, giving it a milder, gentler character that quickly earned a reputation as a luxury product throughout Europe.

Today, "castile soap" has evolved beyond its strict olive-oil-only origins. Modern castile soaps — most notably the widely available Dr. Bronner's brand — typically use a blend of plant oils including coconut, olive, hemp, and jojoba, saponified with potassium hydroxide (for liquid soap) or sodium hydroxide (for bar soap). The defining characteristic remains the same: it is a **true soap** made entirely from plant-derived oils, with no synthetic detergents (sulfates, SLS/SLES), no petroleum derivatives, no artificial preservatives, and no animal fats.

The chemistry of soap is elegant in its simplicity. Soap molecules are amphiphilic — one end is hydrophilic (water-loving, the carboxylate head) and the other is lipophilic (oil-loving, the long hydrocarbon tail). This dual nature allows soap to bridge the gap between oil and water, surrounding oil droplets in micelles that can be rinsed away. This fundamental surfactant chemistry has not been improved upon in thousands of years of use — modern detergents merely offer alternatives, not improvements, for most household applications.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Saponification Chemistry

Soap is produced by the reaction of fats/oils (triglycerides) with a strong base (alkali):

**Triglyceride + Base → Glycerol + Soap (fatty acid salt)**

For liquid castile soap:
- Oil + KOH (potassium hydroxide) → Potassium fatty acid salts + Glycerol

For bar castile soap:
- Oil + NaOH (sodium hydroxide) → Sodium fatty acid salts + Glycerol

### Fatty Acid Composition (typical liquid castile blend)

| Oil Source | Primary Fatty Acids | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Coconut oil | Lauric acid (C12), myristic acid (C14) | Lather, cleansing power |
| Olive oil | Oleic acid (C18:1) | Mildness, conditioning |
| Hemp oil | Linoleic acid (C18:2), alpha-linolenic acid (C18:3) | Skin conditioning |
| Jojoba oil | Wax esters (C20:1) | Skin compatibility |
| Palm kernel oil | Lauric acid (C12) | Lather (used by some brands) |

### Surfactant Properties

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Critical micelle concentration (CMC) | ~1–5 mM (varies by fatty acid) |
| Hydrophilic-lipophilic balance (HLB) | ~18–20 (strongly hydrophilic) |
| Micelle size | 3–5 nm diameter |
| Biodegradability | >98% within 28 days |

### Why pH ~9 Matters

Soap is inherently alkaline because fatty acid salts hydrolyze in water:
- RCOO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ RCOOH + OH⁻
- This maintains pH ~9–10, which is necessary for soap to remain dissolved and active
- Acidifying soap (mixing with vinegar/citric acid) converts it back to insoluble free fatty acids — this is why you should never mix castile soap with acids

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Cold-Process Bar Soap (Home Method)

1. **Calculate lye**: Use an online soap calculator (e.g., SoapCalc) to determine exact NaOH amount for your oil blend
2. **Prepare lye solution**: Carefully dissolve NaOH in water (exothermic — heats up significantly). Always add lye to water, never water to lye.
3. **Heat oils**: Melt solid oils (coconut oil) and combine with liquid oils (olive oil) at ~100–110°F
4. **Combine**: Slowly pour lye solution into oils
5. **Blend**: Use immersion blender until mixture reaches "trace" (thickens like thin pudding)
6. **Pour into mold**: Add essential oils at trace if desired
7. **Cure**: Unmold after 24–48 hours; cure 4–6 weeks (saponification completes, water evaporates, pH drops)

### Liquid Castile Soap (Home Method)

1. Use KOH (potassium hydroxide) instead of NaOH
2. Similar process but produces a paste that is then diluted with water
3. More complex; most people prefer to purchase liquid castile soap

### Commercial Production

- Large-scale industrial saponification in heated reaction vessels
- Precise oil-to-lye ratios for consistent product
- Liquid castile soap is cooked, diluted, and bottled
- Dr. Bronner's uses a proprietary process with organic, fair-trade oils

## Mechanism of Action

### Surfactant / Micelle Formation

1. Soap molecules arrange themselves at the oil-water interface, with hydrophobic tails in the oil and hydrophilic heads in the water
2. Above the critical micelle concentration (CMC), soap molecules spontaneously form **micelles** — spherical structures with oil-loving tails pointing inward and water-loving heads pointing outward
3. Grease, dirt, and bacteria are encapsulated within the micelle core
4. The water-soluble exterior allows micelles (containing trapped contaminants) to be rinsed away
5. Mechanical action (scrubbing, swishing) helps dislodge contaminants for micelle encapsulation

### Skin Cleansing

- Soap removes excess sebum, sweat, environmental contaminants, and bacteria from the skin surface
- True soap is more alkaline than skin's natural pH (~5.5), which temporarily disrupts the acid mantle
- This is why moisturizing after soap use is recommended
- Castile soap with olive oil and glycerol (a natural byproduct of saponification) is milder than most commercial soaps

### Emulsification

- In cleaning applications, soap emulsifies oils and greases, converting them from insoluble to water-dispersible
- Combined with washing soda, provides both chemical (saponification) and physical (micelle) grease removal

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Body Wash**:
- Dilute 1:2 with water
- Use in a squeeze bottle or pump dispenser
- The glycerol content provides natural moisture retention

**Hand Soap**:
- Dilute 1:4 in a foaming dispenser
- Add a few drops of essential oil if desired

**Face Wash**:
- Dilute 1:4 or use bar soap directly
- Follow with ACV rinse or toner to restore skin's acid mantle
- Mild enough for most skin types

**Shaving Cream**:
- Use undiluted as a shaving lather
- Apply to wet skin, lather with hands or brush
- Excellent lubrication from oleic acid content

**Shampoo (Alternative)**:
- 1 tbsp in ½ cup water
- Alkaline — ACV rinse is essential afterward to close cuticle
- Better alternatives exist (soapwort root) but castile soap works

**All-Purpose Cleaner**:
- Squeeze of castile soap in spray bottle with water
- Not for glass (leaves streaks)
- Good for countertops, sinks, tubs, floors

**Dish Soap**:
- Few drops on sponge for hand dishwashing
- Not suitable for dishwashers (too much foam)

### Commercial Uses

- **Personal care**: Body wash, shampoo, hand soap
- **Household cleaning**: Multi-surface cleaners
- **Pet care**: Pet shampoo
- **Gardening**: Insecticidal soap spray (diluted)
- **Camping**: Biodegradable wilderness soap

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Critical Incompatibilities

- **Do NOT mix with vinegar or citric acid**: Acid converts soap back to insoluble free fatty acids, creating a sticky, curdled precipitate. Use sequentially with thorough rinsing between.
- **Do NOT mix with hydrogen peroxide**: No useful interaction; may destabilize peroxide
- **Hard water challenge**: Calcium and magnesium in hard water react with soap to form insoluble soap scum: 2RCOO⁻ + Ca²⁺ → (RCOO)₂Ca↓. Solutions: add washing soda (softens water) or use soapwort (saponins don't form scum).

### Skin Sensitivity

- pH ~9–10 can be drying for sensitive or eczema-prone skin
- Always dilute for skin use
- Follow with moisturizer or pH-balancing toner
- Bar castile soap is typically more mild than liquid (lower concentration)

### Environmental

- Biodegradable (>98% within 28 days)
- Safe for greywater systems
- Still contains surfactants — do not use directly in waterways (even biodegradable soap harms aquatic life at concentration)

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- True soap — no synthetic detergents or chemicals
- Extraordinarily versatile — replaces 4–6 commercial products
- Biodegradable and environmentally responsible
- Effective surfactant for cleaning, personal care, and laundry
- Concentrated — dilutes extensively, providing good value
- Glycerol byproduct adds natural moisturizing
- Long tradition of safe use
- Available in multiple scents or unscented

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Alkaline pH (~9) disrupts skin's acid mantle
- Cannot mix with acids (vinegar, citric acid) — precipitates
- Forms scum in hard water
- No lather comparable to SLS-based products (cosmetic, not functional limitation)
- Relatively expensive per bottle ($12–16/32 oz) but high dilution offsets cost
- Not a disinfectant
- Streaks on glass

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: 3+ years (liquid); bar soap improves with age (loses water, hardens)
- **Storage**: Room temperature, away from extreme heat
- **Container**: Original bottle is fine; glass for decanting
- **Notes**: Natural separation or cloudiness may occur in cold temperatures — normal; shake before use

- **FDA (USA)**: Regulated as a cosmetic (personal care) and household product. True soap is exempt from certain FDA cosmetic regulations when sold solely for cleansing.
- **EU**: Compliant with EU Cosmetics Regulation. INCI: Potassium Olivate, Potassium Cocoate, etc.
- **Biodegradability**: Meets OECD 301B biodegradation standards.

## Related Ingredients

- **Soapwort Root** — Natural saponin surfactant; pH ~5 (milder than soap). No hard water scum. Better for hair.
- **Washing Soda** — Alkaline partner for heavy cleaning. Softens water, preventing soap scum.
- **White Vinegar** — Do not mix directly. Use after soap for descaling or as hair rinse to restore pH.
- **Coconut Oil** — Primary oil in many castile soap formulations; contributes lauric acid for lather and cleansing.
- **Baking Soda** — Gentle alkaline cleaner; complementary to soap for scrubbing applications.
