# Activated Charcoal

> A highly porous form of carbon processed at extreme temperatures to create an enormous internal surface area (500–1,500 m²/g) that adsorbs toxins, impurities, and odors through van der Waals forces — the physical adsorption mechanism that distinguishes it from bentonite clay's ionic exchange.

## Overview

Activated charcoal is ordinary carbon (from coconut shells, wood, peat, or coal) that has been processed at 600–1,200°C in the presence of activating gases (steam, CO₂, or chemical agents) to create a labyrinth of microscopic pores. The result is a material with an astonishing surface area — a single gram of activated charcoal can have over 1,000 m² of internal surface, roughly the area of four tennis courts. This vast surface adsorbs (binds to the surface of) organic molecules, toxins, pigments, and odors through van der Waals forces (weak intermolecular attractions).

The distinction from bentonite clay is critical for a rigorous DIY system. Bentonite works primarily through **ionic exchange** — its negatively charged layers attract and bind positively charged ions (heavy metals, some organic cations). Activated charcoal works through **physical adsorption** — its porous carbon surface binds molecules regardless of charge, through non-specific van der Waals interactions. This makes activated charcoal effective against a much broader range of organic compounds, including those that bentonite cannot adsorb.

In medicine, activated charcoal is the standard emergency treatment for oral poisoning — it adsorbs most organic toxins in the GI tract before they can be absorbed into the bloodstream. In water treatment, granular activated carbon (GAC) filters are the gold standard for removing chlorine, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), pesticides, and taste/odor compounds.

For DIY use, activated charcoal fills roles in water filtration, teeth whitening (cosmetic stain removal), facial masks (pore-deep cleansing), poultice applications, and deodorization.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Pore Structure

Activated charcoal has three categories of pores:

| Pore Type | Diameter | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Micropores | <2 nm | Adsorb small molecules (gases, solvents, chlorine) |
| Mesopores | 2–50 nm | Adsorb medium molecules (dyes, many toxins) |
| Macropores | >50 nm | Transport channels; allow access to interior |

The activation process determines the ratio of these pore types. Coconut shell charcoal tends to have more micropores (better for gas/water filtration); wood-based charcoal has more macropores (better for larger molecules).

### Surface Area Comparison

| Material | Surface Area (m²/g) |
|---|---|
| Activated charcoal (coconut) | 800–1,500 |
| Activated charcoal (wood) | 500–1,000 |
| Bentonite clay | 50–100 |
| Diatomaceous earth | 10–30 |
| Sand (filter) | <1 |

### Adsorption vs. Absorption

- **Adsorption** (activated charcoal): molecules bind to the SURFACE of the material via van der Waals forces. A surface phenomenon.
- **Absorption** (sponge): molecules penetrate INTO the bulk of the material. A volume phenomenon.
- Activated charcoal adsorbs; it does not absorb.

For DIY personal care, **coconut shell activated charcoal, food grade** is recommended.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

**Physical (thermal) activation**:
1. Carbonization: raw material (coconut shells, wood) heated to 400–600°C in absence of oxygen
2. Activation: carbonized material exposed to steam or CO₂ at 800–1,200°C
3. Steam reacts with carbon: C + H₂O → CO + H₂ (selective etching creates pore structure)
4. Process controlled to achieve target surface area and pore distribution

**Chemical activation**:
1. Raw material impregnated with activating agent (ZnCl₂, H₃PO₄, or KOH)
2. Heated to 400–800°C
3. Chemical agent dehydrates the material, creating pore structure
4. Agent washed out, leaving porous carbon

### Home Production

Not practical for cosmetic/food grade. Homemade charcoal (from campfire or wood burning) is NOT activated charcoal — it lacks the controlled pore structure and has much lower adsorption capacity. Purchase food-grade activated charcoal powder.

## Mechanism of Action

### Van der Waals Adsorption

1. Molecules in solution or gas phase contact the charcoal surface
2. Weak intermolecular forces (London dispersion forces, dipole interactions) attract molecules to the carbon surface
3. Molecules are held on the surface, effectively removed from solution/gas
4. Non-specific — binds most organic molecules (does not distinguish between "good" and "bad")
5. Adsorption is reversible in principle but very slow under ambient conditions

### Teeth Whitening

1. Activated charcoal particles are mildly abrasive (physical removal of surface stains)
2. Charcoal adsorbs tannin and chromogen molecules bound to tooth enamel surface
3. **Does NOT bleach teeth** — removes extrinsic (surface) stains only
4. Cannot change intrinsic tooth color (that requires peroxide-based bleaching)
5. Over-use may damage enamel due to abrasivity (RDA not well characterized for AC)

### Water Filtration

1. Water passes through granular activated carbon (GAC) bed
2. Chlorine is catalytically reduced on carbon surface: C + HOCl → CO* + HCl
3. Organic contaminants (VOCs, pesticides, herbicides) adsorb to internal pore surfaces
4. Activated carbon does NOT remove dissolved minerals, heavy metals, fluoride, or pathogens
5. Filter capacity is finite — carbon becomes saturated and must be replaced

### Facial / Skin Cleansing

1. Applied as a mask, activated charcoal contacts skin surface
2. Adsorbs excess sebum, dirt, and organic impurities from pores
3. The mechanism is similar to blotting paper but with enormously greater surface area
4. Rinse removes charcoal particles along with adsorbed impurities

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Activated Charcoal Face Mask**:
- 1 tsp activated charcoal powder
- 1 tbsp bentonite clay
- 1–2 tbsp water or apple cider vinegar
- Mix to paste, apply to face, leave 10–15 minutes
- Rinse — combines van der Waals adsorption (charcoal) with ionic exchange (bentonite)

**Charcoal Tooth Powder**:
- ½ tsp activated charcoal
- 2 tbsp baking soda
- Optional: 1 drop peppermint essential oil
- Dip wet toothbrush, brush gently for 2 minutes
- Use 1–2 times per week maximum (abrasivity concern)
- Removes surface stains from coffee, tea, wine

**Water Filter (DIY Gravity)**:
- Layer in container: gravel → sand → granular activated carbon → sand → gravel
- Pour water through filter
- Removes chlorine, taste/odor, many organic contaminants
- Does NOT make water microbiologically safe (does not kill bacteria/viruses)
- Replace activated carbon every 2–3 months

**Poultice for Insect Bites/Stings**:
- Mix 1 tsp activated charcoal with just enough water to form paste
- Apply to bite/sting, cover with bandage
- Adsorbs venom/irritant compounds at skin surface
- Traditional use — limited clinical evidence but widely reported anecdotally

**Deodorizer**:
- Place open container of granular activated charcoal in refrigerator, closet, or shoes
- Adsorbs odor-causing volatile organic compounds
- More effective than baking soda for organic odors
- Replace every 1–2 months

**Soap Colorant / Additive**:
- Add 1 tsp per pound of soap for black color and adsorptive cleansing
- Popular in facial soaps marketed for oily/acne-prone skin

### Medical Uses

- **Poison treatment**: Standard emergency treatment for oral poisoning (50–100g in adults within 1 hour of ingestion). Adsorbs most organic toxins. NOT effective for: alcohols, iron, lithium, cyanide, strong acids/bases.
- **GI decontamination**: Hospital use for drug overdose
- **Flatulence/bloating**: OTC supplement for gas (limited evidence)

## Efficacy & Research

### Adsorption Capacity

- Marsh, H. & Rodríguez-Reinoso, F. (2006) in *Activated Carbon* provided a comprehensive review of adsorption mechanisms, confirming that coconut shell-derived activated carbon achieves surface areas of 1,000–1,500 m²/g with predominantly microporous structure ideal for small molecule adsorption.

### Teeth Whitening

- Brooks, J.K., Bashirelahi, N., & Reynolds, M.A. (2017) in *Journal of the American Dental Association* reviewed charcoal-based dentifrices and found insufficient clinical evidence to support safety and efficacy claims. While surface stain removal is plausible, concerns about enamel abrasion remain unresolved. The ADA has not approved any charcoal toothpaste.

### Poison Treatment

- Juurlink, D.N. (2016) in *NEJM* reviewed the evidence for activated charcoal in acute poisoning, confirming efficacy when administered within 1 hour of ingestion for most organic poisons, with decreasing benefit after that window.

### Water Filtration

- NSF/ANSI Standard 42 certifies activated carbon filters for aesthetic water treatment (chlorine, taste, odor). Standard 53 certifies for health-related contaminants (VOCs, lead). GAC is the most widely used residential water treatment technology.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Topical Safety

- Activated charcoal is non-toxic topically
- Can stain skin, clothing, countertops, and grout (black pigment)
- Mildly abrasive — limit tooth brushing frequency to 1–2 times per week
- Not a skin irritant or sensitizer

### Inhalation

- Fine charcoal powder can irritate lungs if inhaled
- Use dust mask when handling fine powder
- Mix in well-ventilated area
- Once mixed into paste/product, inhalation risk is eliminated

### Oral Safety

- FDA-approved as OTC drug for poison treatment
- Generally safe for occasional oral use (tooth powder)
- Can adsorb medications if taken simultaneously — separate by 2+ hours
- May cause constipation with repeated oral use
- Can interfere with nutrient absorption if used frequently

### Non-Selective Adsorption

- Activated charcoal does not distinguish between harmful and beneficial substances
- In water filtration, it may adsorb beneficial minerals along with contaminants
- On skin, it adsorbs natural skin oils along with impurities
- Follow charcoal masks with moisturizer to replenish stripped oils

### Important Limitations

- **Not a substitute for medical-grade water purification** — does not remove bacteria, viruses, or dissolved minerals
- **Not a detox supplement** — the body's liver and kidneys are the actual detox organs; oral charcoal supplements for "detox" are marketing, not science
- **Does not whiten teeth beyond stain removal** — cannot change intrinsic tooth color

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Extraordinary adsorption capacity (surface area >1,000 m²/g)
- Van der Waals mechanism is complementary to bentonite's ionic exchange
- FDA-approved for poison treatment — well-established safety profile
- Effective water filter for chlorine, VOCs, taste/odor
- Versatile: face masks, tooth powder, water filtration, deodorization
- Inexpensive in bulk
- Non-toxic topically
- Long shelf life (indefinite if kept dry)

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Non-selective — adsorbs good and bad molecules alike
- Can interfere with medications if taken orally
- Stains everything black (messy to work with)
- Teeth whitening evidence is limited; enamel abrasion concerns
- "Detox" claims are largely unsupported
- Fine powder is an inhalation irritant
- Does not remove bacteria, heavy metals, or minerals from water
- Abrasive — limit use on teeth and delicate skin

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: Indefinite when kept sealed and dry (adsorption capacity does not degrade)
- **Storage**: Sealed container, cool and dry. Protect from moisture and strong odors (charcoal will adsorb ambient odors).
- **Container**: Airtight plastic or glass. Avoid metal containers.
- **Degradation**: Exposure to air slowly reduces adsorption capacity as the surface becomes saturated with atmospheric gases/moisture.

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: Approved OTC drug for treatment of acute poisoning. GRAS as food processing aid (decolorizing agent). Listed as inactive ingredient in approved drugs.
- **EU**: Approved for food contact use (water treatment). Regulated as cosmetic ingredient.
- **USP/NF**: Monographed as "Charcoal, Activated" for pharmaceutical use.
- **NSF International**: Certified under NSF/ANSI 42 and 53 for water filtration.

## Related Ingredients

- **Bentonite Clay** — Complementary adsorption mechanism: bentonite uses ionic exchange (attracts charged particles), while activated charcoal uses van der Waals forces (non-specific organic molecule adsorption). Together they provide comprehensive adsorption coverage.
- **Baking Soda** — Mild abrasive companion in tooth powder formulations.
- **Diatomaceous Earth** — Another physical filtration medium but with much lower surface area and different mechanism (mechanical filtration vs. adsorption).
- **Hydrogen Peroxide** — For teeth whitening, H₂O₂ bleaches intrinsically where charcoal only removes surface stains.
