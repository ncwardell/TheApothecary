# Citric Acid

> A weak tricarboxylic organic acid found naturally in citrus fruits, widely used as a chelating descaler, pH adjuster, and preservative, offering superior limescale removal compared to vinegar with no odor.

## Quick Reference

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Common Names | Citric acid, sour salt, lemon salt, E330 |
| Chemical Name / IUPAC | 2-Hydroxypropane-1,2,3-tricarboxylic acid |
| Chemical Formula | C₆H₈O₇ |
| CAS Number | 77-92-9 (anhydrous); 5949-29-1 (monohydrate) |
| Molecular Weight | 192.12 g/mol (anhydrous); 210.14 g/mol (monohydrate) |
| Appearance | White crystalline powder or granules; odorless |
| pH (in solution) | ~2.2 (1% solution); pKa₁ = 3.13, pKa₂ = 4.76, pKa₃ = 6.40 |
| Solubility | Highly soluble in water (592 g/L at 20°C); soluble in ethanol |

## Overview

Citric acid is one of the most widely produced organic acids in the world, with global production exceeding 2 million tonnes annually. It was first isolated from lemon juice in 1784 by the Swedish chemist Carl Wilhelm Scheele, and today it is found in virtually every kitchen, appearing as a food additive (E330), preservative, flavoring agent, and pH adjuster in thousands of products.

In nature, citric acid is abundant in citrus fruits (lemons contain 5–8% citric acid by weight) and plays a central role in the citric acid cycle (Krebs cycle) — the metabolic pathway by which all aerobic organisms generate energy. However, commercial citric acid is produced almost exclusively by industrial fermentation of sugars (typically corn-derived glucose or molasses) using the fungus *Aspergillus niger*.

For DIY household use, citric acid is a powerhouse descaler and chelating agent. Its three carboxylic acid groups allow it to bind (chelate) metal ions — particularly calcium and magnesium — far more effectively than acetic acid (vinegar), making it the superior choice for removing limescale, hard water deposits, and mineral buildup. It is odorless, food-safe, and biodegradable.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Molecular Structure

Citric acid is a triprotic acid with three carboxyl groups (-COOH) and one hydroxyl group (-OH):

- Three ionizable protons with pKa values of 3.13, 4.76, and 6.40
- This means it can donate up to three protons, acting as a triprotic acid
- The hydroxyl group on the central carbon makes it a good chelating agent — it wraps around metal ions

### Chelation Mechanism

The key property for cleaning applications:

1. Citric acid's three carboxylate groups and hydroxyl group coordinate around metal ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Fe³⁺)
2. This forms stable, water-soluble chelate complexes (metal citrates)
3. The complexation effectively "sequesters" metal ions, preventing them from redepositing
4. More effective than monoprotic acids (acetic acid) because multiple binding sites grip the metal ion more tightly

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Fermentation (Modern Method — >95% of production)

1. **Substrate**: Corn-derived glucose syrup, beet molasses, or sucrose
2. **Organism**: *Aspergillus niger* (a common, safe mold)
3. **Fermentation**: The fungus is cultured in aerated tanks at 25–30°C for 6–10 days
4. **Conditions**: Low pH, limited iron and manganese (forces the fungus to overproduce citric acid as a metabolic byproduct)
5. **Recovery**: The broth is filtered, citric acid is precipitated as calcium citrate by adding Ca(OH)₂, then re-acidified with sulfuric acid to release pure citric acid
6. **Crystallization**: Purified citric acid is crystallized, dried, and packaged

### Historical Extraction

- Originally extracted from citrus fruit juice by precipitation with lime (calcium hydroxide), followed by re-acidification with sulfuric acid
- This method was used commercially until the 1920s
- Citrus extraction is now uneconomical compared to fermentation

### Home "Extraction"

- Not practical. Lemons contain ~5–8% citric acid, but isolation requires chemical processing.
- Commercially produced citric acid powder is pure, food-grade, and very affordable.

## Mechanism of Action

### Descaling

The primary household mechanism:

1. **Dissolves calcium carbonate (limescale)**:
   - 3CaCO₃ + 2C₆H₈O₇ → Ca₃(C₆H₅O₇)₂ + 3H₂O + 3CO₂↑
   - The bubbling is CO₂ release
2. **Chelates dissolved minerals**: Once dissolved, citrate ions wrap around Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions, keeping them in solution and preventing redeposition
3. **More effective than vinegar**: Citric acid's three binding sites provide stronger chelation than acetic acid's single carboxylate

### Iron Chelation (Anti-Yellowing)

- In laundry, citric acid chelates dissolved iron in water
- Free iron ions cause yellowing of white fabrics (iron oxide staining)
- Citrate keeps iron in solution, preventing it from depositing on fabric fibers
- This is the same mechanism used in commercial "iron-out" products

### pH Adjustment

- Lowers pH in formulations where a controlled acidity is needed
- Buffer capacity (due to three pKa values) allows it to maintain pH in a useful range
- Used in bath bombs (reacts with baking soda to produce fizz): NaHCO₃ + C₆H₈O₇ → CO₂ + H₂O + sodium citrate

### Antimicrobial

- Low pH inhibits bacterial growth
- Less effective as a standalone disinfectant than hydrogen peroxide
- Primarily used for its descaling and chelating properties rather than antimicrobial action

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Descaler (Primary Use)**:
- 1–2 tbsp dissolved in hot water
- Kettles: fill and boil, let sit, rinse
- Coffee makers: run full cycle, then 2–3 water cycles
- Showerheads: soak in citric acid solution
- Dishwashers: sprinkle in bottom, run empty cycle
- Faucets: spray solution, let sit 15–30 minutes, wipe

**Toilet Cleaner**:
- Sprinkle 2–3 tbsp in toilet bowl
- Let sit 30 minutes to overnight for heavy limescale
- Scrub with brush, flush

**Laundry Rinse (Iron Chelation)**:
- ½ cup dissolved in rinse cycle water
- Replaces vinegar in the rinse — odorless, better iron chelation
- Prevents yellowing of whites in hard water areas

**Bath Bombs**:
- 1 part citric acid + 2 parts baking soda + binding agent (coconut oil)
- Citric acid + baking soda = fizzing reaction when wet
- Add essential oils, dried flowers, colorants

**Natural Preservative**:
- Lowers pH of homemade formulations, inhibiting bacterial growth
- Used in homemade canning, beverages, and cosmetics

### Industrial Uses

- **Food and beverage**: Acidulant, flavoring agent, preservative (E330) — found in soft drinks, candy, canned foods
- **Pharmaceuticals**: Effervescent tablet formulations; pH adjuster
- **Cosmetics**: pH adjuster in skincare, hair care products
- **Water treatment**: Descaling, metal chelation
- **Cleaning products**: Primary active ingredient in many commercial descalers

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Incompatibilities

- **Do NOT mix with baking soda or washing soda for cleaning purposes**: They neutralize each other, producing salt water and CO₂. The fizzing looks dramatic but wastes both ingredients. Use sequentially for different jobs.
- **Do NOT mix with bleach**: Can produce chlorine gas
- **Do NOT use on natural stone**: Acid etches marble, granite, travertine, and limestone (same caution as vinegar)

### Handling

- Mild skin irritant at high concentrations — wear gloves when handling concentrated solutions
- Can irritate eyes — avoid contact; rinse with water if splashed
- Dust can irritate respiratory tract — avoid inhaling powder
- Non-toxic if ingested in small amounts (it's a food additive)

### Surface Compatibility

- **Safe on**: Stainless steel, glass, ceramic, porcelain, plastic, most tile, chrome
- **NOT safe on**: Marble, granite, limestone, travertine, unsealed grout, some aluminum finishes

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Superior descaling compared to vinegar (chelation)
- Completely odorless (major advantage over vinegar)
- Food-safe and non-toxic
- Highly water-soluble and easy to work with
- Excellent iron chelator (prevents fabric yellowing)
- Concentrated powder — small amounts go far
- Long shelf life
- Biodegradable

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Not effective for grease (need alkaline cleaner for that)
- Cannot use on natural stone
- Must not be mixed with alkaline cleaners (neutralization)
- Not a disinfectant
- Powder can irritate lungs if inhaled
- Slightly more expensive per use than vinegar (but more effective)
- Derived from *Aspergillus niger* fermentation (relevant for those with mold sensitivities, though final product is purified)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: 3–5 years when stored properly; essentially indefinite in sealed container
- **Storage**: Cool, dry place in airtight container. Protect from moisture.
- **Hygroscopy**: Slightly hygroscopic — will clump if exposed to humidity. Clumped citric acid is still fully effective; break up and use normally.
- **Container**: Original bag resealed, or transfer to glass/plastic jar with tight lid

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) as a food additive. 21 CFR 184.1033.
- **EU**: Approved food additive E330. No restrictions on use in cosmetics.
- **Codex Alimentarius**: Listed as permitted food additive with no ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake) limit.
- **Organic**: Available from organic fermentation sources.

## Related Ingredients

- **White Vinegar** — Weaker acid (monoprotic); cheaper but has strong odor. Vinegar is better for general surface cleaning; citric acid is better for heavy descaling.
- **Washing Soda** — Alkaline counterpart. Do not mix. Use washing soda for grease, citric acid for minerals.
- **Baking Soda** — Reacts with citric acid to produce fizz (bath bombs). Not useful to mix for cleaning.
- **Oxalic Acid** — Stronger specialty acid for rust and tannin stains. More toxic; use citric acid where possible.
- **Lemon Juice** — Natural source of citric acid (~5–6%); far more expensive and less concentrated than powder.
