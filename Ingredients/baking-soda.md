# Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate)

> A mild alkaline salt with pH ~8.3 in solution, functioning as an acid neutralizer, gentle abrasive, and deodorizer, with widespread applications spanning oral care, deodorant, cleaning, and laundry.

## Overview

Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃), universally known as baking soda, is one of the most versatile and widely used chemicals in the household. It is a white crystalline solid that appears as a fine powder, with a slightly salty, alkaline taste. Chemically, it is the monosodium salt of carbonic acid, positioning it as a mild alkali -- strong enough to neutralize acids and raise pH, but gentle enough for direct skin contact and oral use in most individuals.

Sodium bicarbonate occurs naturally as the mineral nahcolite, found in evaporite deposits worldwide. The largest known deposit is the Piceance Basin in Colorado, where the Green River Formation contains an estimated 29 billion metric tons of nahcolite. Commercially, sodium bicarbonate is produced either by mining and refining trona ore (a related sodium carbonate mineral) or through the Solvay process, a 19th-century industrial chemistry innovation that synthesizes sodium carbonate from salt and limestone, which is then converted to bicarbonate.

The substance has been used by humans for millennia. Ancient Egyptians used natron (a naturally occurring mixture of sodium carbonate and bicarbonate) for cleaning, mummification, and personal hygiene. In modern times, baking soda became a kitchen staple after Church & Dwight (Arm & Hammer) began marketing it in 1846. Its role in natural personal care has grown enormously since the "natural living" movement gained momentum in the 2000s, where it serves as a core ingredient in DIY deodorant, toothpaste, and cleaning products. However, its alkaline pH has also generated legitimate concerns about skin and enamel safety that must be carefully considered.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Molecular Structure

Sodium bicarbonate consists of sodium cations (Na⁺) and bicarbonate anions (HCO₃⁻) arranged in a monoclinic crystal lattice. The bicarbonate ion has a trigonal planar geometry with the carbon atom at the center, bonded to three oxygen atoms and one hydrogen atom. The hydrogen is bonded to one of the oxygens, creating an asymmetric structure that enables hydrogen bonding between adjacent bicarbonate ions in the crystal.

The crystal structure features chains of bicarbonate ions linked by hydrogen bonds, with sodium ions occupying octahedral coordination sites between the chains. This structure contributes to the moderate hardness (Mohs 2.5) and the tendency of the crystals to fracture into fine, rounded particles rather than sharp shards -- a property that makes baking soda a gentle abrasive.

### Acid-Base Chemistry

Sodium bicarbonate is amphoteric, meaning it can act as either an acid or a base depending on the reaction partner:

**As a base (most common behavior):**
NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑

**As an acid (in the presence of strong bases):**
NaHCO₃ + NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O

**Thermal decomposition:**
2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂↑ (begins at ~50°C, complete at ~200°C)

The pH of sodium bicarbonate solutions is governed by the equilibrium:

HCO₃⁻ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ + OH⁻ (Kb = 2.3 × 10⁻⁸)

This yields a pH of approximately 8.3 in a 1% solution -- mildly alkaline but well within the range tolerated by skin for limited contact.

### Buffer Capacity

One of baking soda's most important chemical properties is its buffering capacity. In solution, the bicarbonate/carbonic acid system (H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻) resists changes in pH. This is, in fact, the same buffer system that regulates blood pH in the human body (maintained at pH 7.35-7.45). This buffering capacity means that baking soda moderates pH rather than driving it to extremes, unlike strong bases such as sodium hydroxide (lye).

| Property | Value | Significance |

### Odor Neutralization Chemistry

Baking soda neutralizes odors through acid-base reactions rather than masking. Many malodorous compounds are acidic in nature:

- **Butyric acid** (body odor component): CH₃CH₂CH₂COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃CH₂CH₂COONa + H₂O + CO₂
- **Isovaleric acid** (foot/sweat odor): (CH₃)₂CHCH₂COOH + NaHCO₃ → sodium isovalerate + H₂O + CO₂
- **Propionic acid** (bacterial metabolite): CH₃CH₂COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃CH₂COONa + H₂O + CO₂

The resulting sodium salts are odorless and water-soluble, effectively eliminating the odor at its chemical source. Baking soda can also absorb some volatile organic compounds through physical adsorption onto its crystal surfaces.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Mining from Nahcolite Deposits

The purest natural source of sodium bicarbonate is nahcolite, mined primarily from the Green River Formation in Colorado. The process involves:

1. **Solution mining**: Hot water is injected into the nahcolite-bearing geological formation through wells
2. The dissolved sodium bicarbonate brine is pumped to the surface
3. The solution is cooled, causing crystallization of pure NaHCO₃
4. Crystals are filtered, washed, dried, and milled to desired particle size

This method produces "natural" sodium bicarbonate, chemically identical to synthetic versions but marketed as a premium product.

### From Trona Ore (Primary US Method)

Trona (Na₂CO₃·NaHCO₃·2H₂O) is mined extensively from deposits near Green River, Wyoming (the world's largest trona deposit):

1. Trona ore is mined (primarily underground mining)
2. Ore is crushed and dissolved in hot water
3. Impurities are filtered out
4. The solution is treated with CO₂ gas under pressure:
   Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ → 2NaHCO₃
5. Sodium bicarbonate crystallizes out of solution
6. Crystals are filtered, dried, and packaged

### Solvay Process (Historical/Global Method)

Developed by Ernest Solvay in 1861, this process synthesizes sodium bicarbonate from:
- Sodium chloride (salt, NaCl)
- Calcium carbonate (limestone, CaCO₃)
- Ammonia (NH₃, used as a recyclable intermediate)

The key reaction:
NaCl + NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NaHCO₃ + NH₄Cl

The ammonia is recovered and recycled, and the calcium chloride byproduct has its own commercial uses. While elegant, the Solvay process has been largely supplanted in the US by trona mining, which is more economical.

### Home "Production"

You cannot practically synthesize sodium bicarbonate at home. However, you can convert washing soda (sodium carbonate, Na₂CO₃) to baking soda by bubbling CO₂ through a concentrated solution:

Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ → 2NaHCO₃

This is impractical for home use. Conversely, you can easily convert baking soda TO washing soda by heating:

2NaHCO₃ --heat--> Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂

Spread baking soda on a baking sheet and heat in an oven at 400°F (200°C) for 1 hour. The resulting washing soda is a stronger alkali (pH ~11) useful for heavy-duty cleaning and laundry.

## Mechanism of Action

### In Deodorant

Baking soda addresses body odor through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Acid neutralization**: The primary odor-causing compounds in sweat are short-chain fatty acids (isovaleric acid, propionic acid, butyric acid) produced by bacterial metabolism of sweat components. Baking soda neutralizes these acids into odorless sodium salts.

2. **pH elevation**: Raising the pH of the underarm environment from its natural ~5.5-6.5 to ~7-8 creates conditions less favorable for the growth of *Corynebacterium* and *Staphylococcus* species, the primary odor-producing bacteria in the axilla.

3. **Moisture absorption**: Baking soda absorbs some moisture, though it is less effective at this than arrowroot or cornstarch.

Note: Baking soda does NOT function as an antiperspirant. It does not reduce sweating. It prevents odor, not wetness.

### In Oral Care

**As an abrasive**: With an RDA (Relative Dentin Abrasivity) of approximately 7, baking soda is one of the least abrasive cleaning agents available -- far gentler than most commercial toothpastes (typical RDA 60-150). For context, the ADA recommends toothpastes with RDA below 250. The rounded particle morphology of sodium bicarbonate crystals further reduces the risk of enamel scratching.

**As a pH buffer**: Baking soda raises the pH of the oral environment, counteracting the acid produced by cariogenic bacteria (*Streptococcus mutans*). Dental caries require sustained pH below ~5.5 (the critical pH for enamel demineralization). By buffering oral pH above this threshold, baking soda can help prevent acid-mediated enamel erosion.

**As an antimicrobial**: The elevated pH environment is less hospitable to acid-loving oral pathogens. Additionally, baking soda can disrupt bacterial biofilms (dental plaque) through a combination of mechanical disruption and osmotic effects.

### In Cleaning

Baking soda functions in cleaning through:

1. **Mild abrasion**: The Mohs hardness of 2.5 means it can scour without scratching most household surfaces (glass, stainless steel, porcelain)
2. **Saponification**: At elevated pH, baking soda partially converts grease (triglycerides) to soap (sodium salts of fatty acids), aiding in grease removal
3. **Effervescent action**: When combined with vinegar or citric acid, the CO₂ release provides mechanical agitation that helps dislodge debris from crevices
4. **Odor neutralization**: Absorbs and neutralizes acidic volatile compounds in refrigerators, trash cans, carpets, etc.

### In Laundry

- **Water softening**: Bicarbonate ions can partially sequester calcium and magnesium ions in hard water (though less effectively than washing soda or borax)
- **pH boosting**: Raising wash water pH to ~8-8.5 enhances the effectiveness of surfactants in laundry detergent
- **Brightening**: Alkaline conditions help suspend soil particles in the wash water, preventing redeposition on fabrics
- **Odor removal**: Neutralizes acidic odor compounds in fabrics

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Natural Deodorant (Primary Use)**:
- Standard recipe: 2-3 tablespoons coconut oil + 1 tablespoon baking soda + 2-3 tablespoons arrowroot powder + essential oils
- The baking soda handles odor neutralization while arrowroot absorbs moisture
- **IMPORTANT**: Reduce baking soda for sensitive skin (1 teaspoon or less per batch), or replace entirely with magnesium hydroxide or zinc oxide

**Tooth Powder / Toothpaste**:
- Simple tooth powder: baking soda + fine sea salt (4:1 ratio)
- Paste version: baking soda + coconut oil + peppermint essential oil
- The extremely low RDA (~7) makes it safe for daily brushing in most individuals
- Do NOT combine with hydrogen peroxide at high concentrations (can damage soft tissue with repeated use)

**Gentle Facial Scrub**:
- Mix small amount of baking soda with water or honey to form a paste
- Gently massage in circular motions, rinse
- Use infrequently (1-2 times per month maximum) due to pH concerns
- Not recommended for daily use on facial skin

**All-Purpose Cleaning Paste**:
- Mix baking soda with enough water to form a thick paste
- Apply to surfaces, scrub with sponge or brush, rinse
- Excellent for sinks, tubs, tile grout, stovetops

**Laundry Booster**:
- Add 1/2 cup baking soda to wash cycle along with regular detergent
- Softens water, boosts cleaning power, helps neutralize odors
- Effective for workout clothes, towels, and cloth diapers

**Drain Maintenance**:
- Pour 1/2 cup baking soda down drain, follow with 1/2 cup vinegar
- Let fizz for 15-30 minutes, flush with hot water
- Effective for maintenance but will not clear serious clogs (use a mechanical snake for those)

**Produce Wash**:
- Dissolve 1 teaspoon baking soda per 2 cups water
- Soak fruits and vegetables for 12-15 minutes
- Research by He et al. (2017) showed this removes more pesticide residues than plain water or commercial produce washes

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Food industry**: Leavening agent in baking (reacts with acids to produce CO₂ for dough rise)
- **Fire suppression**: Class B and C fire extinguishers use sodium bicarbonate powder
- **Medical**: IV sodium bicarbonate for metabolic acidosis; antacid for heartburn
- **Water treatment**: pH adjustment in swimming pools and water treatment plants
- **Agriculture**: Foliar spray for powdery mildew on plants
- **Chemical industry**: Reagent, buffering agent, CO₂ source
- **Flue gas treatment**: Neutralizes sulfur dioxide emissions from power plants

### Historical Uses

- **Ancient Egypt**: Natron (containing NaHCO₃) was used in mummification, personal hygiene, and as a cleaning agent
- **18th century**: "Saleratus" (sodium bicarbonate) was used as a leavening agent in baking, gradually replacing pearlash (potassium carbonate)
- **1846**: Church & Dwight begins selling Arm & Hammer branded baking soda, one of the oldest consumer product brands still in existence
- **Civil War era**: Used as a tooth powder by soldiers and civilians
- **Traditional medicine**: Used as an antacid and remedy for urinary tract infections (alkalinizing urine)

## Efficacy & Research

### Oral Care

- **Plaque removal**: Putt et al. (2008) in the *Journal of Clinical Dentistry* compared baking soda-based dentifrices to non-baking soda dentifrices and found significantly greater plaque removal with baking soda formulations. A meta-analysis by Baxley et al. (2017) in the *Journal of the American Dental Association* confirmed this finding across 17 randomized controlled trials.

- **Gingivitis**: Gerlach et al. (2017) in the *Journal of Clinical Dentistry* found that baking soda toothpastes were associated with reduced bleeding on probing and improved gingival health compared to non-baking soda controls.

- **Antimicrobial effects**: Drake (1997) in *Compendium of Continuing Education in Dentistry* reviewed the antimicrobial properties of sodium bicarbonate, finding significant bactericidal activity against several oral pathogens including *Streptococcus mutans*.

- **Whitening**: Limited evidence supports baking soda as a tooth whitener. Its mechanism is mechanical (abrasive removal of surface stains) rather than chemical bleaching. Effectiveness is modest compared to peroxide-based whitening systems.

### Deodorant Efficacy

- No rigorous clinical trials have compared baking soda deodorant to commercial antiperspirants/deodorants. The evidence is primarily mechanistic (acid neutralization chemistry) and anecdotal.
- Urban et al. (2016) in *Microbiome* characterized the axillary microbiome and confirmed that pH significantly influences the relative abundance of odor-producing bacteria, supporting the rationale for pH modification via baking soda.

### Cleaning Efficacy

- He et al. (2017) in the *Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry* demonstrated that a 1% sodium bicarbonate solution was more effective than water alone or Clorox bleach at removing pesticide residues from apples, including systemic pesticides like thiabendazole.

### Skin Safety

- **Contact dermatitis**: There are documented cases of allergic contact dermatitis from baking soda in deodorant use, though the prevalence is debated. Zirwas and Moennich (2008) in *Dermatitis* noted that sodium bicarbonate is an under-recognized cause of allergic contact dermatitis, particularly in the axillary area.

- **pH disruption**: The skin's acid mantle (pH 4.5-5.5) is disrupted by prolonged contact with alkaline substances. Theoretical concerns about chronic pH disruption from daily baking soda deodorant use exist, though clinical evidence of harm is limited in healthy skin.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Skin Sensitivity (Primary Concern)

**A significant minority of people (estimated 10-25% based on community reports, though no formal epidemiological data exists) develop irritation or rash from baking soda deodorant.** This manifests as:
- Redness and itching in the underarm area
- Rough, darkened skin
- Burning sensation, especially after shaving
- In severe cases, peeling or raw skin

**Risk factors for sensitivity**:
- Shaving (micro-abrasions allow alkaline solution to penetrate deeper)
- Naturally sensitive or eczema-prone skin
- Heavy application
- Hot, humid climates (more sweating = more baking soda dissolving = higher pH exposure)

**Mitigation strategies**:
- Reduce baking soda concentration in deodorant formulas
- Apply to fully dry skin, not immediately after showering
- Wait 10-15 minutes after shaving before applying
- Switch to magnesium hydroxide or zinc oxide as the active deodorizing ingredient if sensitivity develops

### Oral Safety

- Safe for oral use in toothpaste/tooth powder at standard concentrations
- Avoid swallowing large quantities (can cause metabolic alkalosis -- nausea, vomiting, muscle spasms)
- Do not use as a baking soda "rinse" with a very concentrated solution; keep oral products well-diluted
- Despite its low RDA, some dentists caution against using dry baking soda powder directly on teeth without mixing into a paste (the undissolved crystals are more abrasive than the dissolved solution)

### General Safety

- **Non-toxic**: Baking soda has very low toxicity. The LD50 (oral, rat) is 3,360 mg/kg -- meaning it would take extraordinary quantities to cause acute poisoning.
- **Not a skin sensitizer** in the immunological sense for most people; irritation is typically irritant contact dermatitis (chemical irritation) rather than true allergy
- **Pregnancy**: Safe for external use. No concerns for topical application during pregnancy.
- **Children**: Safe for children over 2 years in cosmetic applications. Keep away from eyes.
- **Inhalation**: Fine powder can irritate airways if inhaled. Mix in well-ventilated areas.

### First Aid

- **Eye contact**: Flush with water for 15 minutes. The alkaline pH can cause irritation but rarely serious injury.
- **Ingestion**: Small amounts are harmless (it is a food ingredient). For large quantities, drink water and contact poison control.
- **Skin irritation**: Discontinue use, wash area with mild cleanser and water, apply soothing emollient (aloe vera, coconut oil).

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Extremely effective deodorizer through acid-base chemistry (not just masking)
- One of the gentlest abrasives available (RDA ~7)
- Food-grade safety; non-toxic
- Extraordinarily inexpensive (pennies per use)
- Available everywhere -- grocery stores, pharmacies, bulk suppliers
- Versatile across dozens of applications (personal care, cleaning, cooking, medical)
- Long shelf life with no special storage requirements
- Environmentally benign; biodegradable
- Effective pH buffer
- No synthetic fragrances or preservatives needed

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Causes skin irritation in a significant minority of users when used in deodorant
- Alkaline pH (~8.3) disrupts skin's acid mantle (pH 4.5-5.5)
- Does not reduce sweating (is a deodorizer, not an antiperspirant)
- Can damage certain surfaces (aluminum, natural stone) due to alkalinity
- Effervescent reaction with vinegar, while visually impressive, actually neutralizes both ingredients and produces salt water + CO₂ -- the combination is chemically less effective than either ingredient used alone
- Gritty texture if not fully dissolved; can pill in some formulations
- Not suitable as a sole ingredient in toothpaste (lacks fluoride, which has stronger evidence for caries prevention)
- Reacts with some essential oils at elevated pH

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Storage**: Cool, dry location. Airtight container.
- **Container**: Original box or transferred to glass/food-grade plastic jar. Metal containers are acceptable (unlike with acidic ingredients).
- **Shelf life**: Essentially indefinite in dry form. No chemical degradation occurs at room temperature. However, it may absorb odors and moisture from the environment if left open, which is actually a feature when using an open box as a deodorizer.
- **Testing freshness**: Add a teaspoon to a few tablespoons of vinegar. If it fizzes vigorously, it is still potent. Lack of fizzing indicates it has converted to sodium carbonate (washing soda) from heat or moisture exposure, and should be replaced for baking and personal care use (still fine for cleaning).
- **Avoid**: Humidity, which causes clumping and can accelerate conversion to sodium carbonate. Do not store in the bathroom.

### Quality Indicators

- **Purity**: Food-grade / USP-grade sodium bicarbonate is 99%+ pure. There is effectively no quality difference between brands for personal care use.
- **Particle size**: Finer grind is preferred for deodorant and toothpaste (less gritty). Some specialty suppliers offer "ultra-fine" grades.
- **Source**: "Natural" (mined) vs. "synthetic" (Solvay process) are chemically identical. The distinction is primarily a marketing consideration.
- **Aluminum-free labeling**: Baking soda NEVER contains aluminum. The "aluminum-free" label is marketing that addresses a persistent myth. (Baking POWDER sometimes contains sodium aluminum sulfate; baking SODA does not.)

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) as a food ingredient. Approved as an active ingredient in OTC antacids (21 CFR 331.11). Widely used in FDA-regulated oral care products.
- **ADA**: The American Dental Association has granted the ADA Seal of Acceptance to several baking soda toothpastes.
- **EU**: Permitted in cosmetics without restriction under the INCI name "Sodium Bicarbonate."
- **EPA**: Not classified as a hazardous substance. Registered for use as a biopesticide (fungicide for powdery mildew).
- **USDA Organic**: Permitted as a processing aid in organic food production.

## Related Ingredients

- **Washing Soda (Sodium Carbonate, Na₂CO₃)** - Stronger alkali (pH ~11); made by heating baking soda; better for heavy-duty cleaning and laundry but too harsh for skin
- **Arrowroot Powder** - Paired with baking soda in deodorant; handles moisture absorption while baking soda handles odor
- **Coconut Oil** - Standard base for baking soda deodorant formulations
- **Citric Acid** - Acidic counterpart; reacts vigorously with baking soda to produce CO₂ (used in bath bombs and effervescent cleaners)
- **Borax** - Another alkaline mineral used in cleaning and laundry; stronger than baking soda
- **Magnesium Hydroxide** - Alternative deodorizing active for those sensitive to baking soda; similar acid-neutralizing mechanism but at a lower pH (~9 in saturated solution)
- **Bentonite Clay** - Sometimes combined with baking soda in tooth powder for additional mineral content and adsorptive capacity
