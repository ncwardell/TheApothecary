# Aloe Vera

> A succulent plant whose inner leaf gel contains acemannan and other polysaccharides that accelerate wound healing, reduce burn severity, and provide anti-inflammatory hydration — the definitive natural treatment for minor burns and the missing wound-care ingredient in a comprehensive DIY formulation system.

## Overview

Aloe vera (*Aloe barbadensis* Miller) is one of the most extensively studied medicinal plants, with a therapeutic history spanning over 5,000 years. Egyptian papyri from 1550 BCE describe aloe preparations for wound healing. The plant's inner leaf gel contains over 200 biologically active compounds, but its therapeutic effects are driven primarily by **acemannan** — an acetylated mannose polysaccharide that directly stimulates macrophage activation, fibroblast proliferation, and collagen synthesis in wound healing.

In a rigorous DIY system, aloe vera fills the **wound care and burn treatment gap**. No other ingredient in the repository provides:
1. Direct wound-healing stimulation through immune cell activation
2. First-aid burn treatment with evidence of reduced healing time
3. Anti-inflammatory action at the skin surface without systemic effects
4. A natural hydrogel matrix that maintains wound moisture (critical for optimal healing)

The distinction between the two parts of the aloe leaf is critical:
- **Inner gel** (parenchyma): Clear, mucilaginous gel containing acemannan and other beneficial polysaccharides. This is what is used therapeutically.
- **Latex** (outer leaf, yellow sap): Contains **aloin** (barbaloin), an anthraquinone that is a potent laxative and potential carcinogen. This must be removed and discarded.

Commercial "aloe vera gel" products often contain only 1–10% actual aloe with thickeners, preservatives, and fragrance. For DIY use, either grow the plant and harvest fresh gel or purchase inner-fillet aloe vera juice/gel with minimal additives.

### Acemannan Structure

- β-(1→4)-linked acetylated mannose polymer
- Molecular weight: ~1,000,000 Da
- Degree of acetylation determines biological activity
- Acemannan activates macrophages through mannose receptor binding
- This triggers release of IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α, and interferon — immune mediators that orchestrate wound healing

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Growing Aloe Vera (Recommended for DIY)

Aloe vera is one of the easiest medicinal plants to grow:

1. **Climate**: Hardy in USDA zones 9–11 outdoors; grows as houseplant anywhere
2. **Soil**: Well-draining sandy/cactus mix
3. **Watering**: Allow soil to dry completely between waterings (succulent — overwatering kills)
4. **Light**: Bright indirect light; tolerates some direct sun
5. **Propagation**: Remove pups (offsets) from base of mature plant
6. **Harvest**: Mature leaves (outer, lower) from plants at least 3 years old
7. **Yield**: One mature plant provides 2–3 leaves per month

### Harvesting Fresh Gel

1. Cut an outer (mature) leaf close to the base
2. Stand leaf upright for 10–15 minutes to drain yellow latex (aloin)
3. Wash the leaf to remove remaining latex
4. Slice off the flat side of the leaf
5. Scoop out the clear inner gel with a spoon
6. Blend briefly if desired for smoother consistency
7. Use immediately or refrigerate (shelf life: 5–7 days fresh)

### Preserving Aloe Gel

- **Refrigeration**: 5–7 days in sealed container
- **Freezing**: Freeze in ice cube trays for months of storage; thaw as needed
- **Vitamin C stabilization**: Add 500mg crushed vitamin C per cup of gel (antioxidant + pH buffer)
- **Vitamin E**: Add a few drops to prevent oxidation
- **Commercial preservation**: Requires proper preservative system (potassium sorbate + citric acid is common)

### Commercial Production

1. Leaves harvested from plantation-grown *Aloe barbadensis* plants
2. Outer rind removed mechanically (inner-fillet processing — preferred, removes aloin)
3. Gel is homogenized, pasteurized (or cold-processed), and preserved
4. Spray-dried for powder form or sold as liquid gel/juice
5. Quality varies enormously — IASC (International Aloe Science Council) certification indicates quality standards

## Mechanism of Action

### Wound Healing

1. **Macrophage activation**: Acemannan binds mannose receptors on macrophages, activating them
2. **Cytokine release**: Activated macrophages release IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α — signaling molecules that recruit fibroblasts and immune cells to the wound
3. **Fibroblast proliferation**: Glucomannan and growth hormones (auxins, gibberellins) stimulate fibroblast multiplication
4. **Collagen synthesis**: Fibroblasts produce collagen types I and III, rebuilding the extracellular matrix
5. **Angiogenesis**: Growth factors promote new blood vessel formation in the wound bed
6. **Net effect**: Accelerated progression through inflammatory, proliferative, and remodeling phases of wound healing

### Burn Treatment

1. Aloe gel provides immediate cooling (water content + evaporative cooling)
2. Anti-inflammatory compounds (bradykinase, salicylic acid, magnesium lactate) reduce pain and swelling
3. Acemannan stimulates tissue repair as described above
4. Gel maintains moist wound environment — modern wound care principle (moist healing is faster than dry)
5. Antimicrobial compounds reduce infection risk

### Anti-Inflammatory

1. Bradykinase enzyme breaks down bradykinin (a pro-inflammatory peptide)
2. Salicylic acid provides local analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects
3. Plant sterols (lupeol, campesterol) inhibit inflammation
4. Magnesium lactate inhibits histamine release, reducing itching

### Moisturizing / Hydrating

1. Polysaccharides form a hydrating film on skin surface
2. Mucopolysaccharides bind moisture to the skin
3. The natural pH (4.0–5.5) is compatible with skin's acid mantle
4. Amino acids soften and moisturize the stratum corneum

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**First-Aid Burn Gel (Primary Use)**:
- Apply fresh aloe gel directly to minor burns (first-degree, minor second-degree)
- Cool the burn under running water first, then apply aloe gel
- Reapply 2–3 times daily
- Cover with non-stick bandage if needed
- **Do NOT use on deep or severe burns — seek medical attention**

**After-Sun Soother**:
- ½ cup aloe vera gel
- 1 tbsp coconut oil (melted)
- 5 drops lavender essential oil (optional)
- 1 tsp vitamin E oil
- Mix, store in refrigerator
- Apply to sunburned skin for cooling relief

**Aloe Vera Face Gel / Moisturizer**:
- 2 tbsp aloe vera gel
- 1 tsp jojoba oil
- 2–3 drops essential oil (optional)
- Apply as lightweight moisturizer
- Especially good for oily skin (hydrates without heavy oils)

**Wound Healing Gel**:
- Fresh aloe gel applied directly to minor cuts, scrapes, abrasions
- Maintains moist wound environment
- Acemannan stimulates tissue repair
- Antimicrobial compounds help prevent infection

**Hair Gel / Leave-In Conditioner**:
- Aloe vera gel applied to damp hair
- Provides light hold and moisture
- Reduces frizz without weighing hair down
- Rinse-free

**Soothing Skin Spray**:
- 3 parts aloe vera juice
- 1 part witch hazel
- Optional: few drops tea tree oil
- Spray bottle for insect bites, minor irritation, razor burn

**Hand Sanitizer Base (with alcohol)**:
- 2 parts isopropyl alcohol (91%)
- 1 part aloe vera gel
- Aloe provides skin-conditioning to offset alcohol's drying effect
- Not as effective as commercial formulations but a functional base

### Medical Uses

- **Burn treatment**: Systematic reviews confirm aloe reduces burn healing time by ~9 days versus conventional treatment for first and second-degree burns
- **Radiation dermatitis**: Used in oncology for radiation-induced skin damage (evidence mixed but widely used)
- **Oral mucositis**: Aloe mouthwash for chemotherapy-induced mouth sores
- **Wound dressings**: Aloe-impregnated wound dressings for chronic wounds

## Efficacy & Research

### Burn Healing

- Maenthaisong, R., Chaiyakunapruk, N., et al. (2007) in *Burns* conducted a systematic review of four controlled trials and found that aloe vera reduced burn healing time by an average of 8.79 days compared to conventional treatment (silver sulfadiazine) for first and second-degree burns.

### Wound Healing

- Dat, A.D., Poon, F., et al. (2012) in *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews* reviewed aloe vera for acute and chronic wounds. Found promising evidence for burn healing but insufficient high-quality evidence for other wound types. Called for more rigorous trials.

### Acemannan Mechanism

- Zhang, L. & Tizard, I.R. (1996) in *Immunopharmacology* demonstrated that acemannan activates macrophages to release IL-1 and TNF-α, providing the molecular basis for aloe's wound-healing properties.

### Skin Moisturization

- Dal'Belo, S.E., Gaspar, L.R., & Maia Campos, P.M.B.G. (2006) in *Skin Research and Technology* showed that aloe vera gel improved skin hydration without compromising transepidermal water loss, and increased stratum corneum water content after 2 weeks of use.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Topical Safety (Gel)

- Aloe vera gel is generally safe for topical use
- Allergic reactions are uncommon but possible (more common in people allergic to Liliaceae family plants — garlic, onions, tulips)
- Patch test recommended for first-time use
- Safe for use on children and during pregnancy (topical only)
- Do NOT apply to deep wounds, surgical incisions, or severe burns (seek medical attention)

### Aloin / Latex Warning

- **Aloe latex (yellow sap) is NOT the same as aloe gel**
- Latex contains aloin — a potent stimulant laxative
- Chronic oral use of aloin has been linked to electrolyte imbalance and is classified IARC Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic)
- The FDA banned aloe latex from OTC laxative products in 2002
- When harvesting fresh aloe, always drain the yellow latex completely

### Internal Use Cautions

- Aloe vera juice (inner gel, latex-free) is consumed as a dietary supplement
- Large quantities may cause diarrhea (residual aloin) or interact with medications
- May lower blood glucose — caution in diabetic patients
- May interact with diuretics, digoxin, and anticoagulants

### Fresh Gel Storage

- Fresh aloe gel spoils rapidly (5–7 days refrigerated)
- Oxidation turns gel pink/brown (still safe but diminished potency)
- Bacterial contamination is possible in unpreserved gel
- Freeze for long-term storage; use vitamin C to extend refrigerated shelf life

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Evidence-based wound healing and burn treatment
- Acemannan provides unique macrophage-activating mechanism
- Easy to grow at home — renewable, zero-cost supply
- Mildly acidic pH matches skin's acid mantle
- Lightweight, non-greasy moisturizer
- Multi-functional: burns, wounds, moisturizer, hair gel, after-sun
- Long history of safe topical use
- Compatible with most other DIY ingredients
- Natural hydrogel — maintains moist wound environment

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Fresh gel has very short shelf life (5–7 days)
- Commercial products often contain very little actual aloe
- Must remove aloin/latex carefully when harvesting
- Allergic reactions possible (uncommon)
- Evidence strongest for burns — weaker for other wound types
- Not a standalone preservative or antimicrobial
- Requires mature plant (3+ years) for meaningful gel harvest
- Potency varies between plants, harvests, and products

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Fresh gel**: 5–7 days refrigerated; months frozen (ice cube trays)
- **Commercial gel (preserved)**: 1–2 years (check expiration date)
- **Aloe powder (freeze-dried)**: 2–5 years in sealed, dry storage
- **Living plant**: Indefinite with basic care
- **Stabilization**: Add vitamin C (500mg per cup) and/or vitamin E to extend fresh gel shelf life
- **Signs of spoilage**: Pink/brown discoloration, off-smell, slimy texture

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: Aloe vera gel is approved for use in cosmetics and as a dietary supplement. Aloe latex was banned from OTC laxative products in 2002 due to safety concerns. Aloe gel is listed in the FDA Inactive Ingredients Database.
- **EU**: Permitted cosmetic ingredient. Hydroxyanthracene derivatives (aloin) restricted in food supplements by EU Regulation 2021/468.
- **IASC**: International Aloe Science Council provides voluntary certification for quality and purity.
- **Germany Commission E**: Approved aloe gel for topical treatment of minor wounds and burns.
