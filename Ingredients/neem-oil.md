# Neem Oil (Cold-Pressed)

> A vegetable oil pressed from the seeds of the neem tree (*Azadirachta indica*) containing azadirachtin, a powerful compound that disrupts insect feeding, mating, and molting, while also exhibiting broad-spectrum antifungal properties for garden use.

## Overview

The neem tree (*Azadirachta indica*) is a tropical evergreen native to the Indian subcontinent that has been called "the village pharmacy" in India for millennia. Every part of the tree — leaves, bark, seeds, flowers, and roots — has been used in traditional Ayurvedic and Unani medicine for over 4,000 years. The tree is remarkably pest-resistant itself, a clue to the powerful bioactive compounds concentrated in its seeds.

Neem oil is pressed from the tree's seeds (technically the kernels within the fruit). Its primary active compound, **azadirachtin**, is a complex limonoid (tetranortriterpenoid) that has become one of the most important botanical pesticides in the world. Azadirachtin works by mimicking insect hormones, disrupting the endocrine system of pest insects and interfering with feeding, molting, mating, and egg-laying — without directly killing beneficial insects when applied correctly.

In the DIY context, neem oil is the go-to botanical pesticide and fungicide for home gardens. A simple emulsion of neem oil, liquid soap (emulsifier), and water provides effective control of aphids, whiteflies, mites, caterpillars, powdery mildew, black spot, and rust — all without synthetic chemicals. The key to its selectivity: neem works through ingestion and systemic disruption, not contact toxicity, so beneficial insects that don't feed on treated plant tissue (pollinators, predatory insects) are largely unaffected.

### Azadirachtin Structure

Azadirachtin is one of the most complex natural insecticidal compounds known:
- Tetranortriterpenoid (modified triterpene)
- 16 stereocenters
- Extremely difficult to synthesize (first total synthesis achieved in 2007 by Ley et al. after 22 years of work)
- Structural similarity to insect ecdysteroids (molting hormones) is the basis for its bioactivity

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Cold-Press Extraction

1. **Fruit collection**: Ripe neem fruits are gathered (fruits resemble small olives)
2. **Depulping**: The fleshy outer fruit is removed to expose the seed kernel
3. **Drying**: Kernels are sun-dried to reduce moisture to ~10%
4. **Pressing**: Dried kernels are fed through an expeller press at ambient or low temperature
5. **Filtration**: Crude oil is filtered to remove seed particles
6. **Yield**: Seeds contain 40–50% oil by weight

Cold-pressed neem oil retains the highest azadirachtin content and full complement of bioactive limonoids.

### Solvent Extraction

- Hexane extraction recovers additional oil from pressed seed cake
- Produces higher yields but may have lower azadirachtin content
- Often followed by refining (bleaching, deodorizing)

### Clarified Hydrophobic Extract

- Commercial neem pesticide products often use "clarified hydrophobic extract of neem oil"
- This is neem oil with azadirachtin removed (sold separately as a concentrated pesticide)
- The remaining oil still contains other limonoids and fatty acids with pesticidal activity

### Growing Neem

- Tropical/subtropical tree; cannot tolerate frost
- Grows in USDA zones 10–12
- Fast-growing; begins producing fruit at 3–5 years
- Extremely drought-tolerant once established
- Not practical for most temperate-climate gardeners — purchase oil instead

## Mechanism of Action

### Insecticidal (Azadirachtin)

Azadirachtin operates through multiple mechanisms simultaneously:

1. **Anti-feedant effect**: Azadirachtin triggers sensory receptors in insect mouthparts that signal "unpalatable," causing insects to stop feeding on treated plants. This is often the first and fastest-acting mechanism.

2. **Insect growth regulation (IGR)**: Azadirachtin blocks the release and action of ecdysone (the insect molting hormone):
   - Disrupts molting — larvae cannot shed their exoskeleton and die
   - Disrupts metamorphosis — pupae fail to develop into adults
   - This is a slow-acting mechanism (days to weeks)

3. **Reproductive disruption**: 
   - Reduces fecundity (egg production) in females
   - Reduces fertility (egg viability)
   - Disrupts mating behavior

4. **Oviposition deterrent**: Treated surfaces discourage egg-laying

### Selectivity

- Azadirachtin works primarily through **ingestion** and **systemic disruption**, not contact toxicity
- Beneficial insects (pollinators, predatory insects, parasitoid wasps) that don't feed on treated plant tissue are largely unaffected
- **Critical**: Apply in evening when pollinators are not active, as wet spray can still harm bees on direct contact

### Antifungal

- Neem oil's fatty acids and limonoids disrupt fungal cell membranes
- Effective against powdery mildew (*Erysiphe* spp.), black spot (*Diplocarpon rosae*), rust (*Puccinia* spp.), and other common garden fungi
- Both preventive and mildly curative

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Garden Pest Spray (Primary Use)**:
- 1 tsp cold-pressed neem oil
- ½ tsp liquid soap (emulsifier — Dr. Bronner's or dish soap)
- 1 quart warm water
- Shake vigorously to emulsify
- Spray all leaf surfaces (top and bottom)
- Apply in evening
- Weekly for prevention; every 3–4 days for active infestations

**Fungicide Spray**:
- Same recipe as above; double neem concentration for fungal issues
- Effective against powdery mildew, black spot, rust, anthracnose
- Best as preventive — apply before disease appears

**Dormant Spray (Fruit Trees)**:
- 2 tsp neem oil per quart warm water + soap emulsifier
- Apply in late winter/early spring before bud break
- Smothers overwintering insect eggs and fungal spores on bark

**Soil Drench (Systemic)**:
- Neem oil solution watered into soil at plant base
- Plant roots absorb azadirachtin, making the entire plant unpalatable to feeding insects
- Particularly effective for houseplants with persistent pest issues

**Indoor Plant Treatment**:
- Half concentration (½ tsp per quart)
- Wipe or spray on leaves
- Effective against spider mites, fungus gnats, mealybugs, scale

### Traditional/Medicinal Uses

- **Ayurvedic medicine**: Skin conditions, wound healing, digestive ailments, fever
- **Dental**: Neem twigs used as toothbrushes (antibacterial)
- **Skin care**: Neem-based soaps and creams for eczema, psoriasis, acne (traditional, some evidence)
- **Note**: These uses are outside the scope of this DIY garden-focused guide

## Efficacy & Research

### Pest Control

- Mordue (Luntz), A.J. & Blackwell, A. (1993) in *Journal of Insect Physiology* provided a comprehensive review of azadirachtin's mode of action, confirming its anti-feedant, growth-regulatory, and reproductive effects across over 400 insect species.

### Selectivity

- Schmutterer, H. (1990) in *Annual Review of Entomology* reviewed neem's effects on non-target organisms, finding that properly applied neem oil had minimal impact on beneficial insects, earthworms, and vertebrates.

### Fungicidal Activity

- Coventry, E. & Allan, E.J. (2001) in *Crop Protection* demonstrated that neem extracts provided effective control of several plant pathogenic fungi in greenhouse and field trials.

### EPA Registration

- Neem oil and azadirachtin are registered by the U.S. EPA as biopesticides with reduced-risk status, reflecting their favorable toxicological and environmental profiles.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Application Timing

- **Apply in evening ONLY**: Wet neem spray can harm bees and other pollinators on direct contact. Once dried (within hours), treated surfaces are safe for pollinators.
- Avoid spraying open flowers directly

### Human Safety

- Low mammalian toxicity (azadirachtin oral LD₅₀ in rats: >5,000 mg/kg)
- May cause skin irritation in sensitive individuals — wear gloves when handling concentrate
- Avoid eye contact; rinse with water if contact occurs
- Not for internal use (traditional internal uses exist but are outside scope)

### Strong Odor

- Neem oil has a strong garlic/sulfur smell that many find unpleasant
- Odor dissipates within 24–48 hours after application
- Indoor use: be prepared for the smell; ventilate well

### Environmental

- Biodegradable — azadirachtin breaks down in UV light within hours to days
- This means it does not persist in the environment (advantage for safety, disadvantage for longevity — reapplication needed)
- Low toxicity to fish at normal application rates, but avoid spraying directly into water bodies
- Toxic to aquatic invertebrates at higher concentrations — keep out of waterways

### Plant Sensitivity

- Some plants (particularly seedlings, stressed plants, and certain sensitive species) may show phytotoxicity
- Test on a few leaves first before full application
- Do not apply in intense heat (>90°F) or direct sun — can cause leaf burn
- Reduce concentration for delicate plants

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Effective against wide range of pest insects (400+ species documented)
- Selectively targets plant-feeding pests when applied correctly
- Antifungal as well as insecticidal (two-in-one)
- Insects cannot develop resistance easily (multiple modes of action)
- Biodegradable — breaks down quickly in environment
- Low mammalian toxicity
- EPA-registered biopesticide
- OMRI-listed for organic gardening
- One ingredient replaces multiple pesticide and fungicide products

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Slower than synthetic pesticides — days to weeks, not hours
- Must reapply after rain (washes off)
- Degrades in UV light — short residual activity
- Strong, unpleasant odor
- Must emulsify with soap (doesn't mix with water alone)
- Can harm pollinators if sprayed directly — evening application critical
- Less effective on severe, established infestations
- Must be mixed fresh (emulsion destabilizes over days)
- Cold-pressed oil solidifies in cool temperatures

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: 1–2 years (cold-pressed, sealed); azadirachtin degrades over time
- **Storage**: Cool, dark place. Refrigeration extends shelf life.
- **Container**: Original bottle; dark glass preferred
- **Solidification**: Neem oil may solidify or become very thick below ~25°C. Warm gently in a bowl of warm water before use — do not microwave.
- **Mixed spray**: Use within 8 hours of mixing. Azadirachtin degrades rapidly once emulsified and exposed to light.

- **Cold-pressed**: Essential — retains azadirachtin. Solvent-extracted or refined oils may have reduced bioactivity.
- **Color**: Dark yellow to brown. Very light oil may be refined/depleted.
- **Odor**: Strong garlic/sulfur smell indicates active compounds present
- **Azadirachtin content**: Premium products may list azadirachtin percentage (1,500–3,000 ppm is good)
- **OMRI listed**: Indicates approved for organic gardening

## Regulatory Status

- **EPA (USA)**: Registered biopesticide (reduced-risk classification). Both neem oil and azadirachtin are registered active ingredients.
- **OMRI**: Listed for organic crop production.
- **EU**: Azadirachtin is approved as a plant protection active substance. Some neem oil products are restricted.
- **India**: Widely used and produced; regulated under the Insecticides Act.

## Related Ingredients

- **Diatomaceous Earth** — Physical pest control (abrasion/desiccation) vs. neem's biochemical approach. Complementary: DE for crawling pests indoors, neem for garden pests.
- **Castile Soap** — Used as emulsifier to mix neem oil with water. Essential component of neem spray.
- **Insecticidal Soap** — Soapy water alone suffocates soft-bodied insects (aphids) on contact. Neem adds systemic and growth-regulatory effects.
- **Pyrethrin** — Another botanical insecticide (from chrysanthemum). Faster-acting but less selective than neem. More toxic to beneficials.
