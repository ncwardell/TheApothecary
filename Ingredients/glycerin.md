# Glycerin (Glycerol)

> A simple three-carbon polyol that is the most effective humectant available for DIY formulations — drawing moisture from the environment and deeper skin layers to hydrate the stratum corneum, while also serving as a solvent, preservative booster, and emollient in virtually every category of personal care product.

| pH (in solution) | ~7.0 (neutral) |
| Solubility | Fully miscible with water and ethanol; insoluble in oils, chloroform, ether |

## Overview

Glycerin is arguably the single most important humectant in personal care chemistry. Its three hydroxyl groups (-OH) give it an extraordinary capacity to hydrogen-bond with water molecules, allowing it to absorb moisture from the atmosphere and hold it at the skin surface. This mechanism is fundamentally different from occlusive moisturizers (like petroleum jelly or beeswax) that simply trap existing moisture — glycerin actively recruits water.

In the context of a rigorous DIY formulation system, glycerin fills a critical gap: no other ingredient in the repository provides true humectancy. Oils and butters (coconut oil, shea butter, jojoba oil) are emollients and occlusives. Beeswax is a barrier. But none of them draw water to the skin. Glycerin does, and does so better than any other commonly available humectant (including hyaluronic acid on a cost-effectiveness basis for DIY use).

Glycerin is a byproduct of soap-making (saponification). When fats/oils react with lye (NaOH or KOH), the triglyceride molecules split into fatty acid salts (soap) and glycerol. Commercial soap manufacturers typically remove the glycerin for sale as a separate commodity. DIY soap-makers who use the cold-process method retain all the glycerin in their soap, which is one reason handmade soap feels more moisturizing than commercial soap.

Beyond skin care, glycerin serves as a solvent for herbal tinctures (glycerites), a sweetener and humectant in toothpaste, a preservative booster (by reducing water activity), and a base for numerous pharmaceutical preparations.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Molecular Structure

- Three-carbon backbone with three hydroxyl (-OH) groups
- Each -OH group can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules
- This gives glycerin its hygroscopic (water-attracting) properties
- The molecule is small enough (92 g/mol) to penetrate into the upper layers of the stratum corneum

### Hygroscopic Properties

| Relative Humidity | Glycerin Behavior |
|---|---|
| >65% RH | Absorbs moisture from air — actively hydrates skin |
| 40–65% RH | Maintains equilibrium — holds existing moisture |
| <40% RH | May draw moisture from deeper skin layers — use with occlusive |

This humidity dependence is critical for DIY formulators: in dry climates or winter conditions, glycerin should always be paired with an occlusive ingredient (beeswax, shea butter, coconut oil) to prevent it from pulling moisture out of the skin rather than from the air.

### Physical Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Boiling point | 290°C (554°F) |
| Melting point | 17.8°C (64°F) |
| Density | 1.261 g/cm³ |
| Viscosity | 1.412 Pa·s at 20°C (very viscous) |
| Refractive index | 1.4746 |
| Water activity depression | Significant — reduces aW, inhibiting microbial growth |
| Caloric value | 4.32 kcal/g (FDA-approved food additive) |

### Grades

| Grade | Purity | Source | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| USP/Food grade | ≥99.7% | Vegetable oils (palm, coconut, soy) | Cosmetics, food, DIY |
| Technical grade | 95–99% | Various | Industrial only |
| Crude glycerin | 40–80% | Biodiesel byproduct | Not for personal care |

For DIY use, always purchase **USP-grade vegetable glycerin**.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

**Saponification byproduct (traditional)**:
1. Fats/oils + NaOH → soap + glycerol
2. Glycerol is separated from soap, purified by distillation
3. This was the primary source for centuries

**Transesterification byproduct (modern)**:
1. Biodiesel production: triglycerides + methanol → fatty acid methyl esters (biodiesel) + glycerol
2. Crude glycerol is purified to USP grade
3. Now the dominant source — biodiesel industry produces excess glycerol

**Synthetic (petrochemical)**:
1. Propylene → allyl chloride → epichlorohydrin → glycerol
2. Rarely used now due to abundant bio-based supply

### Home Production

Glycerin is naturally produced during cold-process soap-making. However, extracting pure glycerin from soap requires distillation equipment not practical for home use. Purchase USP-grade vegetable glycerin — it is inexpensive and widely available.

## Mechanism of Action

### Humectancy (Primary Mechanism)

1. Glycerin applied to skin penetrates into the upper stratum corneum
2. Each glycerin molecule hydrogen-bonds with multiple water molecules
3. Water is drawn from two sources:
   - **From the atmosphere** (when RH >65%): glycerin absorbs ambient moisture
   - **From the dermis** (transepidermal): glycerin attracts water from deeper skin layers to the surface
4. The net effect is increased hydration of the stratum corneum
5. Hydrated stratum corneum is more flexible, smoother, and has improved barrier function

### Skin Barrier Enhancement

1. Glycerin modulates aquaporin-3 (AQP3) channels in keratinocytes
2. AQP3 channels transport both water and glycerol across cell membranes
3. Glycerin supplementation increases AQP3 expression, improving the skin's intrinsic moisture-handling capacity
4. Long-term glycerin use improves skin barrier function beyond simple surface hydration

### Water Activity Reduction (Preservation)

1. Glycerin at >30% concentration significantly reduces water activity (aW) in formulations
2. Lower aW inhibits microbial growth (bacteria, mold, yeast)
3. This makes glycerin a useful preservative booster (though not a standalone preservative)
4. At 50%+ concentration, glycerin alone can prevent microbial growth in some formulations

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Moisturizing Lotion/Cream**:
- 5–10% glycerin in water phase
- Combine with shea butter or coconut oil (occlusive) and an emulsifier
- The humectant + occlusive combination is the gold standard for effective moisturizing

**Glycerin & Rosewater Toner**:
- 1 part glycerin to 3 parts rosewater (or distilled water)
- Apply to damp skin after cleansing
- Simple, effective hydrating toner

**Toothpaste Humectant**:
- Glycerin prevents DIY toothpaste from drying out
- Add 1–2 tsp per batch
- Also provides slight sweetness

**Herbal Glycerite (Alcohol-Free Tincture)**:
- Fill jar with dried herbs, cover with vegetable glycerin (or 60:40 glycerin:water)
- Steep 4–6 weeks, shaking daily
- Strain — produces alcohol-free herbal extract
- Good for: elderberry, echinacea, chamomile, lemon balm

**Soap Additive**:
- Add 1 tbsp glycerin per pound of soap base for extra moisturizing
- Already present naturally in cold-process soap
- Melt-and-pour soap bases are glycerin-rich

**Hair Moisturizer**:
- 5–10% glycerin in water-based hair spritz
- Hydrates curly/coily hair without weighing it down
- Adjust concentration based on humidity (less in dry climates)

**Lip Balm Additive**:
- Add small amount (5%) to lip balm for humectancy
- Must be combined with beeswax/oils since glycerin is water-soluble

**Preservative Booster**:
- Adding 10–20% glycerin to water-based formulations extends shelf life
- Reduces water activity, making environment less hospitable to microbes
- Not a substitute for proper preservation in water-containing products

### What It Replaces

- Propylene glycol (synthetic humectant)
- Butylene glycol
- Sorbitol (less effective humectant)
- Hyaluronic acid serums (for basic hydration needs — HA is superior for targeted facial care but glycerin is more versatile and cost-effective)

## Efficacy & Research

### Skin Hydration

- Fluhr, J.W., Darlenski, R., & Surber, C. (2008) in *British Journal of Dermatology* provided a comprehensive review demonstrating that glycerin is the most effective low-molecular-weight humectant for skin hydration, with evidence of both immediate and long-term barrier improvement.

### Aquaporin-3 Modulation

- Hara, M. & Bhilladvala, R.J. (2005) in *Journal of Biological Chemistry* demonstrated that glycerol is transported through aquaporin-3 channels in human keratinocytes, and that AQP3-mediated glycerol transport is essential for stratum corneum hydration.

### Wound Healing

- Fluhr, J.W. et al. (2012) in *British Journal of Dermatology* showed that glycerol-containing formulations accelerated wound healing in a mouse model through AQP3-mediated mechanisms, supporting glycerin's role beyond simple hydration.

### Antimicrobial Preservation

- Glycerin at concentrations above 50% effectively inhibits most bacterial and fungal growth through osmotic stress and water activity reduction, as documented in pharmaceutical preservation literature (Rowe, Sheskey, & Quinn, *Handbook of Pharmaceutical Excipients*).

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Topical Safety

- Glycerin has an exceptionally long safety record in topical use
- FDA GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) for food and cosmetic use
- Non-toxic, non-irritating, non-sensitizing
- Safe for all skin types including babies and sensitive skin
- Non-comedogenic

### Humidity Caveat

- In very dry environments (<40% RH), glycerin can draw moisture FROM the skin rather than from the air
- **Always combine glycerin with an occlusive** (beeswax, shea butter, oils) in dry climates
- In humid environments, glycerin is maximally effective as a standalone humectant
- Recommended concentration: 5–10% in leave-on products (higher concentrations feel sticky)

### Ingestion

- Glycerin is safe to ingest in normal quantities (FDA GRAS food additive)
- Used in food products, medications, and dietary supplements
- Excessive ingestion may cause mild GI distress (osmotic laxative effect)

### Formulation Notes

- Glycerin is water-soluble, not oil-soluble — it must go in the water phase of emulsions
- High concentrations (>20%) feel sticky on skin
- In very dry conditions, reduce concentration or ensure occlusive partner

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Most effective widely-available humectant
- Actively draws moisture to skin (not just trapping it)
- Modulates aquaporin-3 for long-term barrier improvement
- Completely non-toxic, non-irritating
- FDA GRAS for food and cosmetic use
- Inexpensive and widely available
- Multi-functional: humectant, solvent, sweetener, preservative booster
- Fully miscible with water — easy to formulate
- Byproduct of soap-making (inherently sustainable)
- Works synergistically with occlusives for superior moisturizing

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Can dehydrate skin in very dry climates (<40% RH) if used without occlusive
- Sticky at high concentrations (>20%)
- Water-soluble only — cannot be added directly to oil-only formulations
- Not a standalone preservative (booster only)
- Attracts dust/dirt if over-applied
- Different grades exist — must use USP/food grade for personal care

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: 2–3 years (USP grade, sealed); indefinite if kept sealed and dry
- **Storage**: Room temperature, sealed container, away from direct sunlight
- **Hygroscopic**: Will absorb moisture from air if left open — keep sealed
- **Stability**: Chemically stable; does not oxidize or go rancid
- **Freezing**: May crystallize below 17.8°C; returns to liquid at room temperature without damage
