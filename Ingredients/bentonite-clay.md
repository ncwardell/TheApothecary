# Bentonite Clay

> A naturally occurring montmorillonite mineral clay with a powerful negative ionic charge, capable of binding positively-charged toxins, heavy metals, and bacteria, widely used in oral care, skin treatments, and detoxification formulations.

## Overview

Bentonite clay is a type of absorbent smectite clay formed from the weathering of volcanic ash, primarily composed of the mineral montmorillonite. The name derives from Fort Benton, Wyoming, where large deposits were first commercially exploited in the 19th century. Bentonite deposits are found worldwide, with major sources in the United States (Wyoming, Montana), India, China, Greece, and Turkey.

The defining characteristic of bentonite is its remarkable ability to swell when hydrated and to generate a strong negative electrical charge. This charge allows bentonite particles to attract and bind positively charged molecules — including heavy metal ions, bacteria, toxins, and other impurities — making it an effective adsorbent in both industrial and personal care applications.

There are two primary types: **sodium bentonite** (higher swelling capacity, used industrially and for detox applications) and **calcium bentonite** (lower swelling, preferred for oral care due to its calcium content and gentler action). In the DIY personal care world, bentonite clay is a foundational ingredient for face masks, toothpaste, poultices, and detox baths.

Indigenous peoples across the Americas, Africa, and Asia have used clay internally and externally for medicinal purposes for thousands of years — a practice known as geophagy. The Aztecs reportedly used montmorillonite clays for wound healing and digestive complaints. Modern research has begun to validate some of these traditional uses, particularly in the areas of toxin binding and skin care.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Layered Silicate Structure

Bentonite's key properties arise from its layered crystal structure:

- **Tetrahedral sheets**: Silicon-oxygen tetrahedra forming a planar network
- **Octahedral sheets**: Aluminum (or magnesium) coordinated with oxygen and hydroxyl groups
- **2:1 layer structure**: Two tetrahedral sheets sandwiching one octahedral sheet (T-O-T)
- **Interlayer space**: The gap between T-O-T layers where water molecules and exchangeable cations (Na⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) reside

### Isomorphous Substitution

Within the crystal lattice, aluminum (Al³⁺) in the octahedral layer is partially replaced by magnesium (Mg²⁺) or iron (Fe²⁺), and silicon (Si⁴⁺) in the tetrahedral layer may be partially replaced by aluminum (Al³⁺). These substitutions create a net negative charge on the clay layers, which is balanced by the exchangeable cations in the interlayer space.

### Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)

Bentonite has one of the highest cation exchange capacities of any natural material:
- **Sodium bentonite**: 80–120 meq/100g
- **Calcium bentonite**: 40–80 meq/100g

This means bentonite can exchange its interlayer cations for other positively charged species in solution — the basis for its detoxification and purification abilities.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Geological Formation

Bentonite forms through the chemical alteration of volcanic ash (tuff) over millions of years:

1. Volcanic eruptions deposit layers of silica-rich ash
2. Groundwater percolates through the ash deposits
3. Over geological time, chemical weathering (devitrification and diagenesis) transforms the volcanic glass into montmorillonite clay minerals
4. The type of bentonite formed depends on the chemistry of the groundwater — sodium-rich water produces sodium bentonite; calcium-rich water produces calcium bentonite

### Mining and Processing

1. **Open-pit mining**: Bentonite deposits are typically near the surface. Overburden is removed and clay is extracted.
2. **Drying**: Raw clay is spread in drying yards or processed through rotary dryers to reduce moisture from ~30% to ~5–10%.
3. **Crushing and grinding**: Dried clay is crushed and ground to the desired particle size. Ball mills or roller mills produce fine powder.
4. **Screening/classification**: Ground clay is classified by particle size. Cosmetic-grade bentonite is typically 200-mesh or finer.
5. **Quality testing**: Testing for CEC, swelling index, mineral composition, heavy metal content, and microbial contamination.

### Grades

| Grade | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Cosmetic/pharmaceutical | Tested for heavy metals, microbials; fine particle size | Face masks, toothpaste, poultices |
| Food-grade | Meets FDA/GRAS requirements | Internal use (with caution), animal feed |
| Industrial | Untested for contaminants; may contain additives | Drilling mud, cat litter, sealing |

### Purchasing Notes

- Always purchase **food-grade** or **cosmetic-grade** bentonite for personal care use
- Avoid industrial-grade bentonite, which may contain harmful levels of heavy metals or contaminants
- Calcium bentonite is preferred for oral care; sodium bentonite for masks and detox baths
- Available as fine powder in sealed bags or jars

## Mechanism of Action

### Adsorption (Ionic Binding)

The primary mechanism of bentonite clay is **adsorption** — the binding of molecules to its surface through electrostatic attraction:

1. In aqueous suspension, the clay platelets carry a permanent negative charge on their faces
2. Positively charged species (heavy metal cations, bacterial cell walls, organic toxins) are attracted to and bind onto the clay surface
3. The enormous surface area (700–800 m²/g) provides vast binding capacity
4. This is a physical adsorption process (electrostatic), not a chemical reaction

### Absorption (Swelling)

Sodium bentonite absorbs water into its interlayer spaces, swelling dramatically. This:
- Creates a drawing/pulling action when applied to skin (poultice effect)
- Physically traps impurities within the expanded clay matrix
- Provides the characteristic tightening sensation of clay face masks

### Mineral Delivery

When applied topically or used in oral care, bentonite releases trace minerals:
- **Calcium** (from calcium bentonite): Supports enamel remineralization
- **Silica**: Supports skin and connective tissue health
- **Magnesium**: Supports skin barrier function
- **Iron**: Trace amounts contribute to skin health

### Antibacterial Action

Certain bentonite clays have demonstrated direct antibacterial activity:
- Physical disruption of bacterial cell membranes through electrostatic interaction
- Iron-bearing bentonites generate reactive oxygen species that damage bacterial cells
- Haydel et al. (2008) demonstrated broad-spectrum antibacterial activity of specific bentonite clays against MRSA and other pathogens

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Remineralizing Toothpaste (Primary Use)**:
- 3 tbsp bentonite clay + 1 tbsp baking soda + ½ tsp sea salt + 2 tbsp coconut oil + essential oils
- Clay binds bacteria and delivers minerals to teeth
- Must use non-metal utensils and containers (metal disrupts ionic charge)
- Store in glass jar

**Face Mask**:
- Mix bentonite clay with equal parts water or apple cider vinegar to form a paste
- Apply to face, leave 10–20 minutes until dry and tightening
- Rinse with warm water
- Draws out impurities, excess oil, and dead skin cells
- Weekly use recommended for oily/acne-prone skin

**Detox Bath**:
- Add ¼–½ cup bentonite clay to warm bath water
- Soak 20–30 minutes
- Clay binds to impurities on skin surface

**Poultice (Topical)**:
- Mix clay with water to form thick paste
- Apply to insect bites, stings, minor rashes, or poison ivy
- Cover with damp cloth; leave 1–2 hours
- Drawing action helps reduce itching and swelling

**Armpit Detox**:
- Mix 1 tbsp bentonite clay + 1 tsp ACV + water to form paste
- Apply to underarms for 15 minutes when transitioning from commercial to natural deodorant
- Helps draw out residual antiperspirant chemicals and bacteria

### Industrial Uses

- **Drilling mud**: Bentonite is the primary component of drilling fluids in oil and gas exploration
- **Cat litter**: Clumping cat litter is sodium bentonite
- **Construction**: Waterproofing, soil sealing, foundation support (slurry walls)
- **Wine/beer clarification**: Fining agent that removes proteins and tannins
- **Paper manufacturing**: Filler and coating agent
- **Iron ore pelletization**: Binder in iron ore processing

### Historical Uses

- **Geophagy**: Deliberate clay consumption practiced for millennia across cultures for digestive relief and detoxification
- **Aztec medicine**: Used externally for wounds and internally for digestive ailments
- **African traditional medicine**: Clay consumption by pregnant women (providing minerals and binding dietary toxins)
- **Native American use**: Various tribes used local clays for wound healing, skin conditions, and ceremonial purposes

## Efficacy & Research

### Antibacterial Activity

- Haydel, S.E. et al. (2008) in *Journal of Antimicrobial Chemotherapy* demonstrated that specific natural clays (including montmorillonite-rich samples) had broad-spectrum antibacterial activity against MRSA, *E. coli*, and other pathogens. The mechanism involved iron-mediated oxidative stress.

### Toxin Binding

- Phillips, T.D. et al. (2008) in *Food Additives & Contaminants* showed that montmorillonite clay effectively bound aflatoxin in the gastrointestinal tract, reducing bioavailability by >90% in animal studies. This has led to FDA-approved use of bentonite as an aflatoxin adsorbent in animal feed.

### Skin Effects

- Moosavi, M. (2017) in *Iranian Journal of Public Health* reviewed the therapeutic use of clays in dermatology, noting bentonite's effectiveness in reducing skin inflammation, absorbing excess sebum, and improving acne symptoms.

### Oral Care

- Limited clinical studies specifically on bentonite in toothpaste. The rationale is extrapolated from its established antibacterial, mineral-delivery, and adsorption properties.

### Heavy Metal Binding

- Oyanedel-Craver, V.A. and Smith, J.A. (2006) demonstrated effective binding of lead, cadmium, and other heavy metals by montmorillonite in water treatment applications.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Critical Handling Rule

- **Never use metal utensils or containers** with bentonite clay — metal contact is widely claimed to disrupt the clay's ionic charge. While the scientific basis for brief metal contact significantly reducing CEC is debated, using non-metal implements (wood, ceramic, glass, plastic) is standard practice and eliminates any concern.

### Internal Use Caution

- Internal consumption of bentonite is controversial. While practiced traditionally, there are concerns about:
  - **Lead contamination**: Some commercial bentonite products have been found to contain elevated lead levels. FDA has issued warnings about specific brands.
  - **Nutrient binding**: Clay may bind beneficial minerals and medications alongside toxins
  - **Intestinal obstruction**: Excessive consumption could theoretically cause blockages
- If consuming internally, use only products specifically tested and labeled for internal use

### Dust Inhalation

- Fine clay dust can irritate lungs. Avoid breathing in dry bentonite powder.
- Mix in well-ventilated areas. Consider a dust mask when handling dry powder in quantity.

### Drain Clogging

- Bentonite swells in water and can clog drains. Spit toothpaste into trash or toilet, not the sink.

### Drug Interactions

- Bentonite may bind oral medications, reducing their absorption. Do not take internally within 2 hours of medications.

### Pregnancy

- Internal use is not recommended during pregnancy without medical supervision due to potential lead content and mineral binding.

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Extremely powerful adsorbent for toxins, heavy metals, and bacteria
- Rich in beneficial minerals (calcium, silica, magnesium)
- Non-toxic and natural when cosmetic/food-grade
- Versatile — oral care, skin care, detox, poultice
- Long shelf life (indefinite when stored dry)
- Inexpensive per use
- Gentle enough for sensitive skin when used properly
- No synthetic chemicals or preservatives needed

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Must avoid metal utensils (inconvenient)
- Can clog drains
- Potential lead contamination in some products (source carefully)
- Messy to work with
- Dries skin if overused (limit face masks to 1–2x/week)
- Internal use controversial and potentially risky
- Very alkaline pH may not suit all applications
- Can stain fabrics and surfaces

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Storage**: Cool, dry place in a sealed container. Protect from moisture.
- **Container**: Glass, ceramic, or food-grade plastic. Avoid metal containers.
- **Shelf life**: Indefinite when stored dry. Clay is a mineral — it does not spoil, oxidize, or degrade.
- **Moisture**: If bentonite absorbs ambient moisture, it will clump. Dry clumps can be broken up and used normally. Store with tight-fitting lid.
- **Mixed preparations**: Toothpaste made with bentonite lasts 1–2 months in a sealed glass jar. Face mask paste should be made fresh.

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: GRAS as a food additive for specific uses. FDA has issued warnings about some bentonite products with elevated lead levels.
- **EU**: Listed in CosIng database as a cosmetic ingredient (CI 77004). Permitted in cosmetic products.
- **USP**: Not monographed as a pharmaceutical ingredient, though kaolin (a related clay) is.
- **Industrial**: Widely regulated for use in drilling fluids, water treatment, and animal feed.

## Related Ingredients

- **Kaolin Clay** — Milder, less absorbent white clay. Better for sensitive/dry skin. Lower CEC.
- **French Green Clay (Illite)** — Similar absorptive properties, different mineral composition. Popular in European skin care.
- **Rhassoul Clay** — Moroccan clay with high silica and magnesium. Excellent for hair.
- **Diatomaceous Earth** — Silica-based but different mechanism (physical rather than ionic). Used for pest control.
- **Activated Charcoal** — Another powerful adsorbent, but works via van der Waals forces rather than ionic charge.
- **Baking Soda** — Often paired with bentonite in toothpaste; provides alkalinity and mild abrasion.
