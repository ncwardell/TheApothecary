# Hydrogen Peroxide (3%)
> A versatile oxidizing agent that breaks down to water and oxygen, used as an EPA-registered disinfectant, oxygen bleach, oral care agent, and general household cleaner with an excellent environmental profile.

## Overview

Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is the simplest peroxide -- a compound containing an oxygen-oxygen single bond. In its pure form, it is a pale blue, slightly viscous liquid that is a powerful oxidizer. The 3% aqueous solution commonly sold for household use is a versatile cleaning, disinfecting, and bleaching agent that owes its broad utility to its strong oxidizing properties and its environmentally benign decomposition products: water (H2O) and oxygen gas (O2).

Hydrogen peroxide was first prepared by Louis Jacques Thenard in 1818 by reacting barium peroxide with nitric acid. For much of the 19th century, it remained a laboratory curiosity due to difficulties in stabilization and storage. The development of industrial production methods in the early 20th century, particularly the anthraquinone auto-oxidation process developed by BASF in the 1930s-1940s, made large-scale production economical. Today, global production exceeds 5 million tonnes per year (expressed as 100% H2O2), with major applications in pulp and paper bleaching, textile processing, chemical synthesis, water treatment, and environmental remediation.

For household and DIY use, 3% hydrogen peroxide is the standard concentration. It is registered by the EPA as a surface disinfectant effective against bacteria, viruses, yeasts, and bacterial spores. Its ability to decompose completely to water and oxygen makes it one of the most environmentally friendly disinfectants and bleaches available. Higher concentrations (10%, 30%, 35%) are available for specific applications but pose significantly greater safety risks and are generally unnecessary for DIY formulations.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Molecular Structure

Hydrogen peroxide has a non-planar, open-book molecular geometry:

- **O-O bond length:** 147.4 pm (significantly longer than the O=O double bond in O2, 120.7 pm, reflecting the single-bond character)
- **O-H bond length:** 95.0 pm
- **O-O-H bond angle:** 94.8 degrees (gas phase)
- **Dihedral angle:** 111.5 degrees (the two O-H bonds are not coplanar, giving the molecule a skewed geometry resembling a partially open book)

The O-O single bond is relatively weak (bond dissociation energy approximately 213 kJ/mol compared to 498 kJ/mol for O=O in O2), which is the fundamental basis for hydrogen peroxide's reactivity. This bond cleaves homolytically to generate two hydroxyl radicals (HO.), which are among the most reactive oxidizing species known:

H2O2 -> 2 HO. (homolysis)

### Redox Chemistry

Hydrogen peroxide is a versatile redox agent. Uniquely, it can act as both an oxidizer and a reducer, depending on the reaction partner:

**As an oxidizer (more common in household applications):**
- H2O2 + 2H+ + 2e- -> 2H2O (E0 = +1.776 V in acidic solution)
- This is the mode of action in bleaching, disinfection, and stain removal

**As a reducer (against stronger oxidizers):**
- O2 + 2H+ + 2e- -> H2O2 (E0 = +0.695 V)
- H2O2 can be oxidized by permanganate, chlorine, ozone, and other strong oxidizers

### Decomposition

Hydrogen peroxide is thermodynamically unstable and spontaneously decomposes:

2H2O2 -> 2H2O + O2 (delta G = -119.2 kJ/mol per mole H2O2)

This decomposition is catalyzed by:
- **Transition metal ions:** Fe2+/Fe3+, Cu2+, Mn2+, MnO2 (via Fenton-type chemistry)
- **Enzymes:** Catalase (extremely efficient, one of the fastest known enzymes, turnover number approximately 40 million molecules/second), peroxidases
- **Alkali:** Decomposition accelerates at high pH
- **Light:** UV radiation accelerates decomposition (hence dark bottle storage)
- **Heat:** Decomposition rate approximately doubles per 10 degrees C increase
- **Particulate matter and rough surfaces:** Heterogeneous catalytic decomposition

### Fenton Chemistry

The Fenton reaction is particularly relevant to understanding H2O2's oxidizing power in biological and cleaning contexts:

Fe2+ + H2O2 -> Fe3+ + HO. + OH- (Fenton reaction)

The hydroxyl radical (HO.) produced is one of the strongest oxidants known (E0 = +2.80 V), capable of oxidizing virtually any organic molecule. This reaction is relevant to:
- Hydrogen peroxide's antimicrobial mechanism (interaction with intracellular iron)
- Its bleaching action on iron-stained surfaces
- The oxidative damage it can cause to biological tissues

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production: Anthraquinone Auto-Oxidation Process (AO Process)

More than 95% of the world's hydrogen peroxide is produced by the anthraquinone auto-oxidation process:

1. **Hydrogenation:** 2-Ethylanthraquinone (EAQ) dissolved in a mixed organic solvent is catalytically hydrogenated over palladium or nickel catalyst:
   EAQ + H2 -> EAQH2 (2-ethylanthrahydroquinone)

2. **Oxidation:** The hydrogenated working solution is exposed to air (oxygen):
   EAQH2 + O2 -> EAQ + H2O2

3. **Extraction:** Hydrogen peroxide is extracted from the organic phase into water (H2O2 is much more soluble in water than in the organic solvent).

4. **Concentration:** The aqueous H2O2 solution (typically 30-40%) is concentrated by vacuum distillation to the desired strength (3%, 30%, 50%, or 70%).

5. **Stabilization:** Stabilizers are added to retard decomposition:
   - Stannate (sodium stannate, Na2SnO3): 25-50 ppm
   - Phosphoric acid or phosphate esters: sequester metal catalysts
   - Acetanilide (in older formulations)

The anthraquinone is recycled continuously, making this process catalytic in the organic working compound. The only net inputs are hydrogen and oxygen (from air), and the only net output is hydrogen peroxide.

### Electrochemical Production

Hydrogen peroxide can also be produced by the electrochemical reduction of oxygen:

O2 + 2H+ + 2e- -> H2O2

This method is gaining attention for on-site, on-demand H2O2 production (eliminating transportation of concentrated H2O2) and is commercially available for water treatment applications.

### Biological Production

Many living organisms produce hydrogen peroxide enzymatically:
- **Oxidase enzymes** (glucose oxidase, xanthine oxidase, monoamine oxidase) produce H2O2 as a byproduct of substrate oxidation
- **NADPH oxidase** in immune cells (neutrophils, macrophages) deliberately produces superoxide, which is converted to H2O2 by superoxide dismutase, as part of the oxidative burst used to kill pathogens
- **Peroxisomes** in eukaryotic cells contain multiple H2O2-producing oxidases and catalase to decompose the H2O2 produced

### Home Production

Hydrogen peroxide cannot be practically or safely produced at home. Always purchase commercially produced and stabilized product. The 3% concentration available at pharmacies and grocery stores is appropriate for all household and DIY applications.

## Mechanism of Action

### Antimicrobial / Disinfectant Action

Hydrogen peroxide kills microorganisms through oxidative damage to multiple cellular targets:

1. **Membrane lipid peroxidation:** Hydroxyl radicals generated from H2O2 (via Fenton chemistry with intracellular iron) initiate chain reactions in unsaturated fatty acids of cell membranes, compromising membrane integrity and causing cell lysis.

2. **Protein oxidation:** HO. radicals oxidize amino acid residues (particularly methionine, cysteine, histidine, and tryptophan), inactivating essential enzymes, membrane transporters, and structural proteins.

3. **DNA damage:** Hydroxyl radicals cause single- and double-strand breaks, base modifications (8-oxoguanine formation), and DNA-protein crosslinks. At sufficient concentrations, DNA damage exceeds repair capacity, leading to cell death.

4. **Oxidation of thiol groups:** The oxidation of essential sulfhydryl (-SH) groups in enzymes and glutathione depletes cellular antioxidant defenses and inactivates critical metabolic enzymes.

At 3% concentration (approximately 0.88 M), hydrogen peroxide achieves adequate biocidal concentrations against:
- Vegetative bacteria: 1-5 minutes contact time
- Enveloped viruses (including SARS-CoV-2): 1 minute
- Yeasts and fungi: 5 minutes
- Bacterial endospores: 6-10 minutes or longer (higher concentrations more effective)

### Bleaching Mechanism

Hydrogen peroxide bleaches through oxidative decolorization:

1. **Chromophore destruction:** Colored compounds (stains, dyes) contain conjugated double-bond systems (chromophores) that absorb visible light. H2O2 oxidatively cleaves these conjugated systems, breaking the chromophore into smaller, non-conjugated fragments that do not absorb visible light.

2. **Specificity:** H2O2 bleaching is generally gentler than chlorine bleaching because:
   - H2O2 is a weaker oxidizer than hypochlorite (OCl-)
   - The oxidation mechanism tends to cleave bonds within the chromophore rather than non-specifically chlorinating the substrate
   - This makes it "color-safe" for most dyed fabrics (hence "oxygen bleach" or "color-safe bleach")

3. **pH dependence:** Bleaching activity is enhanced at alkaline pH because the perhydroxyl anion (HOO-) is a more effective bleaching agent than molecular H2O2. Sodium percarbonate (Na2CO3 . 1.5H2O2) leverages this by providing both H2O2 and an alkaline environment upon dissolution.

### Oral Care Mechanism

In oral care (tooth whitening, mouth rinse):

1. **Tooth whitening:** H2O2 diffuses through the enamel into the dentin, where it oxidizes chromogenic (colored) compounds trapped in the organic matrix. The small molecular size of H2O2 (MW 34) allows it to penetrate tooth structure effectively.

2. **Antimicrobial (oral):** Kills anaerobic bacteria in the oral cavity. The catalase-negative anaerobic bacteria responsible for periodontal disease and halitosis are particularly susceptible because they lack the enzymatic defenses (catalase) to detoxify H2O2.

3. **Mechanical debridement:** The effervescence (oxygen gas release) when H2O2 contacts blood, wound tissue, or catalase-containing bacteria provides a mild mechanical cleansing action that helps dislodge debris.

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Surface Disinfectant**
- Use 3% hydrogen peroxide directly (no dilution needed)
- Spray or pour onto surface; allow 1-10 minutes contact time
- Wipe clean; no rinsing required (decomposes to water and oxygen)
- Effective on kitchen counters, cutting boards, bathroom surfaces, and doorknobs

**Oxygen Bleach Laundry Booster**
- Add 1 cup of 3% H2O2 to the bleach dispenser of your washing machine
- Alternatively, soak white fabrics in a basin with 1 cup H2O2 per gallon of water for 30-60 minutes
- Safe for most colored fabrics (test an inconspicuous area first)
- Particularly effective on organic stains: blood, wine, coffee, grass

**Stain Remover (Specific Stains)**
- **Blood:** Apply 3% H2O2 directly to fresh blood stains; the effervescence lifts the stain. Blot and repeat. Most effective on fresh stains.
- **Wine/juice:** Blot excess, apply H2O2, allow to sit 5-10 minutes, blot
- **Underarm yellowing:** Mix equal parts H2O2 and baking soda into a paste; apply to the yellowed area, let sit 30 minutes, then launder

**Mouthwash / Oral Rinse**
- Dilute 3% H2O2 1:1 with water (producing a 1.5% solution)
- Swish for 30-60 seconds; do not swallow
- Spit and rinse with plain water
- Use 1-2 times daily for oral hygiene
- ADA-recognized concentration for over-the-counter oral care products is up to 3%

**Tooth Whitening Paste**
- Mix 2 tablespoons baking soda with 1 tablespoon 3% H2O2 to form a paste
- Brush teeth gently with the paste for 2 minutes
- Rinse thoroughly
- Use no more than 2-3 times per week to avoid enamel erosion
- Professional whitening products use much higher concentrations (10-40% carbamide peroxide or 3-15% H2O2)

**Produce Wash**
- Add 1/4 cup 3% H2O2 to a sink basin of cold water
- Soak fruits and vegetables for 5-10 minutes
- Rinse thoroughly with clean water
- Effective at reducing bacterial load (Sapers, G.M. et al., 2001)

**Mold and Mildew Remover**
- Spray 3% H2O2 directly onto mold or mildew on non-porous surfaces
- Allow 10-15 minutes contact time
- Scrub with a brush and wipe clean
- Repeat if necessary
- For porous materials (drywall, wood), H2O2 may bleach the surface but cannot fully eliminate deep mold growth

**Grout Cleaner**
- Apply 3% H2O2 directly to stained grout
- Allow to sit for 10-15 minutes
- For tough stains, make a paste of H2O2 and baking soda, apply, and let sit for 30 minutes
- Scrub with a stiff brush and rinse

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Pulp and paper:** Bleaching of wood pulp (replacing chlorine-based bleaching for environmental reasons) -- the single largest industrial use
- **Textile industry:** Bleaching of cotton, wool, silk, and synthetic fibers
- **Chemical synthesis:** Epoxidation of alkenes, oxidation of organic substrates, production of organic peroxides
- **Environmental remediation:** In-situ chemical oxidation (ISCO) of contaminated groundwater and soil
- **Electronics:** Cleaning of semiconductor wafers (piranha solution: H2SO4/H2O2 mixture)
- **Mining:** Cyanide destruction in gold mining wastewater
- **Food industry:** Aseptic packaging sterilization, bleaching of tripe and other food products
- **Aquaculture:** Water treatment and oxygenation in fish farming
- **Rocketry:** 85-98% H2O2 ("High Test Peroxide" or HTP) as a rocket propellant oxidizer

### Historical Uses

- **World War II:** Concentrated hydrogen peroxide (80-85%) was used as the oxidizer/monopropellant in German V-2 rocket turbopump systems and in torpedo propulsion (Walter turbine).
- **Early antiseptic:** 3% H2O2 was widely used as a wound antiseptic from the late 19th century through much of the 20th century. This practice has largely been abandoned by modern wound care guidelines, as H2O2 damages healthy tissue and delays healing.
- **Cosmetic bleaching:** Used for bleaching hair ("peroxide blonde") since the late 19th century.
- **Milk preservation:** In the early 20th century, H2O2 was illegally added to milk to extend shelf life before refrigeration became widespread.

## Efficacy & Research

**Disinfection Efficacy:**
Rutala, W.A. et al. (2008) published in the *American Journal of Infection Control* (36(10): S311-S328) a comprehensive review of disinfection and sterilization methods. Hydrogen peroxide at 3% concentration was confirmed effective against vegetative bacteria (including MRSA and VRE), fungi, and viruses with 1-10 minutes contact time.

**Against SARS-CoV-2:**
Kampf, G. et al. (2020) published in the *Journal of Hospital Infection* (104(3): 246-251) demonstrating that hydrogen peroxide at 0.5% concentration inactivated coronaviruses (including SARS and MERS) within 1 minute of contact. The standard 3% household concentration is well above this threshold.

**Oral Health:**
Marshall, M.V. et al. (1995) published in the *Journal of Periodontology* (66(9): 786-796) a meta-analysis showing that hydrogen peroxide mouthrinses (1.5-3%) significantly reduced gingival inflammation and bleeding, with efficacy comparable to chlorhexidine for gingivitis management.

**Tooth Whitening:**
Dahl, J.E. and Pallesen, U. (2003) published in *Critical Reviews in Oral Biology & Medicine* (14(4): 292-304) reviewing tooth bleaching with peroxide-based agents. Low-concentration H2O2 (3%) produced modest but measurable whitening effects, while higher concentrations (10-40% carbamide peroxide) produced more significant results. Transient tooth sensitivity was the most common side effect.

**Produce Decontamination:**
Sapers, G.M. and Sites, J.E. (2003) published in the *Journal of Food Science* (68(3): 1003-1007) demonstrating that hydrogen peroxide solutions (1-5%) significantly reduced bacterial populations on fruits and vegetables, including E. coli and Listeria monocytogenes.

**Wound Healing (Negative Evidence):**
Lineaweaver, W. et al. (1985) published in *Annals of Plastic Surgery* (15(4): 307-310) demonstrating that hydrogen peroxide at concentrations as low as 0.5% was toxic to human fibroblasts in vitro and delayed wound healing. This study contributed to the clinical shift away from H2O2 as a wound antiseptic.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Do Not Mix with Vinegar

Combining hydrogen peroxide with vinegar (acetic acid) produces peracetic acid (CH3COOOH):

H2O2 + CH3COOH -> CH3COOOH + H2O

Peracetic acid is a potent oxidizer and respiratory irritant used in industrial sterilization. While effective as a disinfectant, it should not be produced inadvertently at home. Using vinegar and H2O2 sequentially (wipe surface with one, let dry, then apply the other) is acceptable; mixing them together in one bottle is not.

### Concentration Safety

| Concentration | Use | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| 3% | Household use, oral care, first aid | Low (skin/eye irritant) |
| 10% | Hair bleaching, some cleaning | Moderate (skin burns possible) |
| 30% | Laboratory reagent | High (immediate skin burns, fire risk) |
| 35% ("food grade" concentrate) | Dilute before use | High (severe burns, fire risk) |
| 50-70% | Industrial | Very high (explosive with organics) |

**Important:** "Food-grade" 35% hydrogen peroxide is extremely dangerous at full concentration. Despite misleading marketing, it must be diluted to 3% or less before any contact with skin or ingestion. Several deaths have been reported from accidental ingestion of 35% H2O2.

### General Safety (3% Solution)
- **Skin:** May cause temporary whitening (capillary embolism -- oxygen microbubbles in capillaries) and mild irritation. Rinse with water.
- **Eyes:** Causes eye irritation. Flush immediately with water for 15-20 minutes.
- **Ingestion:** Small amounts of 3% solution cause gastric irritation. Larger amounts can cause oxygen gas embolism in rare cases. Do not induce vomiting; contact Poison Control.
- **Inhalation:** Mist or vapor may irritate respiratory tract. Use in ventilated area.

### Wound Care Advisory
Modern wound care guidelines generally recommend against using hydrogen peroxide on wounds. While it kills bacteria, it also damages fibroblasts and keratinocytes, delaying wound healing. Use sterile saline for wound irrigation instead.

### Material Compatibility
- Decomposes rapidly in contact with transition metals (iron, copper, manganese) -- avoid metal containers
- Can bleach colored fabrics, carpets, and hair on contact (even at 3%)
- May damage some natural stones
- Store in original brown/opaque plastic bottle
- Do not transfer to metal containers or containers with metal lids

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages
- Decomposes to water and oxygen only (no toxic residues, no environmental persistence)
- EPA-registered disinfectant effective against bacteria, viruses, and fungi
- Color-safe bleach for most fabrics
- No fumes or harsh chemical odor
- Inexpensive and widely available ($1-3 per bottle)
- Versatile: disinfection, bleaching, oral care, produce washing, stain removal
- Does not contribute to antimicrobial resistance
- No phosphates, chlorine, or volatile organic compounds
- Safe for septic systems

### Disadvantages / Limitations
- Degrades over time, especially when exposed to light, heat, or contamination (limited shelf life once opened)
- Must be stored in opaque containers away from light
- Slower acting than chlorine-based disinfectants
- Can bleach colored surfaces and fabrics on contact (staining risk)
- Not effective against all pathogens at 3% (some spores require higher concentrations or longer contact times)
- Damages healthy wound tissue (cytotoxic to fibroblasts)
- Limited penetration into porous surfaces for deep disinfection
- No residual antimicrobial activity (breaks down too quickly to provide lasting protection)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life (unopened):** 1-3 years in original sealed, opaque container at room temperature
- **Shelf life (opened):** 1-6 months once opened, as each opening introduces contaminants and atmospheric catalysts that accelerate decomposition
- **Storage conditions:** Store in a cool (below 30 degrees C), dark location. Light (especially UV) accelerates decomposition.
- **Container:** Must be stored in opaque (brown or dark) HDPE plastic bottles. Never store in metal containers or clear glass.
- **Testing for potency:** Drop a small amount on a raw potato or meat -- vigorous bubbling indicates active H2O2. Minimal or no bubbling indicates significant decomposition. Test strips are also available for quantitative measurement.
- **Signs of degradation:** The bottle may appear swollen or pressurized (oxygen gas accumulation from decomposition). Lack of effervescence on contact with blood or catalase-containing tissues indicates expired product.
- **Do not:** Freeze (can increase concentration of residual liquid), heat, or contaminate with organic material, dust, or metals.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Typical price:** $1-3 USD per 16 oz (473 mL) bottle of 3% solution at pharmacies and grocery stores.
- **Bulk pricing:** $8-15 per gallon of 3% solution.
- **Sources:** Pharmacies, grocery stores, dollar stores, online retailers. Universally available.
- **Concentrations available:**
  - 3% (household/first aid) -- standard, recommended for all DIY uses
  - 6-10% (hair developer) -- available at beauty supply stores
  - 12% (hair developer, strong) -- beauty supply
  - 30% (laboratory grade) -- chemical suppliers (requires caution)
  - 35% ("food-grade" concentrate) -- specialty retailers (requires extreme caution; must be diluted)

## Regulatory Status

- **EPA:** Registered as an antimicrobial pesticide (disinfectant) under FIFRA. Multiple EPA registration numbers for various formulations and concentrations.
- **FDA:** Approved as a direct food additive (21 CFR 184.1366) for use as an antimicrobial agent and bleaching agent in food processing (milk, cheese, corn syrup, starch, wine). OTC drug ingredient for oral wound debridement.
- **OSHA:** Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL): 1 ppm (1.4 mg/m3) 8-hour TWA for occupational exposure.
- **EU:** Approved as a biocide under BPR (EU 528/2012). Approved as a food additive (E number not assigned; used as a processing aid). Subject to concentration-dependent regulatory requirements.
- **GHS Classification (3%):** Oxidizing liquid Category 1; Acute toxicity Category 5 (oral); Eye irritation Category 2A.
- **Consumer products:** No purchase restrictions on 3% solutions. Some jurisdictions regulate sales of concentrations above 12% due to potential misuse in improvised explosives.

## Related Ingredients

- **Sodium Percarbonate (Na2CO3 . 1.5H2O2):** Solid form of hydrogen peroxide combined with washing soda. Releases H2O2 upon dissolution. Active ingredient in "OxiClean" and similar oxygen bleach products. More stable for storage than liquid H2O2.
- **[Household Ammonia](household-ammonia.md):** Complementary cleaning agent (alkaline vs. oxidizing). Can be used sequentially but should not be mixed.
- **Sodium Hypochlorite (Bleach):** Stronger oxidizer/disinfectant, but leaves chlorinated residues and produces toxic byproducts. H2O2 is the gentler, greener alternative.
- **Benzoyl Peroxide:** Organic peroxide used in acne treatment. More lipophilic than H2O2, better skin penetration, but more irritating.
- **Peracetic Acid:** Formed when H2O2 reacts with acetic acid. Powerful disinfectant used industrially but too hazardous for home preparation.
