# Sea Salt
> Minimally processed sodium chloride harvested from evaporated seawater or ancient salt deposits, containing trace minerals that distinguish it from refined table salt, used in oral care, wound healing, saline solutions, nasal irrigation, and household cleaning via its osmotic antibacterial properties.

## Overview

Sea salt is sodium chloride obtained by the evaporation of seawater or from underground halite deposits left by ancient seas. Unlike refined table salt, which is processed to remove virtually all minerals other than NaCl and then supplemented with anti-caking agents (calcium silicate, sodium aluminosilicate) and iodine (as potassium iodide), sea salt retains a variable spectrum of trace minerals including magnesium, calcium, potassium, iron, zinc, and manganese. These trace minerals account for 1-4% of sea salt's mass and are responsible for its characteristic color and flavor variations.

Himalayan pink salt, mined from the Khewra Salt Mine in Punjab, Pakistan, is technically a rock salt (halite) rather than a sea salt, but it is marketed alongside sea salts and has a similar trace mineral profile. Its pink-to-red color comes from iron oxide (rust) inclusions. Despite marketing claims, its mineral content is not dramatically different from other unrefined sea salts, and the quantities of trace minerals per serving are nutritionally negligible.

In DIY natural products, sea salt is valued for its osmotic antibacterial properties, its ability to promote tissue healing (particularly in oral care), its gentle abrasive quality for scrubs, and its role as a texturizing agent in hair sprays. It is essential in saline solutions for wound irrigation and nasal rinsing, where non-iodized salt is specifically required to avoid mucosal irritation. Sea salt is also a workhorse ingredient in natural cleaning formulations, where it serves as a mild abrasive and contributes to antimicrobial action.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Crystal Structure

Sodium chloride crystallizes in the face-centered cubic (FCC) crystal system, also called the rock salt structure. Each sodium ion (Na+) is surrounded by six chloride ions (Cl-) and vice versa, forming an octahedral coordination geometry. The lattice parameter is 5.6402 angstroms. This regular crystal structure is responsible for salt's characteristic cubic crystal habit and its easy cleavage along {100} planes.

### Isotonic and Hypertonic Solutions

- **Isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl):** Matches the osmolarity of human body fluids (~308 mOsm/L). Used for wound irrigation, nasal rinse, contact lens solution, and IV fluids. Non-irritating to tissues.
- **Hypertonic saline (>0.9% NaCl):** Draws water from cells and tissues by osmosis. Mildly bactericidal. Gargling concentration is typically 1-3% (1-3 teaspoons per cup of warm water).
- **Saturated brine (~26% NaCl):** Used for pickling, fermentation, and as a cleaning agent.

### pH Behavior

Pure NaCl in water is neutral (pH 7.0). However, unrefined sea salts may produce slightly acidic or alkaline solutions depending on their mineral buffers. Magnesium chloride and calcium chloride contribute slight acidity, while bicarbonate traces contribute slight alkalinity. In practice, sea salt solutions range from pH 6.5-7.5, which is close enough to neutral for all DIY applications.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Solar Evaporation (Traditional Method)

The oldest method of salt production, practiced for at least 8,000 years:

1. Seawater is channeled into shallow ponds or pans (salterns/salt pans).
2. Sun and wind evaporate the water over weeks to months.
3. As concentration increases, minerals precipitate in sequence: calcium carbonate first (at ~75% evaporation), then gypsum (calcium sulfate), then halite (NaCl) at ~90% evaporation.
4. The remaining liquid (bittern) is rich in magnesium chloride, potassium chloride, and other highly soluble salts.
5. The NaCl crystals are raked, drained, and dried.
6. **Fleur de sel** ("flower of salt") is the delicate crust that forms on the surface of evaporating brine and is hand-harvested; it commands premium prices.

Solar evaporation produces salt retaining the most trace minerals and moisture.

### Rock Salt Mining

Himalayan and other rock salts are mined from underground halite deposits:

1. Room-and-pillar mining: large chambers are cut into the salt deposit, leaving pillars of salt to support the roof.
2. The mined salt is crushed, screened by particle size, and optionally washed.
3. No chemical processing is required for unrefined rock salt.
4. The Khewra Salt Mine in Pakistan, the source of most "Himalayan" salt, has estimated reserves of 6.7 billion tons.

### Vacuum Evaporation (Industrial Refined Salt)

1. Water is injected into underground salt deposits to dissolve the salt (solution mining).
2. The saturated brine is pumped to the surface.
3. Impurities (calcium, magnesium, sulfate) are removed by chemical precipitation.
4. The purified brine is evaporated under vacuum in multiple-effect evaporators.
5. The resulting salt is >99.5% pure NaCl with fine, uniform crystal size.
6. Anti-caking agents and iodine are added post-crystallization.

### DIY Salt Harvesting from Seawater

For educational or artisanal purposes (ensure the water source is clean and unpolluted):

1. Collect seawater from a clean, unpolluted location (away from harbors, runoff, industrial areas).
2. Pre-filter through cloth to remove debris.
3. Pour into a shallow, wide, food-safe container (stainless steel, glass, or enamel).
4. Place in direct sun with good air circulation. Cover loosely with cheesecloth to keep insects out.
5. Allow to evaporate. Seawater is approximately 3.5% salt, so 1 liter yields approximately 35 g of salt.
6. As crystals form, rake or collect them.
7. Drain off remaining bittern (bitter, magnesium-rich liquid).
8. Air-dry crystals. No heating is necessary.

**Caution:** Seawater from polluted areas may contain heavy metals, microplastics, industrial chemicals, or pathogenic microorganisms. Only harvest from verified clean sources.

## Mechanism of Action

### Osmotic Antibacterial Effect

When bacteria are exposed to a hypertonic salt solution, water is drawn out of the bacterial cell by osmosis across the semi-permeable cell membrane. This causes:

1. **Plasmolysis:** The cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall as water exits.
2. **Dehydration of enzymes:** Loss of intracellular water increases ionic strength, denaturing enzymes and disrupting metabolic processes.
3. **Collapse of turgor pressure:** Bacteria lose the internal pressure needed to maintain cell shape and membrane integrity.

Most pathogenic bacteria cannot survive in solutions above 10% NaCl. Some halotolerant species (e.g., *Staphylococcus aureus*) can tolerate up to 15-20% NaCl, and halophilic archaea thrive at saturated brine concentrations. The antibacterial effect is bacteriostatic (growth-inhibiting) at lower concentrations and bactericidal (killing) at higher concentrations.

### Wound Healing and Oral Care

Isotonic saline (0.9%) supports wound healing by:

1. **Gentle mechanical debridement:** Flushing removes debris, bacteria, and dead cells without damaging viable tissue.
2. **Maintenance of moist wound environment:** Isotonic solution does not draw water from or push water into cells, maintaining cellular integrity.
3. **Non-cytotoxic:** Unlike antiseptics (hydrogen peroxide, povidone-iodine, chlorhexidine), isotonic saline does not damage fibroblasts, keratinocytes, or white blood cells involved in healing.

Hypertonic saline mouth rinse (1-3%) promotes gum healing after dental procedures by:

1. Drawing fluid from swollen gingival tissue (reducing edema).
2. Shifting the oral pH away from the acidic range where cariogenic bacteria thrive.
3. Stimulating increased saliva production, which contains natural antimicrobials (lysozyme, lactoferrin, IgA).

### Nasal Irrigation

Isotonic or slightly hypertonic (1.5-3%) saline nasal irrigation:

1. Physically flushes mucus, allergens, bacteria, and inflammatory mediators from the nasal cavity.
2. Improves mucociliary clearance by rehydrating the airway surface liquid.
3. Reduces mucosal edema (with hypertonic solution).
4. Non-iodized salt must be used because iodine is irritating to nasal mucosa and may impair ciliary function.

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Saline nasal rinse (neti pot solution):**
- Dissolve 2.5 g (1/2 teaspoon) non-iodized sea salt and 1.25 g (1/4 teaspoon) baking soda in 240 mL of previously boiled (and cooled) or distilled water.
- Baking soda buffers the pH and reduces the stinging sensation.
- Use within 24 hours; make fresh each day.
- **Critical:** Use only distilled, previously boiled, or sterile water. Tap water may contain *Naegleria fowleri* (brain-eating amoeba) in rare cases.

**Oral care salt rinse:**
- Dissolve 1/2-1 teaspoon sea salt in 240 mL warm water.
- Swish for 30-60 seconds, spit. Do not swallow.
- Use after tooth extraction, gum surgery, or for general gum health.
- Frequency: 3-4 times daily after dental procedures; 1-2 times daily for maintenance.

**Salt scrub (body exfoliant):**
- Mix 1 cup fine sea salt with 1/2 cup carrier oil (coconut, olive, or sweet almond).
- Add 10-20 drops essential oil (lavender, peppermint, or citrus).
- Apply to damp skin in circular motions; rinse thoroughly.
- Do not use on face (too abrasive) or broken skin.

**Sea salt hair texturizing spray:**
- Dissolve 1 tablespoon sea salt in 240 mL warm water.
- Add 1/2 teaspoon light oil (argan or jojoba) and 1/2 teaspoon aloe vera gel.
- Spray on damp hair for beachy waves and volume.
- Note: salt is hygroscopic and drying with prolonged use; moisturize hair regularly.

**Salt-based natural cleaning paste:**
- Mix equal parts fine sea salt and baking soda.
- Add enough water or white vinegar to form a paste.
- Use as a mild abrasive for sinks, tubs, countertops, and cookware.

**Sole (concentrated mineral water):**
- Place Himalayan or sea salt chunks in a glass jar, cover with water.
- Allow to dissolve for 24 hours; when salt remains undissolved, the solution is saturated (~26%).
- Some proponents add 1 teaspoon of sole to a glass of water each morning for mineral supplementation (evidence for health benefits is anecdotal at best).

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Food preservation:** Salting, brining, curing of meats and fish. Inhibits *Clostridium botulinum* and other foodborne pathogens.
- **De-icing:** Rock salt is spread on roads and sidewalks to lower the freezing point of water (eutectic point of NaCl-water: -21.1 C).
- **Chemical feedstock:** NaCl is the primary raw material for the chlor-alkali industry, producing chlorine gas, sodium hydroxide, and sodium carbonate.
- **Water softening:** Ion exchange water softeners use NaCl brine to regenerate resin beds, replacing calcium and magnesium ions with sodium.
- **Textile dyeing:** Salt is used as a mordant and leveling agent in dyeing cotton with reactive dyes.

### Historical Uses

Salt has been one of the most important commodities in human history. The word "salary" derives from the Latin *salarium*, possibly referring to Roman soldiers' salt allowances. Salt taxes (gabelle in France) financed governments and triggered revolutions. Ancient Egyptians used natron (a mixture of sodium carbonate and sodium chloride) for mummification. Salt was traded along the Saharan salt routes and was literally worth its weight in gold in parts of West Africa. Gandhi's 1930 Salt March to Dandi was a pivotal act of civil disobedience against British colonial salt taxes.

In medicine, Hippocrates (460-370 BCE) recommended salt water for wound treatment. Roman physicians prescribed sea bathing (thalassotherapy) for skin conditions, arthritis, and respiratory ailments.

## Efficacy & Research

**Saline nasal irrigation for sinusitis:**
Rabago et al. (2002) - *Journal of Family Practice* (51(12):1049-1055): Randomized controlled trial demonstrating that daily hypertonic saline nasal irrigation significantly improved symptoms and quality of life in patients with chronic sinonasal disease over a 6-month period.

**Salt water mouth rinse after dental extraction:**
Aravinth et al. (2017) - *International Journal of Oral Health Sciences* (7(2):75-79): Warm saline mouth rinses significantly reduced post-extraction complications including dry socket (alveolar osteitis) and infection compared to controls.

**Hypertonic saline for wound healing:**
Hashimoto et al. (2004) - *Journal of Trauma* (57(5):1044-1048): Hypertonic saline dressings reduced bacterial colonization and promoted granulation tissue formation in chronic wounds compared to isotonic saline.

**Mineral content of Himalayan salt:**
Fayet-Moore et al. (2020) - *Journal of Food Composition and Analysis* (86):103379: Comprehensive mineral analysis of Himalayan pink salt found 84 detectable minerals, but most were present in amounts too small to provide meaningful nutritional benefit at normal dietary intake (i.e., 5-6 g salt/day).

**Thalassotherapy (sea salt bathing) for skin conditions:**
Proksch et al. (2005) - *International Journal of Dermatology* (44(2):151-157): Bathing in Dead Sea salt solutions (5% concentration) improved skin barrier function, enhanced skin hydration, and reduced inflammation in patients with atopic dry skin compared to tap water bathing.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

- **Dietary sodium:** Excessive sodium intake is linked to hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stroke, and kidney disease. The WHO recommends <5 g salt (2 g sodium) per day. Sea salt is not "healthier" than table salt in terms of sodium content.
- **Nasal irrigation:** Use only sterile, distilled, or previously boiled (then cooled) water. Never use unboiled tap water due to risk of *Naegleria fowleri* (primary amoebic meningoencephalitis), which is rare but almost always fatal.
- **Iodine:** Non-iodized salt should be used for nasal irrigation and wound care. However, if sea salt is your primary salt source, be aware that it provides little to no iodine, and iodine deficiency remains a global health concern. Supplement iodine through diet (seaweed, dairy, iodized salt for cooking).
- **Skin application:** Salt scrubs should not be used on broken, inflamed, or sunburned skin. Fine salt is less irritating than coarse salt.
- **Eye contact:** Even isotonic saline can cause stinging if the pH is not buffered. Always buffer with baking soda for eye-adjacent use.
- **Corrosion:** Salt solutions corrode metals. Do not store in or apply with metal containers (except stainless steel and certain alloys).
- **Contaminants in sea salt:** Microplastics have been detected in commercial sea salts worldwide (Kim et al., 2018 - *Environmental Science & Technology*). The health significance is under active investigation but currently considered minimal.
- **Heavy metals:** Some Himalayan salt samples have shown elevated lead levels, though typically below regulatory limits for food (Fayet-Moore et al., 2020).

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Extremely inexpensive and universally available
- Simple, single-ingredient product with well-understood chemistry
- Non-toxic at normal use concentrations
- Effective antibacterial without disrupting the microbiome at moderate concentrations
- No known allergenicity
- Indefinite shelf life when stored dry
- Food-grade quality readily available
- Versatile: oral care, skin care, cleaning, preservation, wound care

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Antibacterial effect is bacteriostatic at low concentrations; not a substitute for proper antiseptics in contaminated wounds
- Drying to skin and hair with prolonged or repeated use
- Trace mineral content, while marketed as a benefit, is nutritionally insignificant at normal intake
- Corrosive to metals and some surfaces
- Overuse in oral care can cause mucosal irritation and sodium overload if swallowed
- Microplastic contamination is a growing concern in sea-sourced salt
- No standardization of mineral content between sources and batches
- Marketing claims about Himalayan salt benefits are largely unsupported by evidence

## Storage & Shelf Life

| Form | Storage Conditions | Shelf Life |
|---|---|---|
| Dry sea salt (sealed) | Cool, dry, airtight container (glass, ceramic, or food-grade plastic) | Indefinite (NaCl does not decompose) |
| Dry sea salt (opened) | Keep moisture out; add a few grains of rice as a desiccant | Indefinite for NaCl; trace minerals stable |
| Saline solution (sterile) | Sealed, room temperature | Per manufacturer (typically 12-24 months) |
| DIY saline solution | Refrigerated | 24 hours (discard and make fresh daily) |
| Salt scrub | Sealed jar, room temperature | 3-6 months (limited by carrier oil shelf life) |

Salt is hygroscopic (absorbs moisture from air), especially in humid environments. This causes clumping but does not affect safety or efficacy. Store with tight-fitting lids. Anti-caking agents (found in refined salt) prevent clumping but are unnecessary in DIY applications.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Generic sea salt (fine or coarse):** $2-5 per kg at grocery stores
- **Himalayan pink salt:** $5-15 per kg (widely available; price premium is largely marketing)
- **Celtic sea salt (grey, moist):** $8-20 per kg (specialty grocery stores, online)
- **Fleur de sel:** $15-40 per 250 g (gourmet stores; unnecessary for DIY products -- this is for cooking)
- **Dead Sea salt (bath grade):** $10-20 per kg (online retailers, spa supply stores)
- **USP-grade NaCl (for sterile solutions):** $5-15 per 500 g (pharmacy or lab supply)

For most DIY applications, inexpensive fine sea salt or Himalayan salt from a grocery store is entirely adequate. There is no evidence that more expensive salts are more effective for topical, oral rinse, or cleaning applications.

## Regulatory Status

- **United States:** Salt is GRAS (21 CFR 182.1). FDA regulates iodized salt labeling. No specific regulation for cosmetic use.
- **European Union:** Codex Alimentarius standard for food-grade salt (CODEX STAN 150-1985). Cosmetic use unrestricted.
- **USP/NF:** Sodium Chloride USP monograph sets standards for pharmaceutical-grade NaCl (>99.0% purity, limits on heavy metals).
- **Cosmetics (INCI):** Maris Sal (Sea Salt), Sodium Chloride.

## Related Ingredients

- **Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate):** Often combined with salt in oral rinses and nasal irrigation solutions as a pH buffer.
- **[Washing soda (sodium carbonate)](/Ingredients/washing-soda.md):** Stronger alkaline salt used in cleaning; different from sea salt.
- **Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate):** Not a sodium chloride salt; used in bath soaks for magnesium supplementation and muscle relaxation.
- **Dead Sea minerals:** A specific mineral blend (high magnesium, potassium, and bromide) from the Dead Sea with studied dermatological benefits.
- **[White vinegar](/Ingredients/white-vinegar.md):** Often paired with salt in cleaning formulations.
