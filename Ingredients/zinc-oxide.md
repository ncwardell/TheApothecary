# Zinc Oxide

> An inorganic mineral compound that provides broad-spectrum UV protection by physically reflecting and scattering ultraviolet radiation, while also serving as a skin protectant, mild antiseptic, and alternative deodorant active ingredient — one of only two FDA-approved mineral sunscreen filters.

## Overview

Zinc oxide is one of the most versatile mineral compounds in personal care and medicine. It has been used topically for thousands of years — ancient texts from India, Greece, and Rome describe zinc-containing preparations for wound healing and skin protection. The compound itself is remarkably simple (Zn²⁺ and O²⁻ in a wurtzite crystal structure) yet provides an extraordinary range of functions: UV radiation blocking, skin protection, astringent action, mild antimicrobial activity, and wound healing promotion.

In modern DIY personal care, zinc oxide fills three critical gaps:

1. **Sunscreen**: Zinc oxide is one of only two mineral UV filters approved by the FDA (the other being titanium dioxide). It provides genuinely broad-spectrum protection against both UVA and UVB radiation by physically reflecting, scattering, and absorbing UV photons. Unlike chemical sunscreens (oxybenzone, avobenzone, octinoxate), zinc oxide sits on the skin surface and does not penetrate or cause endocrine disruption.

2. **Skin protectant**: The FDA monograph for skin protectants lists zinc oxide (1–25%) as a safe and effective protectant. It forms a physical barrier on the skin that protects against moisture (diaper rash), friction, and irritants. Calamine lotion (zinc oxide + iron oxide) is the standard treatment for poison ivy, chickenpox, and other itchy skin conditions.

3. **Deodorant active**: Zinc oxide creates an environment inhospitable to odor-causing bacteria through a combination of surface alkalinity and direct antibacterial action of Zn²⁺ ions. Some people who don't respond well to baking soda or magnesium hydroxide find zinc oxide to be an effective alternative.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Crystal Structure

- Zinc oxide crystallizes primarily in the wurtzite structure (hexagonal)
- Each Zn²⁺ is tetrahedrally coordinated with four O²⁻ atoms
- This crystal structure is responsible for ZnO's semiconductor properties and UV absorption

### UV Protection Mechanism

| UV Type | Wavelength | ZnO Protection | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| UVB | 280–320 nm | Excellent | Absorption (band gap excitation) |
| UVA2 | 320–340 nm | Excellent | Absorption + scattering |
| UVA1 | 340–400 nm | Good | Scattering + reflection |
| Visible light | >400 nm | Minimal (white appearance) | Scattering |

Zinc oxide's UV protection is unique among mineral sunscreens because it covers the full UVA + UVB spectrum. Titanium dioxide protects primarily against UVB and short UVA but is weaker in the UVA1 range.

### Physical Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Melting point | 1,975°C (decomposes) |
| Density | 5.61 g/cm³ |
| Band gap | 3.37 eV (corresponds to UV absorption onset ~370 nm) |
| Refractive index | 2.0 |
| Particle size (cosmetic) | 100–300 nm (non-nano); <100 nm (nano) |

### Nano vs. Non-Nano

| Property | Non-Nano (>100 nm) | Nano (<100 nm) |
|---|---|---|
| White cast | Significant on darker skin | Minimal |
| Penetration | Does not penetrate intact skin | Does not penetrate intact skin (FDA position) |
| UV protection | Good | Slightly better UVA |
| DIY preference | Recommended (easier to handle) | Use with caution (dust inhalation concerns) |
| Transparency | Opaque white | More transparent |

For DIY sunscreen, **non-nano, uncoated zinc oxide** is the standard recommendation.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Industrial Production

**Direct (American) process**:
1. Zinc metal is vaporized at ~907°C
2. Zinc vapor reacts with oxygen in air: 2Zn + O₂ → 2ZnO
3. The resulting ZnO smoke is collected and classified by particle size

**Indirect (French) process**:
1. Zinc ore (zincite, smithsonite, or sphalerite) is reduced to metallic zinc
2. Zinc is re-oxidized to produce ZnO powder

**Wet chemical process (pharmaceutical grade)**:
1. Zinc salts (ZnSO₄ or ZnCl₂) are reacted with sodium hydroxide or sodium carbonate
2. Precipitation: ZnSO₄ + 2NaOH → Zn(OH)₂↓ + Na₂SO₄
3. Calcination: Zn(OH)₂ → ZnO + H₂O
4. Produces very fine, uniform, high-purity powder

### Home Production

Not practical. Zinc oxide is a refined industrial product requiring high-temperature processing. It is inexpensive and readily available as a cosmetic-grade powder.

## Mechanism of Action

### UV Protection (Sunscreen)

Zinc oxide provides UV protection through three simultaneous mechanisms:

1. **Absorption**: ZnO is a semiconductor with a band gap of 3.37 eV. UV photons with energy above this threshold are absorbed, exciting electrons from the valence band to the conduction band. The energy is dissipated as heat rather than reaching the skin.

2. **Scattering**: Particles in the 100–300 nm size range scatter UV photons (Mie scattering), redirecting them away from the skin surface.

3. **Reflection**: The high refractive index (2.0) of ZnO causes partial reflection of UV radiation at the particle-medium interface.

The combination of these three mechanisms provides **truly broad-spectrum** protection across UVB (280–320 nm) and UVA (320–400 nm), including the critical UVA1 range that is most deeply penetrating and associated with photoaging and melanoma.

### Skin Protectant / Barrier

1. ZnO powder forms a physical barrier on the skin surface
2. This barrier separates skin from irritants (urine in diaper area, environmental pollutants, wound drainage)
3. The mild astringent action of Zn²⁺ ions helps reduce inflammation and oozing
4. Promotes wound healing through zinc's role as a cofactor in metalloproteinases involved in tissue repair

### Antimicrobial / Deodorant

1. Zn²⁺ ions released at the skin surface are directly antibacterial
2. ZnO creates a mildly alkaline microenvironment that inhibits odor-causing bacteria
3. Zinc disrupts bacterial enzyme systems (binding to thiol groups in bacterial proteins)
4. Effective against *Corynebacterium* and *Staphylococcus* species involved in body odor

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**DIY Mineral Sunscreen (Primary Use)**:
- 2 tbsp zinc oxide (non-nano, uncoated)
- ¼ cup coconut oil or shea butter (melted)
- 2 tbsp beeswax (for water resistance)
- 1 tbsp jojoba oil
- Optional: vitamin E (antioxidant)
- Melt oils/beeswax, cool slightly, stir in zinc oxide thoroughly
- Estimated SPF 15–25 depending on concentration and application thickness
- **Note**: Homemade sunscreen cannot be reliably SPF-tested. Use generously and reapply frequently.

**Zinc Oxide Concentration Guide**:
- 5–10%: Light protection (~SPF 5–10)
- 15–20%: Moderate protection (~SPF 15–25)
- 25%: Maximum recommended for DIY (~SPF 25–30+)
- Higher concentrations become chalky white and difficult to apply

**Diaper Cream**:
- 2 tbsp zinc oxide
- 3 tbsp coconut oil
- 1 tbsp shea butter
- 1 tsp beeswax (optional, for thicker consistency)
- Melt oils, stir in ZnO, pour into tin
- Creates a protective barrier against moisture and friction

**Calamine-Style Lotion**:
- 4 tbsp zinc oxide
- 1 tsp iron oxide (for pink color and additional soothing)
- 6 tbsp carrier oil (jojoba or olive)
- For poison ivy, insect bites, chickenpox, minor rashes

**Deodorant Additive**:
- Add ½–1 tsp zinc oxide to deodorant recipe
- Alternative or supplement to baking soda/magnesium hydroxide
- Some people respond better to zinc-based deodorant than other actives

**Wound Healing Paste**:
- Mix zinc oxide with a small amount of coconut oil or olive oil
- Apply to minor cuts, scrapes, and abrasions
- Zinc is a cofactor in wound healing enzymes

### Medical / Pharmaceutical Uses

- **Calamine lotion**: ZnO + Fe₂O₃ in a liquid suspension (OTC)
- **Zinc oxide tape**: Adhesive bandaging in sports medicine
- **Dental cements**: Zinc oxide-eugenol (ZOE) cements
- **Surgical dusting powder**: Sterile ZnO for wound care
- **Hemorrhoid treatment**: Zinc oxide suppositories and creams

### Industrial Uses

- **Paint pigment**: "Chinese white" (zinc white)
- **Rubber vulcanization**: Activator in rubber manufacturing
- **Ceramics**: Flux in ceramic glazes
- **Electronics**: Semiconductor material, varistors, piezoelectric devices

## Efficacy & Research

### UV Protection

- Smijs, T.G. & Pavel, S. (2011) in *Nanotechnology, Science and Applications* provided a comprehensive review of ZnO and TiO₂ as mineral UV filters, confirming ZnO's superior broad-spectrum coverage, particularly in the UVA1 range where chemical filters are weakest.

### Skin Penetration (Safety)

- Gulson, B. et al. (2010) in *Toxicological Sciences* used isotope-labeled ZnO to track penetration through human skin in vivo. Found that ZnO nanoparticles did not penetrate beyond the stratum corneum into living tissue in subjects with intact skin.

### Wound Healing

- Lansdown, A.B.G. et al. (2007) in *Wound Repair and Regeneration* reviewed zinc's role in wound healing, confirming that topical zinc promotes epithelialization and reduces bacterial infection through direct antimicrobial action and immune modulation.

### Antimicrobial

- Pasquet, J. et al. (2014) in *International Journal of Pharmaceutics* demonstrated ZnO's antibacterial activity against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria through Zn²⁺ ion release and reactive oxygen species generation.

### Diaper Rash

- Zinc oxide paste/cream is the standard first-line treatment for diaper dermatitis, recommended by the AAP (American Academy of Pediatrics). Randomized trials confirm superiority over petroleum jelly alone for moderate diaper rash.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Topical Safety

- Zinc oxide has an exceptional safety profile for topical use
- FDA-approved as both a sunscreen active (up to 25%) and skin protectant (1–25%)
- Used on babies, pregnant women, and sensitive skin without restriction
- Non-comedogenic
- No evidence of systemic absorption through intact skin

### Inhalation Hazard

- **Do NOT inhale zinc oxide powder** — fine ZnO dust can cause "metal fume fever" (flu-like symptoms lasting 24–48 hours) if inhaled in significant quantities
- Wear a dust mask when handling dry ZnO powder
- Mix in a well-ventilated area
- Once incorporated into an oil/cream base, inhalation risk is eliminated

### Not for Internal Use

- Zinc oxide is not meant to be ingested (except in very small amounts as a dietary supplement formulation)
- Large ingestion can cause nausea, vomiting, and GI irritation

### Sunscreen Limitations

- **DIY sunscreen cannot be reliably SPF-tested** — SPF requires standardized lab testing
- Homemade sunscreen may have uneven ZnO distribution (hotspots and gaps)
- Do not rely solely on homemade sunscreen for extended intense sun exposure
- Apply generously and reapply every 2 hours or after swimming/sweating
- ZnO sunscreen leaves a white cast, especially on darker skin tones

### Environmental

- Zinc oxide is a natural mineral with low aquatic toxicity
- Considered reef-safe (unlike oxybenzone and octinoxate, which are toxic to coral)
- Some concern about nano-ZnO in marine environments — non-nano preferred for environmental safety

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Genuinely broad-spectrum UV protection (UVA + UVB)
- One of only two FDA-approved mineral sunscreen ingredients
- Physical barrier — sits on skin, not absorbed
- No endocrine disruption concerns (unlike chemical sunscreens)
- Reef-safe
- Mild antimicrobial and wound-healing properties
- Non-comedogenic
- Safe for babies, pregnancy, sensitive skin
- Multi-use: sunscreen, diaper cream, deodorant, wound care
- Extremely well-studied safety profile

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Leaves white cast on skin (especially darker skin tones)
- Inhalation hazard when handling dry powder
- DIY sunscreen SPF cannot be precisely determined
- Harder to formulate into elegant cosmetic products at home
- Must be very thoroughly mixed to avoid uneven protection
- Water resistance requires additional formulation (beeswax)
- Nano vs. non-nano debate continues
- More expensive than chemical sunscreen ingredients (though not expensive absolutely)

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: Indefinite (ZnO is a stable inorganic mineral)
- **Storage**: Cool, dry place in sealed container. Protect from moisture.
- **Container**: Original bag/jar. Keep sealed to prevent dust dispersal.
- **Stability**: Does not degrade, oxidize, or lose potency over time
- **Formulations**: Sunscreen/cream formulations have shelf life limited by the carrier oils, not the ZnO

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: Approved OTC sunscreen active ingredient at 2–25% (21 CFR 352). Approved OTC skin protectant at 1–25% (21 CFR 347). GRAS as food additive (21 CFR 182.8991).
- **EU**: Listed in Annex VI of the EU Cosmetics Regulation as approved UV filter (up to 25%). Nano-ZnO subject to additional notification requirements.
- **TGA (Australia)**: Approved sunscreen active ingredient.
- **Japan**: Approved UV filter for cosmetic use.
- **USP/NF**: Monographed as pharmaceutical ingredient.

## Related Ingredients

- **Titanium Dioxide** — The other FDA-approved mineral sunscreen. Better UVB protection but weaker UVA1 coverage than ZnO. Often combined with ZnO in formulations.
- **Beeswax** — Provides water resistance in sunscreen formulations. Structural agent for sunscreen sticks.
- **Shea Butter** — Base oil for sunscreen formulations; adds natural minor SPF (~2–4).
- **Coconut Oil** — Common carrier for ZnO sunscreen; adds minor SPF (~4).
- **Magnesium Hydroxide** — Alternative deodorant active. Some people respond better to one vs. the other.
- **Vitamin E (Tocopherol)** — Antioxidant added to sunscreen formulations to extend shelf life and provide additional UV protection.
