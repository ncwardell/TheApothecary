# Shea Butter
> An ivory-to-yellow fat extracted from the nuts of the African shea tree (*Vitellaria paradoxa*), rich in vitamins A, E, and F, and uniquely high in cinnamic acid esters that provide anti-inflammatory and UV-absorptive properties, melting at body temperature to deliver deep moisturization without comedogenicity.

## Overview

Shea butter is a natural fat extracted from the seed kernels (nuts) of the shea tree (*Vitellaria paradoxa*), which grows exclusively in the semi-arid Sahel and savanna belt of West and Central Africa, spanning from Senegal to Uganda. The shea tree is a slow-growing species that does not begin producing fruit until it is 10-15 years old, reaches full production at 20-30 years, and may live for 200-300 years. The trees are not cultivated in plantations but grow wild, and nut harvesting and butter production provide critical income to millions of rural African women. Global shea butter production is estimated at 600,000-1,000,000 tonnes annually, with Nigeria, Ghana, Burkina Faso, and Mali being the largest producers.

Unrefined shea butter (Grade A) retains its full complement of bioactive compounds, including vitamins A (retinol) and E (tocopherols), vitamin F (essential fatty acids, specifically linoleic acid), allantoin, and -- most distinctively -- cinnamic acid esters. These esters, which constitute 7-12% of the unsaponifiable fraction, are responsible for shea butter's notable anti-inflammatory activity, distinguishing it from other vegetable butters. Refined shea butter (Grade B or C) has been processed with hexane extraction, deodorized, and bleached, which removes or degrades most of the bioactive unsaponifiable fraction while retaining the fatty acid base. For DIY formulations where therapeutic benefit is desired, unrefined shea butter is strongly preferred.

Shea butter melts at 32-38 C, which is approximately human skin temperature. This means it transitions from a solid to a liquid on skin contact, providing a uniquely smooth application feel and allowing deep penetration of lipid-soluble nutrients. Its non-comedogenic nature (comedogenicity rating 0-2) makes it suitable for facial applications in most individuals, though those with very acne-prone skin should patch-test. It is a foundational ingredient in DIY balms, body butters, lip balms, lotion bars, deodorant sticks, and protective skin creams.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Fatty Acid Profile

| Fatty Acid | Percentage | Type |
|---|---|---|
| Oleic acid (C18:1, omega-9) | 40-60% | Monounsaturated |
| Stearic acid (C18:0) | 25-50% | Saturated |
| Linoleic acid (C18:2, omega-6) | 3-11% | Polyunsaturated (essential) |
| Palmitic acid (C16:0) | 2-9% | Saturated |
| Linolenic acid (C18:3, omega-3) | <1% | Polyunsaturated (essential) |
| Arachidic acid (C20:0) | <1% | Saturated |

The high oleic-to-linoleic acid ratio makes shea butter relatively stable against oxidation compared to polyunsaturated oils (e.g., hemp seed oil, rosehip oil). The stearic acid content is responsible for its firm solid consistency at room temperature.

### Unsaponifiable Fraction (5-17%)

The unsaponifiable fraction of shea butter is unusually large compared to most vegetable fats (typically 0.5-2%), and it is this fraction that contains the bioactive compounds:

**Triterpene alcohols (60-70% of unsaponifiable fraction):**
- Alpha-amyrin and beta-amyrin (lupeol)
- Butyrospermol
- Parkeol
- These compounds contribute to anti-inflammatory and wound-healing activity.

**Cinnamic acid esters (7-12% of unsaponifiable fraction):**
- Esters of cinnamic acid with triterpene alcohols
- Responsible for UV absorption (natural SPF estimated at 3-6)
- Primary anti-inflammatory compounds -- inhibit leukotriene B4, prostaglandin E2, and nitric oxide production in macrophages
- Structurally related to cinnamates used in commercial sunscreens

**Tocopherols (vitamin E):**
- Alpha-tocopherol: 50-150 ppm
- Gamma-tocopherol: 20-80 ppm
- Delta-tocopherol: 10-40 ppm
- Antioxidant, protects cell membranes from lipid peroxidation

**Vitamin A (retinol and retinyl esters):**
- Present in small but biologically active quantities
- Promotes keratinocyte differentiation and collagen synthesis

**Allantoin:**
- A urea derivative that promotes cell proliferation and wound healing
- Softens keratin, improving skin texture
- Present at approximately 0.5-1% in unrefined shea butter

**Phytosterols:**
- Stigmasterol, campesterol, beta-sitosterol
- Anti-inflammatory; inhibit complement activation
- Improve skin barrier function

### Triglyceride Structure

Shea butter's physical properties are determined by its triglyceride composition. The dominant triglycerides are:

- **SOO** (stearic-oleic-oleic): liquid at body temperature
- **SOS** (stearic-oleic-stearic): solid at room temperature, melts at ~37 C
- **SSO** (stearic-stearic-oleic): contributes firmness

The SOS fraction is structurally similar to cocoa butter, which is why shea butter is used as a cocoa butter equivalent (CBE) in the confectionery industry.

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Traditional Hand-Processing Method

This is the method used by women's cooperatives across West Africa for centuries:

1. **Harvesting:** Shea fruits are collected from the ground after falling from the tree (June-August). The fleshy pulp is edible and consumed locally.
2. **De-pulping:** The outer fruit pulp is removed, either by eating it or by soaking and washing.
3. **Drying the nuts:** Nuts (with shells) are sun-dried for several days, then smoked or parboiled to prevent germination and facilitate shell removal.
4. **Cracking:** The hard outer shell is cracked manually (with rocks or simple tools) to extract the kernel.
5. **Second drying:** Kernels are dried again until moisture content drops below 7%.
6. **Crushing:** Dried kernels are pounded in a mortar and pestle or ground in a mill to produce a coarse paste.
7. **Roasting:** The crushed kernels are roasted in a large pot over a wood fire, with constant stirring, for 30-60 minutes. This step develops flavor and releases oils. The degree of roasting affects color and aroma (lightly roasted = ivory, heavily roasted = golden/amber).
8. **Grinding to paste:** The roasted material is ground to a fine, smooth paste (similar to peanut butter consistency).
9. **Kneading (beating):** The paste is mixed with water and kneaded vigorously by hand for 30-60 minutes. This critical step separates the fat from the protein and carbohydrate components. The fat begins to float on top as a grayish emulsion.
10. **Washing:** The floating fat is scooped off and washed repeatedly with clean water to remove residual proteins and sugars.
11. **Boiling:** The washed fat is boiled in a large pot. Water evaporates, proteins coagulate and sink, and pure fat rises to the surface.
12. **Skimming and cooling:** The clear golden fat is skimmed from the surface, filtered through cloth, and allowed to cool and solidify.

The entire process from nut to butter takes 20-30 hours of labor and yields approximately 1.5-2 kg of butter per 8-10 kg of nuts.

### Mechanical Extraction (Semi-Industrial)

1. Dried kernels are fed into a mechanical press (screw press or hydraulic press).
2. Oil is expelled at high pressure (cold-pressed if temperature is kept below 60 C).
3. The expelled oil is filtered and allowed to solidify.
4. This method is faster but may yield slightly different unsaponifiable fraction ratios.

### Solvent Extraction (Industrial / Refined)

1. Crushed kernels are treated with hexane to extract the fat.
2. The hexane is evaporated and recovered.
3. The crude fat is degummed, neutralized (with NaOH), bleached (with bleaching earth), and deodorized (steam distillation at high temperature).
4. This produces white, odorless, refined shea butter -- but strips most unsaponifiable compounds.

### Grading System

| Grade | Name | Processing | Color | Odor | Unsaponifiable Content |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Raw/Unrefined | Traditional/cold-pressed | Ivory to yellow | Nutty, smoky | Full (5-17%) |
| B | Refined | Mechanical + light refining | Off-white | Minimal | Partially reduced |
| C | Highly Refined | Solvent extraction + full refining | White | None | Significantly reduced |
| D | Low Grade | Contains impurities | Variable | Variable | Variable |
| E | Contaminated | Contaminated | Variable | Off/rancid | N/A |

For DIY formulations, Grade A (unrefined) is preferred for skin care. Grade B is acceptable for formulations where fragrance neutrality is desired. Grades C-E are not recommended.

## Mechanism of Action

### Moisturization and Barrier Repair

Shea butter is an occlusive moisturizer: it forms a lipid film on the skin surface that reduces transepidermal water loss (TEWL). Its fatty acid profile -- rich in oleic acid and stearic acid -- closely mirrors the lipid composition of human stratum corneum intercellular lipids. This compatibility allows shea butter to integrate into the skin's lipid matrix rather than sitting on the surface, providing deeper moisturization than petroleum-based occlusives (such as petrolatum).

Phytosterols (beta-sitosterol, stigmasterol) in shea butter stimulate the synthesis of ceramides and other intercellular lipids, actively repairing the barrier rather than merely sealing it.

### Anti-Inflammatory Activity

Cinnamic acid esters are the primary anti-inflammatory compounds. They act through multiple pathways:

1. **COX/LOX inhibition:** Reduce production of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) and leukotriene B4 (LTB4), key mediators of inflammation, pain, and redness.
2. **iNOS inhibition:** Reduce production of nitric oxide by activated macrophages.
3. **Complement inhibition:** Triterpene alcohols (butyrospermol, lupeol) inhibit the classical complement pathway.
4. **TNF-alpha suppression:** Lupeol inhibits NF-kB activation, reducing tumor necrosis factor alpha production.

These mechanisms explain shea butter's traditional use for burns, rashes, insect bites, and muscle pain, as well as its efficacy in eczema and psoriasis management.

### UV Protection

Cinnamic acid esters absorb UVB radiation in the 280-320 nm range (peak absorption around 310 nm), providing a natural sun protection factor estimated at SPF 3-6. This is insufficient as a standalone sunscreen but provides additive protection when combined with other UV filters or used as a daily moisturizer.

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Basic whipped body butter:**
- 1 cup unrefined shea butter
- 1/4 cup carrier oil (jojoba, sweet almond, or coconut oil)
- 10-20 drops essential oil (optional)
- Melt shea butter gently (double boiler or microwave in short bursts), add carrier oil, refrigerate until semi-solid, then whip with a hand mixer for 3-5 minutes until fluffy. Store in a jar.

**Natural deodorant stick:**
- 3 tablespoons shea butter
- 2 tablespoons coconut oil
- 3 tablespoons arrowroot starch
- 1 tablespoon baking soda (reduce if sensitive)
- 5-10 drops tea tree or lavender essential oil
- Melt shea butter and coconut oil, stir in dry ingredients, pour into deodorant tube, cool until solid.

**Lip balm:**
- 1 tablespoon shea butter
- 1 tablespoon beeswax
- 1 tablespoon coconut oil or sweet almond oil
- 5 drops peppermint or vanilla essential oil (optional)
- Melt all together, pour into tubes or tins, cool until solid.

**Eczema balm:**
- 2 tablespoons unrefined shea butter
- 1 tablespoon coconut oil
- 1 tablespoon calendula-infused olive oil
- 5 drops lavender essential oil
- 3 drops chamomile essential oil
- Melt, mix, pour into tins.

**Hair moisture treatment:**
- Apply unrefined shea butter to damp hair as a leave-in conditioner (small amount; start with a pea-sized quantity for fine hair, a marble-sized quantity for thick/coarse hair).
- Effective for curly, coily, and textured hair types (Type 3B-4C).
- Acts as a sealant over water-based leave-in conditioner (LOC method: Liquid-Oil-Cream).

**Stretch mark prevention cream:**
- 1/2 cup shea butter
- 1/4 cup cocoa butter
- 1/4 cup coconut oil
- 1 tablespoon vitamin E oil
- Melt, mix, whip, apply to growing belly, thighs, and breasts during pregnancy.

### Industrial & Commercial Uses

- **Cosmetics:** Shea butter is in countless commercial moisturizers, lip products, hair conditioners, soaps, and sun care products.
- **Confectionery:** The SOS triglyceride fraction is used as a cocoa butter equivalent (CBE) in chocolate manufacturing (EU permits up to 5% CBE).
- **Pharmaceutical:** Used as a suppository base and in topical medicinal preparations.
- **Cooking:** In West Africa, shea butter is used as a cooking fat, particularly in regions where palm oil is not available.

### Historical Uses

Shea butter has been used for millennia across the African Sahel. Ancient caravans transported it across the Sahara. Queen Cleopatra is said to have been provided with caravans of shea butter for cosmetic use (though this is possibly apocryphal). In West African tradition, shea butter is used ceremonially for newborns (massaged into skin), for protecting against Harmattan (dry, dusty wind), for cooking, for waterproofing (applied to mud walls and thatched roofs), and for making traditional medicines. The shea tree itself is considered sacred in many cultures, and cutting down a shea tree is taboo or illegal in several West African countries.

## Efficacy & Research

**Anti-inflammatory activity comparable to indomethacin:**
Akihisa et al. (2010) - *Journal of Oleo Science* (59(6):273-280): Demonstrated that cinnamic acid esters isolated from shea butter inhibited TPA-induced ear edema in mice with potency comparable to indomethacin (a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug).

**Skin barrier function improvement:**
Peker et al. (2014) - *Indian Journal of Dermatology* (59(1):42-47): Topical application of shea butter significantly reduced transepidermal water loss (TEWL) and improved skin elasticity in volunteers over 12 weeks of use.

**Nasal anti-inflammatory effect:**
Tella (1979) - *British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology* (7(5):495-497): Early study demonstrating that shea butter nasal instillation reduced nasal mucosal congestion and inflammation, likely attributable to cinnamic acid ester activity.

**Eczema management:**
Verma et al. (2012) - *International Journal of Pharmaceutics* (434(1-2):28-35): Shea butter-based emulsions provided superior moisturization and skin barrier repair compared to mineral oil-based emulsions in patients with mild-to-moderate eczema.

**Wound healing:**
Malachi et al. (2019) - *African Journal of Pharmacy and Pharmacology* (13(5):56-64): Shea butter accelerated wound healing in animal models, attributed to triterpene alcohols and allantoin promoting keratinocyte proliferation and collagen deposition.

**UV absorption:**
Velasco et al. (2008) - *Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology* (7(2):75-80): Demonstrated the UV-absorbing capacity of cinnamic acid esters in shea butter, estimating an in-vitro SPF of 3-6 depending on application thickness.

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

- **Tree nut allergy:** Shea is botanically a tree nut (Sapotaceae family). However, shea butter contains extremely low levels of protein (the allergenic component), and allergic reactions to shea butter are exceedingly rare. The American Academy of Allergy, Asthma & Immunology considers refined shea butter safe for most tree nut-allergic individuals. Unrefined shea butter carries a marginally higher risk due to residual proteins. Individuals with severe tree nut allergies should patch test or consult an allergist.
- **Latex allergy:** Shea belongs to the same order (Ericales) as *Hevea brasiliensis* (rubber tree), but there is no established cross-reactivity with latex. The association is theoretical.
- **Comedogenicity:** While shea butter's comedogenicity rating is low (0-2), individuals with highly acne-prone skin may experience breakouts. Use a small amount on the face and observe for 2-4 weeks.
- **Contamination:** Low-quality or improperly processed shea butter may contain elevated levels of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from smoke-drying the nuts. Purchase from reputable suppliers.
- **Adulteration:** Shea butter is sometimes adulterated with cheaper fats (palm oil, mango kernel oil). Authentic unrefined shea butter has a distinctive nutty/smoky aroma and an ivory-to-yellow color. Pure white "unrefined" shea butter is likely bleached or adulterated.
- **Not a standalone sunscreen:** Despite containing UV-absorbing compounds, shea butter does not provide adequate sun protection on its own. Always use a proper sunscreen for sun protection.

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Exceptionally rich unsaponifiable fraction with multiple bioactive compounds
- Anti-inflammatory activity comparable to pharmaceutical agents in some studies
- Melts at body temperature for ideal skin absorption
- Non-comedogenic for most individuals
- Rich in vitamins A, E, and F
- Sustainable, ethically important crop supporting rural African economies
- Multi-purpose: moisturizer, balm base, deodorant base, hair care, lip care
- Pleasant, mild scent (unrefined) that blends well with essential oils
- Relatively long shelf life for a natural fat
- Allergenicity is extremely rare despite being a tree nut product

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Unrefined grade has a noticeable nutty/smoky aroma that some find unappealing in certain formulations
- Can feel greasy on skin if over-applied; a little goes a long way
- Batch-to-batch variability in color, texture, and bioactive content (wild-harvested product)
- High melting point can make it difficult to incorporate into water-based formulations
- Grainy texture may develop if melted and cooled too slowly (due to polymorphic crystallization of stearic acid triglycerides). Fix by melting completely and cooling quickly.
- Supply chain concerns: deforestation, child labor, and fair trade issues in some regions
- Refined shea butter loses most therapeutic benefits

## Storage & Shelf Life

| Form | Storage Conditions | Shelf Life |
|---|---|---|
| Unrefined (Grade A) | Cool (below 25 C if possible), dark, airtight container | 18-24 months |
| Refined (Grade B/C) | Cool, dark, airtight container | 2-3 years |
| Whipped body butter | Cool, dark, airtight jar | 6-12 months (depends on carrier oil used) |
| Lip balm | Room temperature, closed container | 12-18 months |
| Deodorant stick | Room temperature | 6-12 months |

**Signs of rancidity:** Sour, acrid, or "off" smell replacing the nutty aroma; change in color to dark yellow or brown; bitter taste. Rancid shea butter should be discarded, as oxidized lipids can cause skin irritation and generate free radicals.

**Graininess fix:** If shea butter develops a grainy texture, melt it completely (above 76 C / 170 F to melt all polymorphic crystal forms), stir, then place in the freezer for rapid cooling. This forces crystallization in the desired beta-prime form, yielding a smooth texture.

## Cost & Sourcing

- **Unrefined shea butter (bulk):** $8-15 per 500 g; $15-30 per kg (online retailers, DIY cosmetic suppliers)
- **Unrefined, organic, fair trade:** $12-25 per 500 g (Better Shea Butter, Sky Organics, Baraka Shea Butter)
- **Refined shea butter:** $6-12 per 500 g (cosmetic ingredient suppliers)
- **East African (nilotica) shea butter:** $15-30 per 500 g (softer, creamier variant from *V. paradoxa* subsp. *nilotica*; higher oleic acid content)

When sourcing, look for:
- Certification: USDA Organic, Fair Trade, or cooperative-sourced
- Color: Ivory to yellow (not pure white, which indicates bleaching or adulteration)
- Aroma: Nutty, slightly smoky (not odorless or rancid)
- Country of origin: Ghana, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Nigeria are the largest producers
- Packaging: Opaque container to protect from light

## Regulatory Status

- **United States:** Not specifically regulated by FDA for cosmetic use; governed under general cosmetic safety regulations. GRAS for food use.
- **European Union:** Approved for cosmetic use (CosIng listing). Permitted as a cocoa butter equivalent (CBE) in chocolate at up to 5% (Directive 2000/36/EC).
- **INCI Name:** Butyrospermum Parkii (Shea) Butter
- **Allergen labeling:** Not required to be labeled as a tree nut allergen in the US or EU due to very low protein content. However, some manufacturers voluntarily label "contains tree nuts" as a precaution.

## Related Ingredients

- **Cocoa butter:** Similar melting point and triglyceride structure but lower unsaponifiable fraction (1-2%). Harder texture. More comedogenic (rating 4).
- **Mango butter:** Extracted from mango seed kernel. Similar fatty acid profile to shea, with lower unsaponifiable fraction. Lighter texture. Good substitute for those who want to avoid tree nuts entirely (mango is a drupe, not a tree nut).
- **Kokum butter:** From *Garcinia indica*. Very high stearic acid content, making it the firmest of the exotic butters. Used in lip balms and bar formulations.
- **[Tallow](/Ingredients/tallow.md):** Animal-derived alternative with similar fatty acid profile. Provides fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K.
- **[Coconut oil](/Ingredients/coconut-oil.md):** Often combined with shea butter in balm formulations. Provides lauric acid (antimicrobial). More comedogenic (rating 4).
