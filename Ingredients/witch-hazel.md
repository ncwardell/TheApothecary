# Witch Hazel

> A botanical extract from the bark and leaves of *Hamamelis virginiana* containing tannins and gallic acid, providing astringent, anti-inflammatory, and mild antimicrobial properties, widely used as a skin toner, deodorant base, and minor wound care agent.

## Overview

Witch hazel (*Hamamelis virginiana*) is a deciduous shrub or small tree native to the eastern woodlands of North America, notable for its unusual habit of blooming in late autumn or early winter — producing fragrant yellow flowers after its leaves have fallen. The common name "witch hazel" likely derives from the Middle English *wych* (meaning flexible or pliant), referring to the plant's supple branches, which were historically used as dowsing rods for finding water — though the plant has no actual connection to witchcraft.

Native American tribes, including the Mohegan, Potawatomi, and Osage peoples, used witch hazel bark and leaf preparations extensively for treating skin conditions, wounds, sore muscles, insect bites, and eye inflammations. European settlers quickly adopted these uses, and by the mid-19th century, witch hazel extract had become one of the most widely used natural remedies in American households — a position it continues to hold today.

The active compounds in witch hazel are primarily **tannins** (particularly hamamelitannin, a unique gallotannin specific to *Hamamelis* species) and **gallic acid**, which together provide the astringent, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties that make witch hazel useful. However, it is important to note that commercial witch hazel products vary significantly in their tannin content depending on the preparation method — steam-distilled witch hazel water (the most common form) contains very little tannin, while alcohol-based tinctures and bark decoctions retain much more.

In the DIY personal care context, witch hazel serves as an astringent skin toner, a roll-on deodorant base, a post-shave treatment, and a minor wound care agent.

## Chemical Composition & Structure

### Active Compounds

| Compound | Class | Properties |
|---|---|---|
| Hamamelitannin | Gallotannin (unique to Hamamelis) | Astringent, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, venoprotective |
| Gallic acid | Phenolic acid | Antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial |
| Catechins | Flavan-3-ols | Antioxidant, anti-inflammatory |
| Proanthocyanidins | Condensed tannins | Astringent, antioxidant, collagen-stabilizing |
| Safrole | Phenylpropanoid | Present in trace amounts; potential toxin (relevant for internal use only) |
| Volatile oils | Various terpenes | Contribute to aroma |

### Tannin Chemistry

Tannins are polyphenolic compounds that bind and precipitate proteins:

1. **Astringent effect**: Tannins bind to proteins in the superficial skin layers, causing mild contraction and tightening of tissue
2. **This protein binding**:
   - Reduces pore size appearance
   - Decreases minor bleeding (styptic effect)
   - Creates a protective protein-tannin layer on damaged skin
   - Reduces inflammation by inhibiting inflammatory mediator release
3. **Antimicrobial**: Tannins deprive microorganisms of iron (chelation) and disrupt their cell membranes

### Commercial Product Variation — Critical Distinction

| Product Type | Tannin Content | Alcohol Content | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Witch hazel distillate (e.g., Thayers Alcohol-Free) | Very low (tannins don't survive distillation) | 0% | Mild; primarily hydrating/refreshing |
| Standard witch hazel water (USP) | Very low | 14% (added as preservative) | Astringent from alcohol, not tannins |
| Witch hazel tincture (bark extract) | High | 15–50% | Strong astringent; genuine tannin activity |
| Witch hazel bark decoction (homemade) | Highest | 0% | Strongest tannin activity; short shelf life |

**Important**: Most drugstore "witch hazel" (Dickinson's, generic brands) is the steam distillate with 14% added alcohol. Its astringent effect comes primarily from the alcohol, not from tannins. For genuine tannin-based activity, use a tincture or alcohol-free extract that specifically states it contains bark/leaf extract (not just distillate).

## How It Is Produced / How to Make It from Scratch

### Commercial Steam Distillation (Most Common)

1. Twigs and bark of *H. virginiana* are harvested (primarily from wild or managed stands in Connecticut, which has a long history of witch hazel production)
2. Plant material is loaded into steam stills
3. Steam distillation extracts volatile compounds
4. The resulting distillate is witch hazel water
5. 14% ethanol is added as a preservative (USP standard)
6. **Note**: Most tannins are not volatile and do not survive steam distillation — the distillate contains primarily volatile components and very little tannin

### Tincture / Extract (Higher Tannin)

1. Bark and/or leaves are macerated in alcohol (ethanol or isopropanol) for weeks
2. Alcohol extracts tannins, gallic acid, and other polyphenols
3. The resulting tincture is rich in bioactive compounds
4. Some commercial products use this method and are labeled "witch hazel extract"

### Home Bark Decoction

1. Collect or purchase dried *H. virginiana* bark (available from herb suppliers)
2. Simmer 2 tbsp bark in 2 cups water for 15–20 minutes
3. Strain through fine cloth
4. Allow to cool
5. Store in refrigerator — use within 7–10 days (no preservative)
6. This produces the most tannin-rich preparation, closest to traditional use

### Home Tincture

1. Fill jar ½ full with dried witch hazel bark
2. Cover with 80-proof vodka
3. Seal, shake daily, steep 4–6 weeks
4. Strain and bottle
5. Shelf life: 1–2 years due to alcohol preservation

## Mechanism of Action

### Astringent

1. Tannins bind to proteins in the superficial layers of skin (stratum corneum)
2. This cross-linking causes mild tissue contraction
3. Result: temporary tightening of skin, reduced pore appearance, reduced minor oozing/bleeding
4. The effect is topical and temporary (hours)

### Anti-Inflammatory

- Hamamelitannin inhibits the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β)
- Gallic acid and proanthocyanidins scavenge reactive oxygen species (ROS)
- Suppresses phospholipase A₂ activity, reducing prostaglandin synthesis
- Clinically demonstrated to reduce UV-induced erythema (sunburn redness)

### Deodorant Base

When used as a roll-on deodorant vehicle:
- Mildly acidic pH (~4) creates unfavorable environment for odor bacteria
- Astringent action temporarily tightens pores (may slightly reduce perspiration)
- Tannins have mild antimicrobial activity
- Provides a light, non-greasy liquid base for essential oils and other actives

### Hemostatic (Styptic)

- Tannins precipitate blood proteins, promoting localized coagulation
- Effective for minor cuts, nicks, and razor burns
- Traditional use for post-shave treatment

## Applications & Uses

### In DIY Formulations

**Roll-On Deodorant Base (Primary DIY Use)**:
- 2 tsp witch hazel (alcohol-free extract)
- 2 tbsp magnesium hydroxide
- 1 tsp arrowroot powder
- 5–10 drops essential oil
- Mix in roller bottle; shake before use
- Witch hazel provides the liquid base with mild astringent/antimicrobial support

**Facial Toner**:
- Apply witch hazel (alcohol-free) to cotton pad, wipe over face after cleansing
- Restores mildly acidic pH
- For genuine tannin-based astringency, use a tincture or bark extract rather than standard distillate
- Can add 1–2 drops tea tree oil for acne-prone skin

**Post-Shave Treatment**:
- Apply witch hazel directly to freshly shaved skin
- Reduces irritation, soothes razor burn, stops minor bleeding from nicks
- Alcohol-containing formulations provide additional antiseptic action (but may sting)
- Alcohol-free is gentler for sensitive skin

**Minor Wound / Insect Bite Treatment**:
- Apply directly to minor cuts, scrapes, insect bites, poison ivy
- Astringent and anti-inflammatory
- Not a substitute for proper wound cleaning (saline or soap and water)

**Hemorrhoid Treatment**:
- Witch hazel pads (e.g., Tucks) are a standard OTC hemorrhoid treatment
- Astringent action reduces swelling and soothes irritation
- Can apply witch hazel to cotton pad for same effect

**Under-Eye Treatment**:
- Soak cotton pads in chilled witch hazel
- Apply to closed eyes for 10 minutes
- Astringent effect temporarily reduces puffiness

## Efficacy & Research

### Anti-Inflammatory

- Korting, H.C. et al. (1995) in *European Journal of Clinical Pharmacology* demonstrated that a witch hazel distillate cream suppressed UV-induced erythema comparably to 1% hydrocortisone cream, confirming clinically relevant anti-inflammatory activity.

### Skin Care

- Thring, T.S.A., Hili, P., & Naughton, D.P. (2011) in *Journal of Inflammation* found that *Hamamelis virginiana* extract exhibited significant anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activity in cell culture models.

### Venoprotective

- MacKay, D. (2001) in *Alternative Medicine Review* reviewed the evidence for *Hamamelis* in venous insufficiency and hemorrhoids, finding positive evidence for hamamelitannin's venoprotective and anti-edema effects.

### Antimicrobial

- Moderate antimicrobial activity documented against *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, and *C. albicans*
- Not as potent as tea tree oil or hydrogen peroxide as a standalone antimicrobial

## Safety, Warnings & Contraindications

### Alcohol Content

- Standard USP witch hazel water contains 14% alcohol
- This can be drying and irritating for sensitive skin
- **Alcohol-free** witch hazel (Thayers brand is the most common) is preferred for sensitive skin and facial use
- Choose alcohol-free for deodorant formulations (less irritation on shaved skin)

### Skin Sensitivity

- Generally very well tolerated
- Rare contact dermatitis from tannins or other plant compounds
- Patch test if using for the first time, especially on sensitive or broken skin

### Internal Use

- **Not recommended for internal use**
- Contains trace amounts of safrole (a potential carcinogen/hepatotoxin in large doses)
- Traditional internal use (as a tea) should be avoided
- FDA classifies witch hazel as an external-use OTC product only

### Eye Use

- Despite traditional use as an eye wash, witch hazel is not sterile and should not be applied directly to eyes
- Commercial eye products containing witch hazel are specifically formulated and sterilized

## Pros & Cons

### Advantages

- Gentle, well-tolerated astringent
- Anti-inflammatory (clinically demonstrated)
- Excellent liquid base for roll-on deodorant
- Effective post-shave treatment
- Reduces puffiness and minor irritation
- Long history of safe topical use
- Widely available and inexpensive
- Mildly acidic pH (good for skin)
- Alcohol-free options available

### Disadvantages / Limitations

- Most commercial products (distillates) contain minimal tannins — astringency comes from added alcohol
- Standard versions contain 14% alcohol (drying for sensitive skin)
- Not a strong antimicrobial (tea tree oil or H₂O₂ are better for disinfection)
- Astringent effects are temporary (hours)
- Not suitable for deep wounds or serious skin conditions
- "Natural" marketing can overstate benefits
- Alcohol-free versions may not preserve as long

## Storage & Shelf Life

- **Shelf life**: 2–3 years (commercial, sealed); 1 year after opening (alcohol-preserved)
- **Alcohol-free versions**: Shorter shelf life — check expiration date; use within 6–12 months of opening
- **Homemade decoction**: 7–10 days refrigerated (no preservative)
- **Homemade tincture**: 1–2 years (alcohol preserves)
- **Storage**: Room temperature (commercial); refrigerator extends freshness of alcohol-free versions

- **Avoid**: Products with added fragrances, parabens, or other unnecessary additives

## Regulatory Status

- **FDA (USA)**: Classified as an OTC skin protectant/astringent drug (21 CFR 347). Witch hazel is one of the few botanical ingredients with FDA OTC monograph status.
- **USP**: Monographed as "Hamamelis Water" — distillate containing 14% alcohol.
- **EU**: Listed in CosIng database. No restrictions on cosmetic use.
- **Health Canada**: Natural health product status.

## Related Ingredients

- **Magnesium Hydroxide** — Paired with witch hazel in roll-on deodorant formulations (witch hazel as liquid base, Mg(OH)₂ as active).
- **Arrowroot Powder** — Moisture absorber added to witch hazel-based deodorant for texture.
- **Tea Tree Oil** — Stronger antimicrobial; can be added to witch hazel for enhanced antiseptic toner.
- **Apple Cider Vinegar** — Alternative acidic toner with different active compounds (acetic acid vs. tannins).
- **Aloe Vera** — Complementary soothing agent; often paired with witch hazel in skincare.
