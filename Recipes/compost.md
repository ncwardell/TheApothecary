# Composting

> Turning kitchen scraps and yard waste into complete soil fertility for free — the most cost-effective replacement for commercial fertilizers and soil amendments.

## Overview

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Category | Garden & Home |
| Replaces | Bagged fertilizer (Miracle-Gro, Osmocote), potting soil, soil amendments, peat moss |
| Cost Per Use | Free (from waste you already produce) |

## What This Replaces & Why

Commercial fertilizers are manufactured using energy-intensive industrial processes. Synthetic nitrogen fertilizer (the Haber-Bosch process) consumes approximately 1-2% of global energy production. Bagged compost and potting soil are often peat-based, contributing to the destruction of irreplaceable peat bogs that took thousands of years to form and are critical carbon sinks.

Meanwhile, the average household sends 30-40% of its waste stream to landfill as organic matter — food scraps, yard trimmings, paper. In landfills, this organic matter decomposes anaerobically (without oxygen), producing methane, a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than CO₂ over a 20-year period. Organic waste in landfills is the third-largest source of anthropogenic methane emissions.

Composting intercepts this waste stream, converts it through aerobic decomposition into a nutrient-rich soil amendment, and eliminates the need for commercial fertilizers and soil amendments. It is one of the few environmental actions that simultaneously:

- Reduces waste going to landfill
- Eliminates methane emissions from that waste
- Replaces products whose manufacture has significant environmental impact
- Produces a superior product (compost feeds soil biology; synthetic fertilizer does not)
- Costs nothing

## Ingredients

### Core Ingredients

Composting does not reference specific ingredient files — the inputs are everyday waste materials categorized by their carbon-to-nitrogen ratio.

**Green Materials (Nitrogen-Rich, C:N ratio under 30:1):**

| Material | C:N Ratio | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Fruit and vegetable scraps | 15-25:1 | The bulk of kitchen compost input |
| Coffee grounds and filters | 20:1 | Excellent nitrogen source; slightly acidic |
| Fresh grass clippings | 15-25:1 | Add in thin layers to prevent matting |
| Fresh garden trimmings | 15-30:1 | Chop or shred for faster decomposition |
| Tea leaves and bags | 15-20:1 | Remove staples from bags; or use loose tea |
| Eggshells | N/A | Primarily calcium carbonate; slow to break down — crush finely |
| Fresh manure (herbivore only) | 5-25:1 | Horse, cow, chicken, rabbit — never cat, dog, or human |

**Brown Materials (Carbon-Rich, C:N ratio over 30:1):**

| Material | C:N Ratio | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dry leaves | 40-80:1 | The ideal brown material — collect in autumn |
| Cardboard (uncoated) | 300-500:1 | Tear into small pieces; remove tape and staples |
| Newspaper (black ink only) | 150-200:1 | Shred; modern soy-based inks are safe |
| Straw | 80-100:1 | Excellent bulking agent for airflow |
| Wood chips / sawdust | 200-750:1 | Use sparingly — very high carbon ties up nitrogen |
| Dryer lint (from natural fiber clothes) | Variable | Small amounts only |
| Paper towels and napkins (unbleached) | 150-200:1 | As long as not contaminated with cleaning chemicals |

### What NOT to Compost

| Material | Reason |
|---|---|
| Meat, fish, bones | Attract rodents and flies; produce foul odors during decomposition |
| Dairy products | Same as above |
| Oils and fats | Coat materials, reducing oxygen access; attract pests |
| Dog or cat feces | Contain parasites and pathogens not killed by typical compost temperatures |
| Diseased plants | Pathogens may survive composting and reinfect garden |
| Treated or painted wood | Contains preservative chemicals (arsenic, chromium in CCA-treated wood) |
| Coal or charcoal ash | Contains sulfur and heavy metals; wood ash is fine in small amounts |
| Glossy or coated paper | Plastic coatings do not decompose |
| Synthetic fabrics | Will not decompose; contaminate finished compost |

## Equipment Needed

**Minimal setup (passive composting):**
- A designated corner of the yard (no container needed)
- A pitchfork or garden fork for turning
- A kitchen compost pail or bucket for collecting scraps

**Standard setup:**
- Compost bin (purchased or DIY from pallets, wire mesh, or cinder blocks) — minimum 3 ft x 3 ft x 3 ft (1 cubic meter) for effective hot composting
- Garden fork or compost aerator tool
- Kitchen compost pail with lid
- Hose or watering can
- Thermometer (optional but useful for monitoring hot composting)

**Apartment/indoor setup (vermicomposting):**
- Worm bin (plastic storage tote with drilled ventilation holes, or commercial worm bin)
- Red wiggler worms (*Eisenia fetida*) — 1 lb per square foot of bin surface area
- Bedding material (shredded newspaper, cardboard, coconut coir)

## Method

### Standard Outdoor Composting (Hot/Active Method)

**The goal**: Maintain conditions that favor thermophilic (heat-loving) bacteria, which decompose material rapidly and generate temperatures of 55-65 degrees C (130-150 degrees F) — hot enough to kill weed seeds and pathogens.

1. **Choose a location.** A level, well-drained spot with partial shade is ideal. Full sun dries the pile too quickly; full shade stays too cool and wet. Place on bare soil (not concrete) so soil organisms can colonize the pile from below.

2. **Build the pile in layers.** Alternate green and brown materials in roughly equal volumes (by volume, not weight — browns are much lighter per volume than greens):
   - Start with a 4-6 inch layer of coarse brown material (sticks, straw) at the base for drainage and airflow.
   - Add a 2-4 inch layer of green material.
   - Add a 2-4 inch layer of brown material.
   - Repeat until the pile is at least 3 feet (1 meter) in each dimension.

3. **Moisten as you build.** Each layer should be damp like a wrung-out sponge — moist but not dripping. Dry materials decompose very slowly; waterlogged materials go anaerobic and smell terrible.

4. **Turn the pile regularly.** Use a garden fork to turn the entire pile, moving material from the outside to the center:
   - **Hot composting**: Turn every 3-5 days. The pile should heat up within 24-48 hours of building/turning. Temperature of 55-65 degrees C indicates active thermophilic decomposition.
   - **Moderate composting**: Turn every 1-2 weeks. Slower but less labor.
   - **Passive composting**: Turn monthly or not at all. Takes 6-12 months but requires minimal effort.

5. **Monitor moisture.** Squeeze a handful of material — it should feel like a damp sponge with no water dripping out. Add water if too dry; add dry brown material if too wet.

6. **The pile is finished when:**
   - It has reduced to 1/3-1/2 of its original volume
   - It is dark brown to black
   - It has an earthy, forest-floor smell (no sour or ammonia odors)
   - Individual input materials are no longer recognizable
   - It is ambient temperature (no longer generating heat)
   - Typical timeline: 2-3 months (hot method), 4-6 months (moderate), 6-12 months (passive)

7. **Cure the finished compost** for 2-4 weeks before use. Spread it out or leave it in an open pile to stabilize. Fresh compost that is still slightly active can temporarily tie up nitrogen in soil — curing prevents this.

### Vermicomposting (Worm Composting — Apartments)

For people without yards, worm composting is an odorless, compact, indoor alternative:

1. **Set up the bin.** Use a shallow, opaque plastic storage tote (10-20 gallon). Drill 1/4-inch holes in the lid and upper sides for ventilation. Drill a few small drainage holes in the bottom and place on a tray.

2. **Prepare bedding.** Fill the bin 2/3 full with moistened shredded newspaper, cardboard, or coconut coir. The bedding should be damp like a wrung-out sponge. Fluff it up — do not compact.

3. **Add worms.** Red wigglers (*Eisenia fetida*) are the standard composting worm — do not use garden earthworms. Start with 1 lb of worms per square foot of bin surface area. Purchase from vermiculture suppliers online or at garden centers.

4. **Feed the worms.** Bury food scraps under the bedding in different locations each feeding. Red wigglers eat roughly half their body weight per day. A 1 lb population processes about 3.5 lbs of food scraps per week.

5. **What to feed**: Fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, tea leaves, crushed eggshells, small amounts of bread or pasta.

6. **What NOT to feed**: Citrus (too acidic), onions and garlic (worms dislike), meat, dairy, oily foods, spicy foods.

7. **Harvest castings** every 2-3 months. Push finished material to one side of the bin, add fresh bedding and food to the other side. Over 1-2 weeks, worms will migrate to the food side, allowing you to harvest the finished castings (worm compost) from the other side.

8. **Worm castings** are one of the most nutrient-rich and biologically active composts available — excellent for houseplants, seed starting, and garden beds.

## The Science Behind It

### The Decomposition Process

Composting is controlled aerobic decomposition — the same process that breaks down organic matter on the forest floor, accelerated by managing conditions to favor the most efficient decomposers.

**Phase 1: Mesophilic (ambient to 40 degrees C)**
Mesophilic bacteria and fungi begin breaking down easily accessible sugars, starches, and proteins. The metabolic heat from their activity raises the temperature of the pile. This phase lasts 1-3 days.

**Phase 2: Thermophilic (40-70 degrees C)**
As temperature rises, thermophilic bacteria (Bacillus, Thermus, Actinomycetes) take over. These organisms break down more resistant materials — cellulose, hemicellulose, fats, and some lignin. This phase is the most active, with temperatures reaching 55-65 degrees C. At these temperatures, pathogenic bacteria, weed seeds, and many plant diseases are killed. This phase lasts 1-4 weeks with regular turning.

**Phase 3: Cooling and Curing**
As easily decomposed materials are consumed, microbial activity slows and the pile cools. Mesophilic organisms recolonize, along with larger decomposers — fungi, actinomycetes, protozoa, nematodes, mites, springtails, and eventually earthworms. These organisms further process the material and begin forming stable humic substances. This phase lasts 1-3 months.

**Phase 4: Mature Compost**
The finished product is humus — a complex mixture of stable organic molecules, mineral nutrients, and living microorganisms. Humus is dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling, and biologically rich.

### The Carbon-to-Nitrogen Ratio

The C:N ratio is the most important variable in composting. Microorganisms need carbon for energy and nitrogen for building proteins and reproducing. The ideal ratio for active composting is 25-30:1 (carbon to nitrogen by weight).

- **Too much carbon** (high C:N): Decomposition slows dramatically. Microorganisms cannot reproduce fast enough. The pile stays cool and takes months or years.
- **Too much nitrogen** (low C:N): Excess nitrogen is released as ammonia gas, creating a strong unpleasant smell and wasting a valuable nutrient.

In practice, mixing roughly equal volumes of green and brown materials achieves a C:N ratio in the optimal range, because greens are denser and wetter than browns.

### What Compost Provides to Soil

Unlike synthetic fertilizers, which provide only NPK (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium) in soluble form, compost improves soil in multiple ways simultaneously:

- **Nutrients**: All essential plant nutrients in slow-release organic form
- **Soil biology**: Billions of beneficial bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and nematodes per gram
- **Soil structure**: Improves drainage in clay soils; improves water retention in sandy soils
- **Cation exchange capacity**: Humic substances hold nutrient ions and release them to plant roots on demand
- **pH buffering**: Mature compost is near-neutral pH and buffers soil against acidification
- **Disease suppression**: Diverse soil biology outcompetes and suppresses plant pathogens

## Variations

- **Trench composting**: Dig a trench or hole in the garden, bury kitchen scraps directly, cover with 8-10 inches of soil. The simplest possible method. Material decomposes in place over 2-6 months. No bin, no turning, no smell.
- **Bokashi composting**: A Japanese anaerobic fermentation method using inoculated bran. Food waste (including meat and dairy) is layered with bokashi bran in a sealed bucket. After 2 weeks of fermentation, the pre-composted material is buried in soil to finish decomposition. Suitable for apartments.
- **Leaf mold**: Collect autumn leaves, pile them in a wire mesh enclosure, and wait 1-2 years. The resulting leaf mold is an excellent mulch and soil conditioner. Requires zero effort beyond initial collection.
- **Compost tea**: Steep a bag of finished compost in a bucket of water for 24-48 hours (optionally with an aquarium pump for aeration). The resulting "tea" is a liquid fertilizer rich in soluble nutrients and beneficial microorganisms. Apply as a soil drench or foliar spray.
- **Three-bin system**: Build three adjacent bins. Bin 1 receives fresh material, Bin 2 contains actively decomposing material turned from Bin 1, Bin 3 holds finished/curing compost. This provides a continuous supply of finished compost.

## Pros

- Completely free — made from waste you already produce
- Produces a superior product compared to synthetic fertilizers (feeds soil biology, not just plants)
- Dramatically improves soil structure in any soil type
- Reduces household waste by 30-40%
- Eliminates methane emissions from organic waste in landfills
- Replaces peat moss (whose extraction destroys irreplaceable bog ecosystems)
- Worm composting works in apartments with no outdoor space
- Finished compost suppresses plant diseases through biological competition
- Provides slow-release nutrients that do not burn plants (unlike synthetic fertilizers, which can cause fertilizer burn)
- Improves water retention in sandy soils and drainage in clay soils — simultaneously

## Cons & Limitations

- Requires space (at minimum a 3x3x3 foot area for outdoor composting)
- Requires ongoing attention — turning, moisture monitoring, adding materials
- Takes 2-6 months to produce finished compost (not instant)
- Can attract pests (rodents, flies) if meat, dairy, or oils are added, or if the pile is not managed properly
- Improperly managed piles can produce foul odors (usually from being too wet or having too much nitrogen)
- Cold climates slow decomposition significantly in winter
- Does not produce enough compost for large-scale agricultural needs from household waste alone
- Worm composting requires purchasing and maintaining a live worm population
- Some municipal bylaws restrict compost pile size or placement

## Tips & Troubleshooting

- **Pile smells like ammonia?** Too much nitrogen (greens). Add brown material (dry leaves, cardboard, straw) and turn the pile to incorporate.
- **Pile smells like rotten eggs?** The pile is anaerobic (too wet, too compacted, or not turned). Turn the pile immediately to introduce oxygen. Add dry brown material to absorb excess moisture.
- **Pile is not heating up?** Could be too small (needs to be at least 3x3x3 feet), too dry (add water), too much carbon (add greens), or not enough mass. In cold weather, insulate the pile with straw bales.
- **Fruit flies around the pile?** Bury food scraps under a layer of brown material. Fruit flies are annoying but harmless. A layer of straw or leaves on top eliminates them.
- **Rats attracted to the pile?** Avoid adding meat, dairy, oils, or cooked food. Use a bin with a bottom (hardware cloth/wire mesh floor) to prevent burrowing.
- **Composting in winter**: Decomposition slows but does not stop entirely. Continue adding material. In spring, the accumulated winter additions will decompose rapidly as temperatures rise.
- **Chop materials small.** The smaller the pieces, the faster they decompose. Shred leaves, tear cardboard, chop food scraps. Surface area is everything in decomposition.
- **The squeeze test**: Grab a handful of material and squeeze. A few drops of water should come out. If it streams, too wet. If no drops, too dry.

## Safety Notes

- **Bioaerosols**: Turning compost piles releases fungal spores and bacterial aerosols. People with compromised immune systems or chronic lung conditions should wear a dust mask when turning compost and avoid inhaling directly over the pile.
- **Aspergillus**: This common compost fungus can cause aspergillosis in immunocompromised individuals. Healthy individuals are not at risk from normal exposure.
- **Tetanus**: Use gloves when handling compost materials, especially if they contain rusty metal (nails in old wood, wire). Ensure tetanus vaccination is current.
- **Do not compost pet feces** (dog, cat): These can contain Toxoplasma, Toxocara, and other parasites that survive normal composting temperatures.
- **Treated wood**: Never compost CCA-treated (chromated copper arsenate) lumber or painted wood. The arsenic and other chemicals persist in compost and contaminate soil.
- **Finished compost pH**: Test the pH of finished compost before applying to acid-loving plants (blueberries, azaleas). Mature compost is typically pH 6.5-7.5 — near neutral.
