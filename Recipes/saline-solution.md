## Method

### Basic Isotonic Saline
1. **Prepare the water.** Use distilled water directly, or bring tap water to a rolling boil for 3-5 minutes and let cool to lukewarm. **CRITICAL: For nasal irrigation, you MUST use distilled or boiled-and-cooled water. Never use unboiled tap water in the nasal passages** (see Safety Notes for the serious reason why).
2. **Dissolve the salt.** Add 1/2 tsp non-iodized sea salt to 1 cup (240 ml) of the prepared water. Stir until completely dissolved.
3. **Add baking soda (optional).** If using for nasal irrigation, add 1/4 tsp baking soda and stir. This buffers the pH closer to body pH (7.4) and significantly reduces the stinging/burning sensation.
4. **Temperature check.** For nasal irrigation, the solution should be lukewarm -- body temperature or slightly below (about 98-100 degrees F). Test on the inside of your wrist like you would baby formula. Too hot burns the delicate nasal mucosa; too cold causes discomfort and vasoconstriction.
5. **Use immediately or store.** Use within 24 hours. Discard any unused solution after 24 hours and make fresh.

### For Nasal Irrigation (Neti Pot / Squeeze Bottle)
1. Fill your neti pot or squeeze bottle with the lukewarm saline solution.
2. Lean over a sink. Tilt your head to one side at about a 45-degree angle.
3. Insert the spout into the upper nostril. Pour gently (neti pot) or squeeze gently (bottle). The solution will flow through the nasal cavity and out the lower nostril.
4. Breathe through your mouth during the process.
5. Use approximately half the solution per nostril.
6. Gently blow your nose after each side to clear residual fluid.
7. Repeat 1-2 times daily during active sinus congestion, or as directed by a physician.

### For Wound Washing
1. Prepare isotonic saline as above (baking soda optional).
2. Pour or spray onto the wound to flush debris and bacteria. Do not apply pressure directly into deep wounds.
3. Pat the area dry with clean gauze.
4. Apply appropriate wound dressing.

### For Sore Throat Gargle
1. Prepare saline at a slightly higher concentration: 1 tsp salt per cup of warm water (hypertonic).
2. Gargle for 30 seconds.
3. Spit. Do not swallow.
4. Repeat 3-4 times daily.

## The Science Behind It
Saline solution works through straightforward physical chemistry:

**Isotonic principle:** Normal body fluids (blood, interstitial fluid, mucus) have a salt concentration of approximately 0.9% -- this is called "isotonic." A solution at this same concentration does not cause cells to swell (which happens with pure water due to osmosis) or shrink (which happens with concentrated salt solutions). This is why isotonic saline feels comfortable in the nose and on wounds, while pure water stings and concentrated salt burns.

**Nasal irrigation -- the evidence:** A 2007 Cochrane systematic review found that saline nasal irrigation improves symptoms of chronic rhinosinusitis. Subsequent studies have confirmed benefits for:
- Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) -- physically washes away allergens
- Post-nasal drip -- thins mucus and promotes drainage
- Cold/flu recovery -- clears congestion, reduces viral load on nasal mucosa
- Post-sinus surgery recovery -- standard medical recommendation

The mechanism is mechanical: irrigation physically removes mucus, allergens, bacteria, and inflammatory mediators from the nasal passages. It also restores mucociliary function (the "ciliary escalator" that moves mucus out of the sinuses) by removing thick, stagnant mucus.

**Wound washing:** Isotonic saline is the gold standard for wound irrigation in clinical settings. It cleans without damaging newly forming tissue (granulation tissue), unlike hydrogen peroxide or alcohol, which are cytotoxic and actually impede wound healing. The mechanical flushing action removes bacteria and debris. Studies show that wound irrigation with saline reduces infection rates.

**Sore throat gargle:** Hypertonic saline (saltier than body fluids) draws water out of swollen throat tissues through osmosis, reducing inflammation and pain. It also creates a hostile environment for bacteria. This is a time-tested remedy with genuine physiological basis.

## Variations
- **Hypertonic solution (decongestant):** Use 1 tsp salt per cup of water instead of 1/2 tsp. This higher concentration draws fluid out of swollen nasal tissues through osmosis, providing a mild decongestant effect. It will sting slightly more. Use for acute congestion.
- **Hypotonic solution (gentle rinse):** Use 1/4 tsp salt per cup of water. Less effective for decongestion but gentler, suitable for children or people who find isotonic saline uncomfortable.
- **Alkaline nasal rinse:** Add 1/2 tsp baking soda per cup (double the standard amount). Some ENT specialists recommend a more alkaline rinse for patients with thick, crusty mucus. The higher pH helps dissolve mucus.
- **Large batch wound wash:** Scale up to 1 quart (4 cups water + 2 tsp salt). Store in a clean squeeze bottle. Use within 24 hours. Useful for daily wound care where frequent irrigation is needed.
- **Gargle with honey:** After gargling with hypertonic saline, follow with 1 tsp raw honey dissolved in warm water and gargle again. Honey has established antimicrobial properties (due to hydrogen peroxide production, methylglyoxal in manuka honey, and low water activity). A 2020 BMJ systematic review found honey superior to usual care for upper respiratory symptoms.

## Cons & Limitations
- **24-hour shelf life.** Must make fresh daily. Cannot be stored long-term without preservatives. This is the tradeoff for avoiding preservatives.
- **Requires distilled or boiled water for nasal use.** This is a non-negotiable safety requirement. There is no shortcut (see Safety Notes).
- **Learning curve for neti pot.** Nasal irrigation technique takes a few tries to get comfortable with. Initial attempts may feel strange or cause coughing.
- **Measurement precision matters.** Too much salt burns; too little swells tissues. The 1/2 tsp per cup ratio is important. Use proper measuring spoons, not a pinch-and-guess approach.
- **Not sterile.** Adequate for home nasal irrigation and minor wound care but not suitable for surgical wounds, IV use, or contact lens direct storage (use commercial sterile saline for contacts).

## Tips & Troubleshooting
- **Solution stings the nose:** Either the water is too hot, the salt concentration is wrong (too high or too low), or you skipped the baking soda buffer. Remake with exact measurements and add baking soda.
- **Ear pressure or popping:** You are irrigating too forcefully or tilting your head too far. Use gentler pressure and keep head tilt at 45 degrees, not fully sideways.
- **Water goes down the throat:** Tilt your head slightly more forward (chin toward chest). Open your mouth and breathe through it. Say "ahhh" to raise the soft palate, which blocks the throat.
- **Solution tastes wrong:** You may have used iodized salt (metallic taste) or salt with anti-caking agents. Use pure non-iodized sea salt or pure pickling salt.
- **Neti pot feels unsanitary:** Clean your neti pot with hot soapy water after each use. Run it through the dishwasher weekly. Ceramic and stainless steel neti pots are more sanitary than plastic.
- **How to know if the concentration is right:** 0.9% isotonic saline should taste about as salty as tears. If it tastes saltier than tears, it is hypertonic. If it tastes less salty, it is hypotonic.

## Safety Notes
- **CRITICAL: Use only distilled water or water that has been boiled for 3-5 minutes and cooled for nasal irrigation.** Unboiled tap water can contain *Naegleria fowleri*, a free-living amoeba that, while harmless if swallowed, can cause primary amebic meningoencephalitis (PAM) -- a nearly always fatal brain infection -- if it enters the nasal passages and reaches the brain via the olfactory nerve. This is extremely rare but has occurred from neti pot use with tap water. Boiling kills *Naegleria fowleri*. Distilled water is also safe. There are no exceptions to this rule.
- Use non-iodized salt without anti-caking agents. Iodine and additives can irritate nasal mucosa.
- Do not share neti pots or irrigation devices between people.
- Stop nasal irrigation and consult a physician if you develop ear pain, nosebleeds, or signs of infection (fever, green/yellow discharge, facial pain).
- For wound care: saline irrigation is for superficial wounds. Deep, puncture, or heavily contaminated wounds require professional medical evaluation.
- This is not suitable for intravenous use. Homemade saline is not sterile and contains no pyrogen testing.
