# Complete Laundry System
> A bulk-powder laundry system covering detergent, softener, and stain treatment at a fraction of commercial cost using four core ingredients.

## Overview
| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Category | Laundry |
| Replaces | Laundry detergent + fabric softener + dryer sheets + stain removers ($0.50+/load) |
| Cost Per Use | ~$0.04/load |
| Shelf Life | Indefinite (dry powder) |
| Effectiveness vs Commercial | 7/10 |
| Difficulty | Easy |

## What This Replaces & Why
A typical household spends $150-300/year on laundry products: detergent, fabric softener, dryer sheets, stain sprays, and specialty treatments. Commercial laundry detergent contains roughly seven categories of ingredients: surfactants (to lift dirt), water softeners/builders (to handle hard water), pH buffers (to optimize cleaning), enzymes (to break down proteins and starches), optical brighteners (to make clothes appear whiter under UV), fragrances, and dyes. This DIY system covers four of those seven categories effectively for about $0.04 per load. The one genuine gap is enzymes -- more on that below.

## Ingredients

### Core Ingredients
| Ingredient | Amount | Role |
|---|---|---|
| [Washing Soda](../Ingredients/washing-soda.md) | 1 cup | Water softener and alkaline builder; precipitates calcium and magnesium ions; primary cleaning agent |
| [Baking Soda](../Ingredients/baking-soda.md) | 1 cup | pH buffer; deodorizer; milder alkali that supplements washing soda |
| [Borax](../Ingredients/borax.md) | 1 cup | pH buffer; water softener; mild bleaching/whitening agent; antifungal |
| [White Vinegar](../Ingredients/white-vinegar.md) | 1/2 cup in rinse cycle | Fabric softener replacement; dissolves detergent residue; restores fiber flexibility |

### Optional Ingredients
| Ingredient | Amount | Role |
|---|---|---|
| [Castile Soap](../Ingredients/castile-soap.md) | 1 bar, grated (or 1/4 cup liquid) | True soap surfactant; provides actual dirt-lifting lather |
| [Ammonia](../Ingredients/ammonia.md) | 1/4 cup per load (replaces borax) | Excellent for protein stains (blood, sweat, grass); volatile so leaves no residue |
| [Citric Acid](../Ingredients/citric-acid.md) | 1/2 cup in rinse cycle (replaces vinegar) | Odorless acidic rinse; softens fabric; removes soap residue |
| [Hydrogen Peroxide](../Ingredients/hydrogen-peroxide.md) | 1/2 cup per load (3% solution) | Oxygen bleach alternative; safe color-brightener; whitens without chlorine |
| [Oxalic Acid](../Ingredients/oxalic-acid.md) | 1 tbsp dissolved in water, applied to stain | Rust stain and tannin stain remover (tea, coffee, wine); specialty treatment |

## Equipment Needed
- Large mixing bowl or bucket
- Cheese grater (if using bar soap)
- Airtight storage container (large, holds several pounds of powder)
- Measuring spoons (1-2 tbsp per load)
- Optional: food processor (to grind grated soap into finer powder)

## Method

### Making the Base Detergent Powder
1. **Grate the bar soap** (if using). Use a standard cheese grater or food processor to reduce one bar of castile soap to fine shreds. The finer the shreds, the better they dissolve in water.
2. **Combine dry ingredients.** In a large bowl, mix 1 cup washing soda, 1 cup baking soda, and 1 cup borax. If using grated bar soap, add it now.
3. **Mix thoroughly.** Stir or shake until all ingredients are uniformly distributed. Break up any clumps of washing soda.
4. **Store.** Transfer to an airtight container. Label with the date and "Laundry Powder - 1-2 tbsp per load."

### Using the System
1. **Add detergent.** Add 1-2 tbsp of powder directly to the washing machine drum before adding clothes. Use 1 tbsp for normal loads and 2 tbsp for heavily soiled loads. For HE (high-efficiency) machines, 1 tbsp is usually sufficient -- these machines use less water.
2. **Wash normally.** Run on your preferred cycle and temperature. Hot water improves cleaning performance for whites and heavily soiled items. Cold water is fine for everyday loads.
3. **Add vinegar rinse.** Pour 1/2 cup white vinegar into the fabric softener dispenser or add manually at the start of the rinse cycle. The vinegar neutralizes alkaline detergent residue, softens fibers, and eliminates any residual odor.
4. **Dry as usual.** Tumble dry or line dry. If you want the static-reduction effect of dryer sheets, toss a ball of aluminum foil or wool dryer balls into the dryer.

### Stain Treatment Protocols
- **Protein stains (blood, egg, sweat, grass):** Soak in cold water with 1/4 cup ammonia for 30 minutes before washing. Never use hot water on protein stains -- heat denatures the protein and sets it permanently.
- **Grease/oil stains:** Rub a small amount of liquid castile soap directly into the stain. Let sit 15 minutes. Wash with 2 tbsp detergent powder in hot water.
- **Rust stains:** Dissolve 1 tbsp oxalic acid in 1 cup hot water. Apply to the stain with a cloth. Let sit 10-15 minutes. Rinse and wash normally. Do not use chlorine bleach on rust stains -- it sets them.
- **Tannin stains (tea, coffee, wine):** Same oxalic acid treatment as rust stains. Alternatively, pour boiling water through the stain from a height of 12 inches (the force helps push the tannin through the fibers).
- **Yellowed whites:** Soak in a solution of 1/2 cup hydrogen peroxide per gallon of warm water for 1-2 hours. Wash normally. This is gentler than chlorine bleach and does not weaken fibers as aggressively.
- **General tough stains:** Make a paste of washing soda and water. Apply to stain, let sit 30 minutes, then wash.

## The Science Behind It
Commercial laundry detergent contains approximately seven functional categories of ingredients. Here is how this DIY system addresses each:

| Function | Commercial Ingredient | DIY Equivalent | Covered? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Surfactant (lifts dirt) | Linear alkylbenzene sulfonate (LAS) | Castile soap (sodium oleate) | Partially |
| Water softener/builder | Sodium tripolyphosphate, zeolites | Washing soda, borax | Yes |
| pH buffer | Various carbonates | Baking soda, washing soda | Yes |
| Enzymes (break down stains) | Protease, lipase, amylase, cellulase | None | No |
| Optical brighteners | Stilbene derivatives | None | No |
| Fragrance | Synthetic musks, limonene | None (or essential oils) | Optional |
| Dye | Various | None | Not needed |

**What the DIY system does well (4/7):**

*Water softening* is arguably the most important function of laundry detergent, and washing soda excels at it. Hard water contains dissolved calcium and magnesium ions that react with soap to form insoluble "soap scum" (calcium stearate) that deposits on fabrics, making them stiff and gray. Washing soda precipitates these ions out of solution: Na2CO3 + Ca2+ -> CaCO3 + 2Na+. With the hard water ions removed, even a small amount of soap works effectively.

*pH buffering* is handled by the combination of baking soda (pH ~8.3) and washing soda (pH ~11.5), which maintain an alkaline wash environment that saponifies oils and fats on fabric.

**The genuine gap -- enzymes:**

Enzymes are the one category where commercial detergent genuinely outperforms DIY. Protease breaks down protein stains (blood, grass, body soil). Lipase breaks down fat stains (food grease, body oil). Amylase breaks down starch stains (food, pasta water). Cellulase smooths cotton fibers, reducing pilling. Without these enzymes, the DIY system relies solely on chemical cleaning (alkaline hydrolysis), which works but is slower and less complete on biological stains. Pre-soaking and stain-specific treatments (ammonia for protein, castile soap for grease) partially compensate.

## Variations
- **Borax-free version:** Some people prefer to avoid borax (though toxicity concerns at laundry concentrations are not well-supported). Replace borax with an additional cup of washing soda. The system loses some whitening and antifungal benefit but still cleans effectively.
- **Liquid version:** Dissolve 2 tbsp detergent powder in 1 quart of hot water. Stir until dissolved. Store in a bottle and use 1/4 cup per load. This dissolves faster in cold water cycles. Note: liquid version without preservatives should be used within 2-4 weeks.
- **Scented version:** Add 10-15 drops of lavender, lemon, or eucalyptus essential oil per batch of dry powder. Mix thoroughly. Essential oils in the dryer are not recommended as they are flammable.
- **HE machine formula:** Use 1 tbsp powder per load (HE machines use much less water). If suds are an issue, reduce or eliminate the grated soap component -- HE machines need low-sudsing detergent.
- **Cloth diaper version:** Omit castile soap (soap residue reduces absorbency in cloth diapers). Use washing soda + borax only, with a double vinegar rinse. Add 1/4 cup hydrogen peroxide for sanitization.

## Effectiveness Assessment
For everyday laundry -- t-shirts, jeans, sheets, towels -- this system works well. Clothes come out clean, fresh-smelling (or neutral-smelling), and soft (with the vinegar rinse).

**What it does better:** Dramatically cheaper. No synthetic fragrances that cause skin irritation and headaches. No optical brighteners (which are essentially UV-reactive dyes that do not actually clean but make clothes appear whiter under fluorescent light). No fabric softener coating (commercial softeners deposit a thin layer of quaternary ammonium compounds on fibers, which reduces absorbency in towels and can irritate sensitive skin).

**What it does worse:** Tough stains require pre-treatment rather than being handled automatically by enzymes. Heavily soiled work clothes may need a double wash or pre-soak. Clothes may not appear as bright-white as with commercial detergent (this is largely the optical brighteners, which are cosmetic rather than functional). In very hard water areas (>180 ppm), you may need to increase washing soda or add a water softener to the machine.

## Pros
- Costs ~$0.04/load versus $0.25-0.50+ for commercial detergent
- No synthetic fragrances, dyes, or optical brighteners
- Ingredients available at any grocery store
- Simple to make and store (indefinite shelf life)
- Vinegar rinse genuinely softens fabric without coating fibers
- Works in standard and HE machines
- Can be customized for specific needs (sensitive skin, cloth diapers, etc.)

## Cons & Limitations
- **No enzymes.** This is the most significant limitation. Protein and starch stains require manual pre-treatment. If enzymatic cleaning is important to you, you can purchase enzyme presoak powder (like Biz) and add 1 tbsp per load -- this combines the best of both approaches.
- **No optical brighteners.** Whites may gradually appear less bright compared to commercial detergent. This is cosmetic, not functional. Hydrogen peroxide soak periodically helps.
- **Grated soap dissolves slowly in cold water.** If you wash primarily in cold water, either pre-dissolve the powder in hot water before adding to the machine, or use liquid castile soap instead of bar soap.
- **Vinegar smell in rinse.** It dissipates completely during drying, but some people notice it on wet clothes. Citric acid is an odorless alternative.
- **Not pre-measured.** You need to scoop powder per load rather than tossing in a pod. Minor inconvenience but real.

## Tips & Troubleshooting
- **Clothes feel stiff:** You are either skipping the vinegar rinse or you have very hard water. Increase vinegar to 3/4 cup or switch to citric acid rinse (1/2 cup dissolved in water).
- **White residue on dark clothes:** Too much powder for the water volume. Reduce to 1 tbsp per load. Ensure powder is fully dissolved -- pre-dissolve in warm water if needed.
- **Machine smells musty:** Run an empty hot cycle with 2 cups white vinegar to clean the drum and hoses. Leave the door open between washes to dry.
- **Soap clumps in powder:** Grate the soap more finely or pulse in a food processor. Alternatively, switch to liquid castile soap added separately.
- **Ammonia safety:** NEVER mix ammonia with chlorine bleach. This produces chloramine gas, which is extremely toxic. If you use ammonia for protein stain treatment, ensure no bleach is present in the wash.
- **Want commercial-grade cleaning occasionally:** Keep a box of enzyme presoak (like Biz or OxiClean) for occasional use on heavily stained loads. Using it selectively keeps costs low while covering the enzyme gap.

## Safety Notes
- **NEVER mix ammonia with bleach.** Chloramine gas is toxic and can be lethal in enclosed spaces. This is the most important safety rule in laundry chemistry.
- **Washing soda is mildly caustic.** Wear gloves when handling concentrated amounts. Keep away from eyes.
- **Borax is toxic if ingested in quantity.** Store away from children and pets. At laundry concentrations (1-2 tbsp per 15+ gallons of water), residue on clothing is negligible.
- **Oxalic acid is toxic.** Wear gloves when handling. Do not inhale powder. Store separately from other ingredients and label clearly.
- **Hydrogen peroxide (3%) is generally safe** but can bleach colored fabrics. Test on an inconspicuous area first.
- **Vinegar in the fabric softener dispenser** is safe for all washing machines, including HE models. It does not damage rubber seals at 5% acidity.
