## What This Replaces & Why
This replaces oral magnesium supplements and commercial magnesium oil sprays. Magnesium deficiency is estimated to affect 50-80% of Americans according to some analyses, largely because modern agricultural practices have depleted soil magnesium, and processed food diets do not replace it. Oral magnesium supplements work but can cause gastrointestinal distress (particularly magnesium citrate and oxide, which are also laxatives). Transdermal application bypasses the GI tract entirely. Commercial magnesium oil sprays are simply magnesium chloride dissolved in water -- the exact same product you can make for a fraction of the price. A $15 bag of magnesium chloride flakes produces the equivalent of 10-15 bottles of commercial spray.

## Equipment Needed
- Glass measuring cup or small saucepan
- Glass spray bottle (4-8 oz)
- Funnel (optional, for neat pouring)
- Measuring cups

## Method
1. **Boil water.** Bring 1/2 cup distilled water to a boil. Boiling ensures the water is sterile and helps the flakes dissolve faster.
2. **Dissolve flakes.** Remove from heat. Pour the boiling water over 1/2 cup magnesium chloride flakes in a glass measuring cup or bowl. Stir until completely dissolved. The solution will be clear and slightly oily to the touch (hence the name "magnesium oil," though it contains no actual oil).
3. **Cool.** Let the solution cool to room temperature. This takes 15-20 minutes.
4. **Add optional ingredients.** If using aloe vera gel, stir it in now. If using lavender essential oil, add 5-10 drops and stir.
5. **Transfer.** Pour into a clean glass spray bottle using a funnel if needed. Label with the date.
6. **To use:** Spray 5-10 sprays onto skin (arms, legs, abdomen, or feet -- avoid freshly shaved skin and broken skin). Massage in briefly. Allow 20-30 minutes for absorption before showering. Best applied before bed. A slight tingling or stinging sensation is normal, especially the first few applications. This diminishes with regular use.

## The Science Behind It
Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the human body, including energy production (ATP synthesis), protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, blood glucose control, and blood pressure regulation. It is the fourth most abundant mineral in the body and is essential for bone health (60% of body magnesium is in bones), cardiovascular function, and nervous system regulation.

**Why deficiency is so common:** Modern agriculture has depleted soil magnesium significantly. Refined grains lose 80-97% of their magnesium during processing. Water treatment removes magnesium that people historically got from drinking water. Stress, alcohol, caffeine, and certain medications (proton pump inhibitors, diuretics) increase magnesium excretion.

**The transdermal question:** The central claim of magnesium oil is that magnesium can be absorbed through the skin. This is plausible -- the skin is not an impermeable barrier, and magnesium chloride is a small, water-soluble molecule. A 2017 NIH-supported pilot study found that participants using transdermal magnesium for 12 weeks showed increased serum magnesium levels compared to controls. However, the evidence base remains limited. A 2017 systematic review concluded that transdermal magnesium absorption is "promising but requires more rigorous study."

What is well-established:
- Magnesium deficiency is common and has real health consequences
- Oral magnesium supplementation reliably raises serum levels but causes GI side effects in many people
- Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate) baths have been used therapeutically for centuries with reported benefits
- Topical magnesium chloride application produces measurable changes in serum magnesium in at least one controlled study

What is not yet proven:
- The exact rate and amount of transdermal absorption
- Whether transdermal delivery is as effective as oral supplementation
- Optimal dosing for transdermal application

**Practical recommendation:** Use magnesium oil spray as a complement to dietary magnesium sources (dark leafy greens, nuts, seeds, whole grains, dark chocolate), not as a sole source. If you have a confirmed deficiency, oral supplementation under medical guidance is the evidence-backed approach. Transdermal application is a reasonable adjunct with minimal risk.

## Variations
- **Magnesium body butter:** Melt 1/4 cup shea butter and 1/4 cup coconut oil. Cool until semi-solid. Whip with a hand mixer. Slowly drizzle in 2 tbsp magnesium oil while whipping. This produces a moisturizing cream with magnesium content. The fat base may slow absorption but reduces the tingling sensation entirely.
- **Magnesium foot soak:** Dissolve 1 cup magnesium chloride flakes in a basin of warm water. Soak feet for 20-30 minutes. The soles of the feet have thick skin with numerous sweat glands, making them a practical absorption site. More relaxing than spraying.
- **Sleep spray:** Add 10 drops lavender essential oil and 5 drops chamomile essential oil to the base spray. Apply to the soles of the feet before bed. Magnesium's role in GABA receptor activation supports relaxation, and the essential oils add aromatherapy benefit.
- **Athletic recovery spray:** Use a higher concentration (3/4 cup flakes to 1/2 cup water). Apply to muscles after exercise. Magnesium plays a direct role in muscle relaxation (it competes with calcium at the neuromuscular junction). Higher concentration will tingle more.
- **Sensitive skin version:** Dilute the standard spray 1:1 with additional distilled water and add 1 tbsp aloe vera gel. This halves the magnesium concentration but eliminates tingling for people with sensitive skin.

## Effectiveness Assessment
The honest assessment is that this product probably works but the evidence is not yet definitive. Many users report subjective benefits: better sleep, reduced muscle cramps, improved relaxation, and less restless legs syndrome. These reports are consistent with what you would expect from correcting a magnesium deficit. However, the transdermal absorption pathway is less proven than oral supplementation.

**What it does better:** No GI side effects (no diarrhea, no nausea). Extremely cheap (~$0.05/spray vs $0.30-0.50 for commercial spray). Can be applied directly to sore muscles for targeted relief. No fillers, binders, or additives found in oral supplements. Easy to make.

**What it does worse:** Absorption rate is uncertain. Tingles/stings on application (annoying, not harmful). Cannot deliver precise dosing the way an oral supplement can. Not suitable as sole treatment for diagnosed deficiency. Limited peer-reviewed evidence compared to decades of oral supplementation data.

## Cons & Limitations
- **Transdermal absorption evidence is limited.** While promising, it is not as well-established as oral supplementation. The 2017 NIH pilot study is encouraging but small.
- **Tingling/stinging sensation.** Most users experience mild to moderate tingling or itching upon application, especially during the first 1-2 weeks of use. This is the magnesium chloride interacting with skin and is not harmful, but it is unpleasant for some people.
- **Cannot replace dietary sources.** Whole food magnesium sources (dark leafy greens, pumpkin seeds, almonds, dark chocolate, avocados) should remain the foundation of magnesium intake.
- **Leaves a slightly sticky residue.** The mineral-rich solution can feel tacky on skin until fully absorbed. Washing off after 20-30 minutes removes the residue while retaining the absorbed magnesium.
- **Not appropriate for open wounds or broken skin.** Magnesium chloride on cuts or abrasions will sting intensely.

## Tips & Troubleshooting
- **Tingling is too intense:** Dilute the spray 1:1 with more distilled water. Apply to less sensitive areas (soles of feet, calves) rather than inner arms or torso. The tingling typically decreases after 1-2 weeks of regular use as skin adapts.
- **Sticky feeling:** Apply before bed and let absorb overnight. Or wash off after 20-30 minutes -- most absorption occurs in this window.
- **Spray nozzle clogs:** Mineral deposits can clog the spray mechanism. Rinse the nozzle with warm water periodically. Use a glass spray bottle with a plastic nozzle (avoid metal components that may corrode).
- **Flakes won't dissolve:** Use truly boiling water and stir vigorously. Ensure you are using magnesium chloride flakes (not Epsom salt, which is magnesium sulfate and has different solubility).
- **Want to maximize absorption:** Apply after a warm shower when pores are open. Massage the area to increase blood flow. The soles of the feet and inner forearms are considered good absorption sites.
- **Pair with dietary magnesium:** Best food sources include pumpkin seeds (37% DV per ounce), spinach (19% DV per cup cooked), almonds (19% DV per ounce), dark chocolate 70%+ (15% DV per ounce), and avocado (14% DV per avocado).

## Safety Notes
- This product is for external use only. Do not ingest.
- Avoid contact with eyes, mucous membranes, and broken skin.
- If you are taking medications (especially antibiotics, diuretics, or heart medications), consult a healthcare provider before using supplemental magnesium in any form. Magnesium interacts with several drug classes.
- People with kidney disease should not use supplemental magnesium without medical supervision. The kidneys regulate magnesium excretion, and impaired kidney function can lead to dangerous magnesium accumulation (hypermagnesemia).
- If you experience symptoms of magnesium excess (nausea, facial flushing, lethargy, muscle weakness), discontinue use and consult a physician.
- This is a supplement, not a medication. It is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.
