## What This Replaces & Why

Commercial whitening strips contain hydrogen peroxide or carbamide peroxide (which breaks down into hydrogen peroxide) as the active ingredient. A box of Crest Whitestrips costs $30-50 and contains enough strips for 10-20 treatments. A bottle of 3% hydrogen peroxide costs $1-2 from any pharmacy and provides hundreds of rinses.

The mechanism is identical: hydrogen peroxide penetrates the enamel surface and oxidizes chromophore (color-producing) molecules embedded in the tooth structure. The difference is concentration and contact time. Commercial strips use 10-14% peroxide held against teeth for 30+ minutes. This rinse uses 1.5% (3% diluted by half) for 60 seconds. The result is slower, more gradual whitening — but also significantly less sensitivity and enamel risk.

Whitening toothpastes, meanwhile, mostly rely on abrasives rather than peroxide. They sand away surface stains but cannot change the intrinsic color of teeth. This rinse addresses both surface and sub-surface staining through chemical oxidation.

## Equipment Needed
- A small cup or shot glass for mixing
- A measuring spoon (tablespoon)

## Method

1. **Mix** — Pour 1 tablespoon of 3% hydrogen peroxide into a small cup. Add 1 tablespoon of water. Stir briefly.
2. **Swish** — Take the mixture into your mouth and swish vigorously for 60 seconds. Push the liquid through your teeth and across all surfaces. Do not gargle.
3. **Spit** — Spit the rinse into the sink. Do not swallow.
4. **Rinse** — Rinse your mouth with plain water.
5. **Brush** — Follow with normal brushing if desired.

### Schedule
- 2-3 times per week maximum. Do not use daily.
- Use after brushing for maximum contact with clean tooth surfaces, or before brushing to loosen stains.
- Allow at least one rest day between sessions.
- Cycle: Use for 2-3 weeks, then take a 1-week break.

## The Science Behind It

Tooth discoloration occurs in two forms:

- **Extrinsic stains**: Surface deposits from coffee, tea, wine, tobacco, and certain foods. These sit on or just within the enamel surface.
- **Intrinsic stains**: Chromophore molecules embedded deeper within the enamel and dentin. These cause the yellowish appearance that brushing alone cannot address.

Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) works through oxidation:

1. H2O2 penetrates the porous enamel structure (enamel is not a solid barrier — it has microscopic rods with inter-rod spaces).
2. Inside the enamel, H2O2 decomposes into water and reactive oxygen species (free radicals).
3. These free radicals react with the conjugated double-bond systems in chromophore molecules, breaking them into smaller, colorless compounds.
4. The result is a lighter appearance as the stain molecules are chemically dismantled.

At 1.5% concentration (3% diluted 1:1), the whitening effect is gradual but real. Studies show measurable shade improvement over 2-4 weeks of regular use. The lower concentration significantly reduces the risk of enamel demineralization and tooth sensitivity compared to higher-concentration professional treatments.

Hydrogen peroxide also has antimicrobial properties, killing anaerobic bacteria responsible for bad breath and some periodontal pathogens.

## Variations

- **Baking soda paste boost** — Mix a small amount of [Baking Soda](../Ingredients/baking-soda.md) with hydrogen peroxide to form a paste. Apply to teeth with a finger or soft brush, leave for 2 minutes, rinse. Use once per week maximum. The combination provides both chemical and mechanical whitening.
- **Extended contact method** — Dip a cotton swab in the 1:1 mixture and apply directly to stained teeth. Hold for 1-2 minutes. Allows targeted treatment of specific stains.
- **Pre-brush rinse** — Swish with the diluted peroxide before brushing. The peroxide loosens stains that the toothbrush then removes mechanically.

## Tips & Troubleshooting

- **Foaming in the mouth**: This is normal. Hydrogen peroxide reacts with catalase enzyme in saliva, producing oxygen bubbles. It means the peroxide is active and working.
- **Sensitivity after use**: You are using it too frequently. Reduce to once per week and ensure you are diluting properly (1:1 with water). If sensitivity persists, discontinue for 2 weeks.
- **No visible results after 2 weeks**: Ensure your peroxide is not expired or degraded. Test by pouring a small amount on a cut potato — it should fizz vigorously. If no fizz, the peroxide has broken down. Buy a fresh bottle.
- **Gum irritation**: The peroxide may be contacting inflamed or broken gum tissue. If you have active gum disease, address that first before whitening.
- **Storing peroxide**: Keep in the original brown or opaque bottle, in a cool, dark place. Light accelerates decomposition. Do not transfer to a clear container.

## Safety Notes
- **Never use concentrations above 3%** for oral rinsing. Food-grade 35% hydrogen peroxide is extremely dangerous if used undiluted — it can cause chemical burns to oral tissue and, if swallowed, can be life-threatening.
- **Do not swallow.** While small amounts of diluted 1.5% peroxide are not dangerous, intentional ingestion should be avoided.
- **Stop if you experience persistent pain or sensitivity.** Some mild tingling is normal; sharp pain is not.
- **Not recommended during pregnancy** without consulting a healthcare provider.
- **Children under 12** should not use peroxide rinses due to swallowing risk.
- **Do not use on damaged teeth** — teeth with cracks, cavities, or exposed dentin will experience pain and potential pulp damage from peroxide penetration.
- **Consult your dentist** before starting any whitening regimen if you have existing dental work, gum disease, or sensitive teeth.
