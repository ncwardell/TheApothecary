## What This Replaces & Why
Walk down the cleaning aisle of any grocery store and you will see dozens of products: surface spray, glass cleaner, degreaser, bathroom cleaner, oven cleaner, toilet bowl cleaner, lime/calcium/rust remover, stainless steel spray. Each has different branding, different fragrance, different price. But strip away the marketing and nearly every cleaning product falls into one of two categories: acid or base. That is all cleaning chemistry is. Acids dissolve mineral deposits (lime, calcium, rust, hard water). Bases dissolve organic grime (grease, oil, food, soap scum). Five bulk ingredients -- two acids, one base, one oxidizer, and one surfactant -- replace the entire shelf.

## Method

### The Fundamental Rule: Acid OR Base, Never Both Together
Every cleaning job requires either an acid solution or a base solution. Determine which one based on what you are cleaning:

**Use ACID (vinegar or citric acid) for:**
- Hard water spots
- Lime/calcium deposits on faucets
- Mineral buildup in kettles and coffee makers
- Soap scum in showers
- Toilet bowl rings
- Glass streaks
- Stainless steel polish (wipe with grain)

**Use BASE (washing soda) for:**
- Kitchen grease
- Stovetop grime
- Oven cleaning
- Sticky residue
- Floor cleaning
- General surface cleaning
- Laundry pre-soak

### Preparing the Solutions

**Acid spray (general purpose):**
1. Fill a spray bottle halfway with white vinegar.
2. Fill the rest with water.
3. Optional: add 5-10 drops tea tree oil.
4. Label clearly: "ACID - Vinegar Solution."

**Strong acid (descaling):**
1. Dissolve 2 tbsp citric acid in 1 quart of warm water.
2. Use for kettles, coffee makers, dishwashers, showerheads.

**Base spray (general purpose):**
1. Dissolve 1 tbsp washing soda in 1 quart of warm water. Stir until dissolved.
2. Pour into spray bottle.
3. Label clearly: "BASE - Washing Soda Solution."

**Heavy-duty base (oven cleaner):**
1. Make a paste: 1/2 cup washing soda + enough water to form a thick paste.
2. Spread inside the oven. Let sit overnight (8-12 hours).
3. Wipe clean with damp cloth. Spray with vinegar solution to neutralize any residue and remove streaks.

**Disinfectant spray:**
1. Fill a spray bottle with 3% hydrogen peroxide (straight from the store bottle).
2. Spray surfaces, let sit 5-10 minutes, wipe. No rinsing needed -- it breaks down to water and oxygen.

**Surfactant solution (for lifting grime):**
1. Add 1 tbsp castile soap to 1 quart warm water.
2. Use for mopping floors, washing walls, general surface cleaning.
3. Note: Do NOT mix castile soap with vinegar. The acid will react with the soap and produce a white, oily curd.

## The Science Behind It
All cleaning is chemistry, and all cleaning chemistry reduces to a few principles:

**Acid-base reactions:** Mineral deposits (limescale, hard water spots) are calcium carbonate and magnesium carbonate -- alkaline compounds. Acids dissolve them through a simple neutralization reaction: CaCO3 + 2CH3COOH -> Ca(CH3COO)2 + H2O + CO2 (the fizzing you see when vinegar hits lime scale is CO2 being released). Conversely, grease and oils are organic compounds that saponify (turn into soap) in the presence of strong bases. Washing soda's high pH breaks ester bonds in fats.

**Oxidation:** Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is an oxidizer. It kills bacteria and viruses by generating free radicals that damage cell membranes and DNA. It also bleaches stains by breaking the conjugated double bonds that give colored compounds their color. Unlike chlorine bleach, H2O2 decomposes into water and oxygen -- no toxic residue.

**Surfactants:** Castile soap molecules have a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and a hydrophobic (water-fearing) tail. The tail embeds in grease/grime; the head dissolves in water. This physically lifts dirt off surfaces so water can rinse it away.

**Why you never mix acid and base:** Mixing vinegar and washing soda produces sodium acetate, water, and CO2. You waste both ingredients and produce a neutral salt solution with less cleaning power than either ingredient alone. Use the right tool for the job.

## Variations
- **Scented vinegar spray:** Fill a jar with citrus peels (orange, lemon, grapefruit) and cover with white vinegar. Let sit for 2 weeks, strain, and use as your acid spray. The citrus oils add pleasant scent and the d-limonene provides mild degreasing action.
- **Scrubbing paste:** Mix baking soda with just enough water to form a paste. Use as a mild abrasive for sinks, tubs, and stovetops. The fine particle size of baking soda is abrasive enough to scrub but soft enough not to scratch most surfaces (test on a hidden area first).
- **Mold killer:** Spray undiluted white vinegar or hydrogen peroxide directly on mold. Let sit 30-60 minutes. Scrub and wipe. Tea tree oil (10 drops per cup of water in a spray bottle) is also effective and prevents regrowth.
- **Drain maintainer:** Pour 1/2 cup baking soda down the drain, followed by 1/2 cup vinegar. The fizzing reaction physically agitates buildup. Follow with boiling water after 15 minutes. This maintains drains but will not clear serious clogs.

## Effectiveness Assessment
For 90% of household cleaning tasks, this system performs as well as commercial products. The chemistry is identical -- commercial cleaners are just pre-mixed acids and bases with added surfactants, fragrances, and dyes.

**What it does better:** Cheaper by an enormous margin. A gallon of white vinegar costs $2-3 and lasts months. A box of washing soda costs $4-5 and lasts months. No plastic waste from dozens of spray bottles. No fragrance chemicals or dyes. No misleading "antibacterial" claims (most commercial antibacterial sprays use triclosan, which is problematic for resistance).

**What it does worse:** No enzymes (for protein-based stains like blood, egg). No chelating agents like EDTA (for extremely hard water). No quaternary ammonium compounds (for hospital-grade disinfection, though hydrogen peroxide comes close). Requires knowing which solution to use rather than grabbing a single all-purpose spray. No pleasant commercial fragrance (unless you add essential oils or use citrus-infused vinegar).

## Cons & Limitations
- **Requires knowing acid vs. base.** You need to think about what you are cleaning rather than grabbing a single product. This is a minor learning curve but a real one.
- **No enzymes.** Commercial cleaners often include protease and lipase enzymes that break down protein and fat stains biologically. DIY cannot replicate this cheaply.
- **Vinegar smell.** It dissipates in 15-30 minutes, but some people find it unpleasant during use. Citric acid is an odorless alternative.
- **Not suitable for all surfaces.** Vinegar/acids will etch natural stone (marble, granite, travertine), damage hardwood floor finishes, and corrode some metals (iron, aluminum). Use base solutions on these surfaces instead.
- **Washing soda can irritate skin.** Wear gloves when using concentrated washing soda solutions, especially for oven cleaning.
- **No built-in disinfection in acid/base sprays.** Vinegar and washing soda clean but do not reliably disinfect (kill 99.9% of pathogens). Use hydrogen peroxide as a separate step when disinfection is needed.

## Tips & Troubleshooting
- **Hard water leaving spots on glass:** Switch from vinegar to citric acid solution. Citric acid is a stronger chelating agent and handles hard water better.
- **Soap scum not budging:** Apply undiluted vinegar, let sit 15-30 minutes, then scrub. For severe buildup, use citric acid paste.
- **Oven still greasy after overnight treatment:** The washing soda paste may have dried out. Next time, cover with plastic wrap after applying to keep it moist. Reapply if needed.
- **Castile soap leaving residue:** You may have hard water. Add 1 tsp washing soda to the castile soap solution to soften the water. Or switch to a washing soda solution instead.
- **Want antibacterial action:** Follow your cleaning spray (acid or base) with a hydrogen peroxide spray. Let it sit 5-10 minutes. This two-step process actually outperforms most commercial "antibacterial" sprays.
- **Stainless steel streaks:** Spray vinegar solution on a microfiber cloth and wipe with the grain of the steel, not against it. Buff dry immediately.

## Safety Notes
- **Never mix vinegar/acid with washing soda/base in a closed container.** The CO2 produced can build pressure and pop the lid off.
- **Never mix hydrogen peroxide with vinegar.** This produces peracetic acid, which is corrosive and can cause respiratory irritation.
- **Wear gloves** when handling washing soda solutions, especially concentrated paste for oven cleaning.
- **Ventilate** when using vinegar in enclosed spaces (bathrooms).
- **Label all spray bottles clearly.** You do not want to grab the wrong one for the wrong surface.
- **Keep all cleaning solutions away from children and pets**, even though the ingredients are generally safer than commercial alternatives.
- **Oxalic acid is toxic if ingested.** Store separately and label clearly. Wear gloves and avoid inhalation of dry powder.
- **Test on an inconspicuous area** before using any solution on a new surface type.
